# Acute Bronchitis

## Key Facts
- Acute bronchitis is inflammation of the bronchial tubes (bronchi), usually caused by viral infection.
- It is one of the most common respiratory conditions, affecting approximately 5% of adults annually.
- The hallmark symptom is a cough that may last 1–3 weeks, often with mucus production.
- Over 90% of cases are viral in origin; antibiotics are not recommended for uncomplicated acute bronchitis.
- Acute bronchitis is typically self-limiting and resolves without complications in otherwise healthy individuals.
- Smoking and exposure to air pollution increase the risk and severity of bronchitis episodes.

## Overview
Acute bronchitis is a short-term inflammation of the large airways (bronchi) of the lungs. It is distinct from chronic bronchitis, which is a component of COPD. Acute bronchitis occurs most frequently during the winter months and often follows an upper respiratory tract infection (common cold or influenza). The inflammation causes swelling of the bronchial mucosa and increased mucus production, leading to cough and airway discomfort.

The condition is extremely common and accounts for a significant number of outpatient visits worldwide. While uncomfortable, acute bronchitis in healthy adults rarely leads to serious complications. However, in elderly patients, young children, and those with chronic respiratory or cardiac conditions, it can contribute to more severe lower respiratory tract disease.

## Symptoms
- Persistent cough, initially dry, then often productive with clear, white, yellow, or greenish sputum
- Chest discomfort or soreness from coughing
- Low-grade fever and chills
- Sore throat and nasal congestion (from preceding upper respiratory infection)
- Fatigue and general malaise
- Mild shortness of breath or wheezing (in some patients)

The cough of acute bronchitis typically lasts 10–21 days but may persist for up to 6 weeks in some cases. Sputum color alone does not reliably indicate bacterial infection.

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Causative agents:**
- **Viruses (>90% of cases):** Influenza A and B, parainfluenza, respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), rhinovirus, adenovirus, coronavirus, human metapneumovirus
- **Bacteria (uncommon, <10%):** *Mycoplasma pneumoniae*, *Chlamydophila pneumoniae*, *Bordetella pertussis* (whooping cough)

**Risk factors:**
- Cigarette smoking or secondhand smoke exposure
- Exposure to air pollution, dust, or chemical fumes
- Weakened immune system
- Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)
- Lack of influenza or pertussis vaccination
- Close contact with infected individuals (family, school, workplace)

## Diagnosis
Acute bronchitis is primarily a clinical diagnosis:

- **History and physical examination:** Cough with or without sputum, often following an upper respiratory infection. Auscultation may reveal rhonchi or scattered wheezes; crackles are typically absent (their presence suggests pneumonia).
- **Chest X-ray:** Not routinely needed. Indicated when pneumonia is suspected (high fever, tachycardia, focal lung findings, elderly patient, or significant comorbidities).
- **Pulse oximetry:** To rule out hypoxemia in patients with significant dyspnea.
- **Influenza testing:** Rapid antigen or PCR testing during influenza season if results would change management.
- **Pertussis testing:** Consider in patients with cough lasting >2 weeks, especially with paroxysms, post-tussive vomiting, or inspiratory whoop.

## Treatment
Treatment is primarily supportive, as most cases are viral and self-limiting:

- **Rest and hydration:** Adequate fluid intake and rest to support recovery.
- **Cough management:** Honey (for adults and children >1 year) may provide modest cough relief. Over-the-counter cough suppressants (dextromethorphan) may be used for disruptive dry cough, but evidence of benefit is limited.
- **Antipyretics/analgesics:** Paracetamol (acetaminophen) or ibuprofen for fever and chest discomfort.
- **Bronchodilators:** Inhaled beta-agonists (salbutamol) may help patients with significant wheezing, but routine use is not recommended.
- **Antibiotics:** NOT recommended for uncomplicated acute bronchitis. Antibiotics do not shorten illness duration and contribute to antibiotic resistance. They should only be considered when pertussis or bacterial superinfection is suspected.
- **Influenza antivirals:** Oseltamivir if influenza is confirmed and within 48 hours of symptom onset.

## Prevention
- **Influenza vaccination:** Annual flu vaccination reduces the incidence of influenza-related bronchitis.
- **Pertussis vaccination:** Tdap vaccine for adolescents and adults; Td/Tdap booster every 10 years.
- **Hand hygiene:** Frequent handwashing with soap and water; use of alcohol-based hand sanitizer.
- **Respiratory etiquette:** Covering mouth and nose when coughing or sneezing; disposing of tissues properly.
- **Smoking cessation:** Quitting smoking significantly reduces the risk and severity of respiratory infections.
- **Avoiding irritants:** Minimize exposure to air pollution, dust, and chemical vapors.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if cough lasts more than 3 weeks, if you cough up blood, or if you develop high fever (>38.5°C / 101.3°F), significant shortness of breath, or chest pain. Seek medical advice if you have underlying chronic respiratory disease, heart disease, or are immunocompromised, as bronchitis may progress to pneumonia in these groups.
