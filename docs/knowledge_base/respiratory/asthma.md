# Asthma

## Key Facts
- Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disease of the airways characterized by recurrent episodes of wheezing, breathlessness, chest tightness, and coughing.
- An estimated 262 million people were affected by asthma in 2019, and it caused 455,000 deaths.
- Asthma is the most common chronic disease among children.
- Most asthma-related deaths occur in low- and lower-middle-income countries due to underdiagnosis and undertreatment.
- Asthma cannot be cured, but it can be effectively controlled with inhaled medications, allowing most patients to lead active lives.
- Avoiding asthma triggers and using preventive medication can significantly reduce the frequency and severity of attacks.

## Overview
Asthma is a major noncommunicable disease affecting both children and adults. It is characterized by chronic inflammation of the bronchial airways, leading to reversible airflow obstruction and bronchial hyperresponsiveness. During an asthma episode, the lining of the airways swells, muscles around the airways tighten (bronchospasm), and excess mucus is produced, all of which narrow the airways and reduce airflow.

The underlying causes of asthma are not fully understood, but it is believed to result from a combination of genetic predisposition and environmental exposures. Asthma is often associated with other allergic conditions such as allergic rhinitis (hay fever) and eczema (atopic dermatitis).

## Symptoms
Asthma symptoms vary in frequency and severity among individuals. Common symptoms include:

- Wheezing (a whistling sound when breathing, especially on exhalation)
- Shortness of breath
- Chest tightness or pain
- Coughing, particularly at night or early morning
- Difficulty sleeping due to coughing or breathlessness

Symptoms may be triggered or worsened by viral respiratory infections, exercise, allergen exposure, weather changes, air pollution, tobacco smoke, and strong emotions. An **asthma attack** (exacerbation) is a sudden worsening of symptoms that may require emergency treatment.

## Causes / Risk Factors
The exact cause of asthma is unknown, but it involves a combination of genetic and environmental factors:

- **Genetic factors:** Family history of asthma or atopic conditions (eczema, allergic rhinitis, food allergy).
- **Allergens:** House dust mites, animal dander, cockroach droppings, mold spores, pollen.
- **Irritants:** Tobacco smoke, air pollution, chemical fumes, strong odors.
- **Respiratory infections:** Viral infections in early childhood may contribute to asthma development.
- **Occupational exposures:** Chemicals, dust, flour, wood dust, latex (occupational asthma).
- **Other factors:** Obesity, physical exercise (exercise-induced bronchoconstriction), cold air, certain medications (aspirin, beta-blockers).

## Diagnosis
- **Spirometry:** Measures forced expiratory volume in 1 second (FEV1) and forced vital capacity (FVC). A reduced FEV1/FVC ratio with reversibility after bronchodilator administration (improvement of ≥12% and 200 mL in FEV1) confirms variable airflow limitation.
- **Peak expiratory flow (PEF) monitoring:** Variability in PEF measurements over time supports asthma diagnosis.
- **Bronchial provocation testing:** Methacholine or exercise challenge tests assess airway hyperresponsiveness.
- **Allergy testing:** Skin prick tests or specific IgE blood tests identify allergic triggers.
- **FeNO (fractional exhaled nitric oxide):** Elevated levels suggest eosinophilic airway inflammation.

## Treatment
Asthma treatment follows a stepwise approach based on symptom control and risk of exacerbations:

- **Reliever (rescue) medication:** Short-acting beta-agonists (SABA) such as salbutamol (albuterol) for quick relief of acute symptoms. WHO now recommends low-dose inhaled corticosteroid (ICS)-formoterol as the preferred reliever.
- **Controller (maintenance) medication:** Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) are the cornerstone of long-term asthma control (e.g., budesonide, fluticasone). Long-acting beta-agonists (LABA) are added for moderate-to-severe asthma.
- **Add-on therapies:** Leukotriene receptor antagonists (montelukast), long-acting muscarinic antagonists (tiotropium), or biologic therapies (omalizumab, mepolizumab) for severe uncontrolled asthma.
- **Written asthma action plan:** All patients should have a personalized action plan for daily management and emergency situations.

## Prevention
- **Avoid known triggers:** Identify and minimize exposure to personal asthma triggers (allergens, irritants, tobacco smoke).
- **Maintain a clean indoor environment:** Regular cleaning, using allergen-proof bedding covers, controlling humidity to prevent mold.
- **Influenza vaccination:** Annual flu vaccination to reduce risk of respiratory infection-triggered exacerbations.
- **Regular medical follow-up:** Periodic review of asthma control and adjustment of treatment by a healthcare provider.
- **Tobacco avoidance:** Do not smoke and avoid secondhand smoke exposure.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek emergency medical care if you experience severe breathlessness making it difficult to speak or walk, rapid worsening of symptoms despite using reliever inhaler, bluish discoloration of lips or fingernails (cyanosis), or confusion and drowsiness. These signs indicate a severe asthma attack that requires immediate treatment.
