# Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)

## Key Facts
- COPD is a chronic lung disease that causes obstructed airflow from the lungs, leading to breathing difficulties.
- An estimated 392 million people had COPD in 2019; it was the third leading cause of death worldwide, causing 3.23 million deaths.
- More than 90% of COPD deaths in those under 70 years of age occur in low- and middle-income countries.
- Tobacco smoking is the primary cause of COPD, accounting for over 70% of cases in high-income countries.
- Other risk factors include indoor and outdoor air pollution, occupational dust and chemicals, and frequent lower respiratory infections during childhood.
- COPD is not curable, but treatment can slow disease progression, relieve symptoms, and improve quality of life.

## Overview
Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is a heterogeneous lung condition characterized by chronic respiratory symptoms (dyspnea, cough, sputum production) due to abnormalities of the airways (bronchitis, bronchiolitis) and/or alveoli (emphysema) that cause persistent, often progressive, airflow obstruction. The airflow limitation is not fully reversible, distinguishing COPD from asthma.

COPD develops gradually over years. Most people are not diagnosed until they are in their 40s or 50s when significant lung damage has already occurred. The disease significantly impairs quality of life and is associated with frequent exacerbations (acute worsening episodes) and comorbidities including cardiovascular disease, lung cancer, osteoporosis, and depression.

## Symptoms
- Chronic cough (often called a "smoker's cough"), which may be productive with mucus
- Progressive breathlessness (dyspnea), initially on exertion, eventually at rest in advanced stages
- Frequent respiratory infections (bronchitis, pneumonia)
- Wheezing and chest tightness
- Fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance
- In advanced disease: weight loss, ankle swelling, cyanosis (bluish lips or fingernails)

Symptoms typically worsen over time and can be punctuated by **exacerbations** — episodes of acutely worsened symptoms that may require hospitalization.

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Tobacco smoking:** The most important risk factor. Cigarette, pipe, cigar, and water pipe smoking all contribute.
- **Indoor air pollution:** Exposure to biomass fuel smoke (wood, animal dung, crop residue) used for cooking and heating in poorly ventilated homes — a major factor in low- and middle-income countries.
- **Outdoor air pollution:** Particulate matter, nitrogen dioxide, and ozone from traffic and industrial sources.
- **Occupational exposures:** Dust, chemicals, fumes, and vapors in workplaces (mining, construction, agriculture, textiles).
- **Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency:** A rare genetic condition that predisposes to early-onset emphysema.
- **Early life events:** Low birth weight, childhood respiratory infections, and childhood asthma may increase risk.

## Diagnosis
- **Spirometry:** Essential for diagnosis. A post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC ratio < 0.70 confirms persistent airflow limitation. Spirometry also grades severity (GOLD stages I–IV based on FEV1 percentage predicted).
- **Clinical assessment:** History of dyspnea, chronic cough, sputum production, and exposure to risk factors.
- **Chest X-ray:** May show hyperinflation, flattened diaphragm; mainly used to exclude other conditions.
- **CT scan:** Identifies emphysema distribution and can detect lung cancer or bronchiectasis.
- **Arterial blood gas analysis:** Assesses oxygenation and carbon dioxide levels in advanced disease.
- **Alpha-1 antitrypsin level:** WHO recommends testing all COPD patients at least once.

## Treatment
COPD management aims to reduce symptoms, decrease exacerbation frequency, and improve exercise tolerance:

- **Smoking cessation:** The single most effective intervention to slow COPD progression. Includes counseling, nicotine replacement therapy, and pharmacotherapy (varenicline, bupropion).
- **Bronchodilators:** Inhaled long-acting bronchodilators are the mainstay — long-acting muscarinic antagonists (LAMA, e.g., tiotropium) and long-acting beta-agonists (LABA, e.g., salmeterol, formoterol).
- **Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS):** Added for patients with frequent exacerbations, especially those with eosinophilic inflammation.
- **Pulmonary rehabilitation:** Structured exercise training, education, and self-management support — improves dyspnea and quality of life.
- **Oxygen therapy:** Long-term supplemental oxygen for patients with chronic severe hypoxemia.
- **Exacerbation management:** Short courses of systemic corticosteroids and antibiotics (if bacterial infection suspected).

## Prevention
- **Tobacco cessation and avoidance:** Not starting smoking and quitting if currently smoking are the most important preventive measures.
- **Clean indoor air:** Use of clean fuels (LPG, electricity) and improved cookstoves to reduce household air pollution.
- **Occupational safety:** Use of protective equipment and ventilation systems to reduce exposure to workplace dust and chemicals.
- **Vaccination:** Influenza and pneumococcal vaccination to reduce exacerbation risk.
- **Air quality policies:** Government regulations to reduce outdoor air pollution.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if you have a persistent cough, produce sputum, or experience increasing breathlessness — especially if you have a history of smoking or exposure to air pollution. Seek emergency care if you experience severe breathlessness at rest, confusion, rapid heartbeat, or bluish discoloration of lips, as these may indicate a severe exacerbation or respiratory failure.
