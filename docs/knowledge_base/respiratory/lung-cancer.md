# Lung Cancer

## Key Facts
- Lung cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer worldwide, with 2.5 million new cases in 2022.
- Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death globally, responsible for approximately 1.8 million deaths in 2022.
- Tobacco smoking is the single greatest risk factor, causing approximately 85% of all lung cancer cases.
- Non-smokers can also develop lung cancer due to secondhand smoke, radon exposure, air pollution, and occupational carcinogens.
- Lung cancer is often diagnosed at advanced stages due to late onset of symptoms, resulting in poor prognosis.
- Early detection through low-dose computed tomography (LDCT) screening can reduce lung cancer mortality in high-risk populations.

## Overview
Lung cancer originates in the tissues of the lung, most commonly in the cells lining the airways (bronchi, bronchioles). It is broadly classified into two main types based on histology:

- **Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC):** Accounts for approximately 85% of cases. Subtypes include adenocarcinoma (most common), squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma.
- **Small cell lung cancer (SCLC):** Accounts for approximately 15% of cases. It is strongly associated with heavy smoking, grows rapidly, and tends to spread early.

Lung cancer is staged using the TNM system (Tumor, Node, Metastasis), ranging from stage I (localized) to stage IV (metastatic). Prognosis is strongly related to stage at diagnosis; the 5-year survival rate for localized disease exceeds 60%, but drops to less than 10% for distant metastatic disease.

## Symptoms
Lung cancer often produces no symptoms in early stages. When symptoms appear, they may include:

- Persistent cough that does not resolve or worsens over time
- Coughing up blood (hemoptysis), even small amounts
- Chest pain, often worsened by deep breathing, coughing, or laughing
- Shortness of breath and hoarseness
- Unexplained weight loss and loss of appetite
- Fatigue and weakness
- Recurrent respiratory infections (bronchitis, pneumonia)
- Bone pain, headache, or neurological symptoms (if cancer has metastasized)

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Tobacco smoking:** The dominant risk factor. Risk increases with duration and intensity of smoking. Both active smoking and secondhand (passive) smoke exposure significantly increase risk.
- **Radon exposure:** A naturally occurring radioactive gas that can accumulate in buildings; the second leading cause of lung cancer after smoking.
- **Occupational carcinogens:** Asbestos, arsenic, chromium, nickel, tar, and soot exposures in workplaces.
- **Air pollution:** Outdoor air pollution (particulate matter, diesel exhaust) and indoor air pollution from solid fuel combustion.
- **Genetic factors:** Family history of lung cancer may indicate genetic susceptibility.
- **Previous radiation therapy:** Chest radiation for other cancers increases future lung cancer risk.

## Diagnosis
- **Chest X-ray:** May reveal a lung mass or nodule, but limited sensitivity for small tumors.
- **Computed tomography (CT) scan:** More sensitive than X-ray; low-dose CT (LDCT) is used for screening in high-risk populations.
- **Biopsy:** Tissue diagnosis is essential. Methods include bronchoscopy with biopsy, CT-guided percutaneous needle biopsy, or surgical biopsy.
- **Histopathology and molecular testing:** Determines cancer type and identifies actionable mutations (EGFR, ALK, ROS1, PD-L1 expression) to guide targeted therapy.
- **PET-CT scan:** Assesses disease extent and detects metastases.
- **Staging workup:** Brain MRI, bone scan, and blood tests to determine cancer stage.

## Treatment
Treatment depends on the type of lung cancer, stage, molecular profile, and patient's overall health:

- **Surgery:** Lobectomy, segmentectomy, or pneumonectomy for early-stage NSCLC. Surgery offers the best chance of cure for localized disease.
- **Radiation therapy:** Used alone for inoperable early-stage disease, or combined with chemotherapy for locally advanced disease. Stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) for small tumors in patients unfit for surgery.
- **Chemotherapy:** Platinum-based doublet chemotherapy (cisplatin or carboplatin plus a second agent) for advanced NSCLC and both limited and extensive SCLC.
- **Targeted therapy:** For NSCLC with specific driver mutations — EGFR inhibitors (osimertinib), ALK inhibitors (alectinib), and others.
- **Immunotherapy:** Immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab, atezolizumab) for PD-L1-positive NSCLC and extensive SCLC.
- **Palliative care:** Symptom management and quality of life support for advanced disease.

## Prevention
- **Tobacco cessation:** Not smoking or quitting smoking is the most effective prevention strategy. Even long-term smokers benefit from quitting at any age.
- **Secondhand smoke avoidance:** Support smoke-free policies in homes, workplaces, and public spaces.
- **Radon testing and mitigation:** Test homes for radon levels and install mitigation systems if levels are elevated.
- **Occupational safety:** Protective equipment and regulatory compliance to minimize exposure to workplace carcinogens.
- **Air quality improvement:** Policies to reduce ambient air pollution.
- **Screening:** LDCT screening for high-risk individuals (heavy smokers aged 50–80) can detect lung cancer at earlier, more treatable stages.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if you experience a persistent cough lasting more than 3 weeks, coughing up blood, unexplained weight loss, chest pain, or recurrent respiratory infections — particularly if you are a current or former smoker. Seek prompt evaluation for new shortness of breath, hoarseness, or bone pain.
