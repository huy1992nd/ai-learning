# Pneumonia

## Key Facts
- Pneumonia is an acute infection of the lungs that causes the alveoli to fill with pus and fluid, impairing gas exchange.
- Pneumonia is the single largest infectious cause of death in children worldwide, killing an estimated 740,180 children under 5 in 2019.
- Pneumonia affects people of all ages but is most dangerous for young children, older adults, and immunocompromised individuals.
- The leading bacterial cause is *Streptococcus pneumoniae*; viral causes include influenza and respiratory syncytial virus (RSV).
- Pneumonia can be prevented by vaccination, adequate nutrition, and addressing environmental risk factors.
- Most cases of bacterial pneumonia can be treated with affordable oral antibiotics.

## Overview
Pneumonia is an infection that inflames the air sacs (alveoli) in one or both lungs. The alveoli may fill with fluid or pus, causing cough with phlegm, fever, chills, and difficulty breathing. Pneumonia can be caused by bacteria, viruses, or fungi, with bacterial pneumonia being the most common type requiring antibiotic treatment.

Pneumonia can be classified by setting of acquisition: community-acquired pneumonia (CAP), hospital-acquired pneumonia (HAP), or ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP). It can also be classified by anatomic distribution: lobar pneumonia (affecting one lobe), bronchopneumonia (patchy distribution), or interstitial pneumonia (affecting the tissue between alveoli).

## Symptoms
- Cough (often productive, with yellow, green, or blood-tinged sputum)
- Fever, sweating, and shaking chills
- Shortness of breath and rapid, shallow breathing
- Sharp or stabbing chest pain that worsens with deep breathing or coughing (pleuritic chest pain)
- Fatigue and general malaise
- Nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea (especially in children)
- Confusion or altered mental status (particularly in older adults)

**In infants:** Signs may include inability to feed, convulsions, unconsciousness, hypothermia, lower chest wall indrawing, and fast breathing (tachypnea: >60 breaths/min in infants <2 months, >50 in infants 2–11 months, >40 in children 1–5 years).

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Common causative agents:**
- **Bacteria:** *Streptococcus pneumoniae* (most common), *Haemophilus influenzae* type b (Hib), *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Mycoplasma pneumoniae*, *Legionella pneumophila*
- **Viruses:** Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), influenza, SARS-CoV-2, adenovirus, parainfluenza
- **Fungi:** *Pneumocystis jirovecii* (in HIV/AIDS patients), *Histoplasma*, *Coccidioides*

**Risk factors:** Age (very young and elderly), malnutrition, immunosuppression (HIV, chemotherapy), chronic lung disease (COPD, asthma), smoking, air pollution (indoor and outdoor), crowded living conditions, and recent respiratory viral infection.

## Diagnosis
- **Clinical examination:** Auscultation reveals crackles (rales), bronchial breath sounds, or dullness to percussion over the affected lung area.
- **Chest X-ray:** Confirms the presence and extent of lung infiltrates; helps distinguish from other respiratory conditions.
- **Pulse oximetry:** Measures oxygen saturation; low SpO2 (<90–92%) indicates need for supplemental oxygen.
- **Sputum culture and Gram stain:** Identifies the causative organism and guides antibiotic selection (mainly for hospitalized patients).
- **Blood tests:** Complete blood count (elevated white blood cell count), C-reactive protein, procalcitonin, blood cultures.
- **Urinary antigen tests:** For *S. pneumoniae* and *Legionella pneumophila* (rapid, non-invasive).

## Treatment
- **Bacterial pneumonia:** Oral antibiotics for mild community-acquired cases (e.g., amoxicillin is the first-line treatment for children and adults). Macrolides (azithromycin) or respiratory fluoroquinolones for atypical pathogens or penicillin-allergic patients. Severe cases require hospitalization and intravenous antibiotics.
- **Viral pneumonia:** Supportive care (rest, fluids, antipyretics). Antiviral therapy for influenza pneumonia (oseltamivir) or severe COVID-19 pneumonia (antivirals as per guidelines).
- **Supportive care:** Oxygen therapy for hypoxemia, fluid management, chest physiotherapy. Mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure.
- **Pneumocystis pneumonia (PCP):** Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole is the treatment of choice in HIV/AIDS patients.

## Prevention
- **Vaccination:** Pneumococcal vaccines (PCV13/PCV15/PCV20 and PPSV23), Hib vaccine, influenza vaccine, COVID-19 vaccine, and RSV vaccine (for infants and older adults) are effective at preventing pneumonia.
- **Adequate nutrition:** Exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life protects infants. Addressing malnutrition in children reduces susceptibility.
- **Environmental measures:** Reducing indoor air pollution (clean cookstoves), avoiding tobacco smoke, and improving ventilation.
- **Hand hygiene:** Regular handwashing with soap and water to prevent transmission of respiratory pathogens.
- **HIV management:** ART for HIV-positive individuals and cotrimoxazole prophylaxis to prevent PCP.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek medical care if you develop a persistent cough with fever, chest pain, or difficulty breathing. Emergency care is needed for severe breathlessness, high fever unresponsive to antipyretics, confusion, cyanosis (blue lips/fingernails), or if a child shows chest indrawing, inability to feed, or convulsions. Older adults may present with confusion as the primary symptom rather than typical respiratory signs.
