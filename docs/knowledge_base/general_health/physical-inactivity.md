# Physical Inactivity

## Key Facts
- Physical inactivity is the fourth leading risk factor for global mortality, accounting for approximately 3.2 million deaths per year.
- Globally, 1 in 4 adults (27.5%) and more than 80% of adolescents do not meet WHO recommended levels of physical activity.
- Physical inactivity increases the risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, cancers, depression, and premature death.
- Insufficient physical activity is estimated to cost healthcare systems USD 27 billion annually worldwide.
- WHO's Global Action Plan on Physical Activity aims to reduce physical inactivity by 15% by 2030.
- Regular physical activity is one of the most important things people can do to improve their health.

## Overview
Physical inactivity refers to an insufficient level of physical activity to meet current WHO recommendations. WHO defines physical activity as any bodily movement produced by skeletal muscles that requires energy expenditure, including activities during work, play, household chores, travel, and recreational pursuits.

WHO recommendations (2020 guidelines):
- **Adults (18–64 years):** At least 150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity or 75–150 minutes of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity per week, plus muscle-strengthening activities ≥2 days/week
- **Older adults (≥65 years):** Same as adults, plus multicomponent activities emphasizing balance and strength to prevent falls (≥3 days/week)
- **Children and adolescents (5–17 years):** At least 60 minutes/day of moderate-to-vigorous-intensity physical activity; vigorous-intensity ≥3 days/week; limit sedentary screen time

Physical inactivity is distinct from sedentary behavior (prolonged sitting or reclining with low energy expenditure), which carries independent health risks.

## Symptoms
Physical inactivity itself is a behavior rather than a disease, but its health consequences include:

- Reduced cardiovascular fitness and endurance
- Muscle weakness and loss of muscle mass (sarcopenia)
- Reduced flexibility and joint mobility
- Weight gain and increased body fat
- Poor metabolic health (insulin resistance, dyslipidemia)
- Decreased bone density
- Reduced cognitive function, poor concentration
- Mood disturbances: increased anxiety, depression, irritability
- Poor sleep quality
- Fatigue and low energy levels

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Urbanization:** Environments that discourage walking and cycling (lack of sidewalks, parks, safe cycling infrastructure)
- **Sedentary occupations:** Office-based work, prolonged sitting
- **Technology and screen time:** Television, computers, smartphones, gaming
- **Transportation:** Dependence on motorized transport
- **Socioeconomic factors:** Low income, limited access to recreational facilities, unsafe neighborhoods
- **Cultural factors:** Gender norms discouraging physical activity in women/girls in some settings
- **Age:** Activity levels decline with age
- **Disability and chronic illness:** Physical limitations, pain, fatigue
- **Knowledge and motivation:** Lack of awareness of benefits, low self-efficacy

## Diagnosis
- **Self-report questionnaires:** Global Physical Activity Questionnaire (GPAQ), International Physical Activity Questionnaire (IPAQ)
- **Objective measurement:** Accelerometers, pedometers, wearable fitness trackers — more accurate than self-report
- **Assessment against WHO guidelines:** Compare individual activity levels to age-specific recommendations
- **Cardiorespiratory fitness testing:** VO2 max, 6-minute walk test, submaximal exercise testing — objective measure of fitness level
- **Clinical assessment:** Healthcare providers should routinely ask about physical activity as a "vital sign"

## Treatment
Physical inactivity is addressed through behavior change rather than medical treatment:

- **Individual-level interventions:**
  - Brief physical activity counseling by healthcare providers (exercise prescription)
  - Behavioral strategies: Goal setting, self-monitoring (pedometers/apps), social support, gradual progression
  - Start with achievable goals: Even small increases in activity provide health benefits ("every move counts")
  - Structured exercise programs: Aerobic, resistance, flexibility, and balance training

- **Community-level interventions:**
  - Group exercise programs, walking groups, community sports
  - Workplace wellness programs (standing desks, active breaks, on-site fitness)
  - School-based physical education and active play

- **Policy-level interventions (WHO ACTIVE framework):**
  - **A**ctive societies (public education campaigns)
  - **C**reate active environments (urban design, parks, cycling infrastructure)
  - **T**ackle sedentary behavior (workplace and school policies)
  - **I**nclude everyone (equitable access regardless of age, gender, disability, income)
  - **V**erify and validate (surveillance, monitoring, evaluation)
  - **E**veryone working together (multisectoral partnerships)

## Prevention
- Integrate physical activity into daily routines (walking, cycling for transport; taking stairs)
- Promote active play in early childhood
- Ensure quality physical education in schools
- Create walkable, bikeable communities with accessible green spaces
- Implement workplace policies that reduce prolonged sitting
- Use digital tools and apps for motivation and tracking
- Address barriers to activity for marginalized groups (women, elderly, people with disabilities)
- Government investment in active transport infrastructure and recreational facilities
- Healthcare systems: "Exercise is medicine" — integrate physical activity promotion into primary care

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider before starting a new exercise program if you have a chronic health condition (heart disease, diabetes, arthritis), have been completely sedentary for an extended period, experience chest pain, dizziness, or shortness of breath during physical activity, or are over 50 with risk factors for cardiovascular disease. Seek guidance from a physiotherapist or exercise professional for safe, individualized exercise programming if you have physical limitations.
