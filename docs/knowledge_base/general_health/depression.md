# Depression (Depressive Disorder)

## Key Facts
- Depression is a leading cause of disability worldwide, affecting an estimated 280 million people globally.
- Depression is more than ordinary sadness — it is a common and serious medical condition that negatively affects how you feel, think, and act.
- Depression can occur at any age but often begins in adulthood; median age of onset is 25 years.
- Women are approximately twice as likely as men to be affected by depression.
- Depression is a major risk factor for suicide, which accounts for over 700,000 deaths per year globally.
- Effective treatments are available, but more than 75% of people in low- and middle-income countries receive no treatment.

## Overview
Depressive disorder (clinical depression or major depressive disorder — MDD) is characterized by persistent low mood, loss of interest or pleasure in activities, and a range of cognitive, behavioral, and physical symptoms lasting at least 2 weeks. Depression significantly impairs a person's ability to function at work, school, and in personal relationships.

Depression involves complex interactions between biological factors (neurotransmitter imbalances — serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine; neuroplasticity changes; HPA axis dysregulation), genetic predisposition, psychological factors (cognitive distortions, rumination), and social/environmental stressors.

## Symptoms
According to DSM-5 criteria, MDD requires ≥5 of the following symptoms for ≥2 weeks (must include at least one of the first two):

- **Depressed mood** most of the day, nearly every day
- **Loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia)** in most or all activities
- Significant weight loss or gain / appetite changes
- Insomnia or hypersomnia
- Psychomotor agitation or retardation (observable by others)
- Fatigue or loss of energy
- Feelings of worthlessness or excessive/inappropriate guilt
- Diminished ability to think, concentrate, or make decisions
- Recurrent thoughts of death, suicidal ideation, or suicide attempts

**Other features:** Anxiety, irritability, somatic complaints (headaches, chronic pain, digestive issues), social withdrawal, decreased libido

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Biological:** Genetic predisposition (heritability ~40%), neurotransmitter dysregulation, neuroendocrine changes, chronic inflammation
- **Psychological:** Negative cognitive patterns, low self-esteem, history of adverse childhood experiences (ACEs), trauma
- **Social/environmental:** Poverty, unemployment, social isolation, bereavement, relationship difficulties, domestic violence
- **Medical:** Chronic illness (diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer, chronic pain), hypothyroidism, neurological conditions
- **Medications:** Corticosteroids, beta-blockers, interferon, isotretinoin
- **Substance use:** Alcohol and drug abuse (bidirectional relationship)
- **Life events:** Major stressful events, loss, transitions
- **Perinatal:** Perinatal depression affects ~10–15% of women during pregnancy or postpartum

## Diagnosis
- **Clinical assessment:** Structured interview based on DSM-5 or ICD-11 diagnostic criteria
- **Screening tools:** PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9) — widely used, validated. Score ≥10 suggests moderate depression warranting clinical evaluation.
- **Severity assessment:** Mild, moderate, or severe (based on symptom count, intensity, and functional impairment)
- **Differential diagnosis:** Rule out bipolar disorder (screen for manic episodes), dysthymia, adjustment disorder, grief, anxiety disorders, substance-induced mood disorder
- **Medical workup:** CBC, TSH, vitamin B12, folate, vitamin D, glucose — to exclude medical causes
- **Suicide risk assessment:** Essential at every evaluation

## Treatment
**Mild depression:**
- Guided self-help, psychoeducation, structured physical exercise, active monitoring

**Moderate to severe depression:**
- **Psychotherapy (evidence-based — WHO recommended):**
  - Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) — strongest evidence base
  - Behavioral activation
  - Interpersonal therapy (IPT)
  - Problem-solving therapy
- **Pharmacotherapy:**
  - SSRIs (first-line): Sertraline, escitalopram, fluoxetine — effective, generally well-tolerated
  - SNRIs: Venlafaxine, duloxetine
  - Other: Mirtazapine, bupropion
  - Treatment duration: Minimum 6–9 months after remission for first episode; longer or indefinite for recurrent depression
  - Response assessment: 4–6 weeks; switch or augment if inadequate response
- **Combined psychotherapy + pharmacotherapy:** Most effective for moderate–severe depression

**Treatment-resistant depression:**
- Augmentation strategies: Lithium, atypical antipsychotics (aripiprazole, quetiapine)
- Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT): Effective for severe, treatment-resistant, or psychotic depression
- Esketamine (intranasal): FDA-approved for treatment-resistant depression
- Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)

## Prevention
- Strengthen social support networks and community connections
- Develop healthy coping strategies and resilience (through psychoeducation and life skills training)
- Regular physical exercise (protective effect — 150 min/week moderate activity)
- Address adverse childhood experiences and trauma early
- Screen at-risk populations (chronic illness, perinatal women, bereaved individuals)
- Workplace mental health programs
- Reduce stigma around mental health to encourage help-seeking
- WHO mhGAP program: Integrating mental health care into primary health systems

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek help if you experience persistent sadness, loss of interest, or feelings of hopelessness for more than 2 weeks that interfere with daily life. Seek immediate help if you have suicidal thoughts, plans, or engage in self-harm — contact emergency services or a crisis helpline. If you are supporting someone with depression, encourage them to seek professional help and do not leave them alone if they express suicidal intent.
