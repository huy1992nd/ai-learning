# Obesity

## Key Facts
- Worldwide obesity has nearly tripled since 1975.
- In 2022, more than 1 billion people globally were living with obesity (890 million adults, 160 million children and adolescents).
- Overweight and obesity are defined as abnormal or excessive fat accumulation that presents a risk to health.
- Obesity is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, musculoskeletal disorders, and several cancers.
- Obesity is preventable and treatable.
- WHO classifies obesity as a chronic, relapsing disease.

## Overview
Obesity results from a chronic energy imbalance — energy intake exceeding energy expenditure — influenced by genetic, environmental, behavioral, and socioeconomic factors. Body mass index (BMI) is the most widely used classification:

- Overweight: BMI ≥25 kg/m²
- Obesity class I: BMI 30–34.9 kg/m²
- Obesity class II: BMI 35–39.9 kg/m²
- Obesity class III (severe): BMI ≥40 kg/m²

Waist circumference is an additional measure of central adiposity and metabolic risk (elevated risk: >94 cm in men, >80 cm in women — WHO thresholds for European populations; ethnicity-specific thresholds apply).

## Symptoms
- Excess body fat accumulation
- Breathlessness on exertion
- Excessive sweating
- Joint pain (especially weight-bearing joints)
- Fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance
- Snoring and obstructive sleep apnea
- Psychological impact: low self-esteem, depression, social stigma
- Skin changes: acanthosis nigricans, striae, intertrigo

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Energy imbalance:** Increased consumption of energy-dense, nutrient-poor foods (high in fat and sugar); reduced physical activity
- **Environmental:** Urbanization, sedentary occupations, increased availability of processed foods, food marketing, built environment discouraging physical activity
- **Behavioral:** Poor dietary habits, physical inactivity, insufficient sleep, excessive screen time
- **Genetics:** Heritability of BMI estimated at 40–70%; polygenic in most cases; rare monogenic obesity (leptin deficiency, MC4R mutations)
- **Endocrine:** Hypothyroidism, Cushing syndrome, PCOS (secondary causes — uncommon)
- **Medications:** Glucocorticoids, antipsychotics, some antidepressants, insulin, sulfonylureas, anticonvulsants
- **Socioeconomic:** Low income, food insecurity (paradoxically linked to obesity in some settings), limited access to healthy food

## Diagnosis
- **BMI calculation:** Weight (kg) / Height (m²) — primary screening tool
- **Waist circumference:** Measures central adiposity
- **Body composition assessment:** DEXA scan, bioelectrical impedance — for research or clinical decision-making
- **Metabolic evaluation:** Fasting glucose/HbA1c, lipid profile, liver function, thyroid function, blood pressure
- **Screening for complications:** Sleep study (OSA), echocardiography, joint assessment
- **Exclude secondary causes:** TSH, cortisol (if clinical suspicion)

## Treatment
**Comprehensive, patient-centered approach:**

**Lifestyle modification (foundation):**
- Dietary counseling: Caloric deficit of 500–750 kcal/day; Mediterranean diet, low-carbohydrate, or other evidence-based dietary patterns
- Physical activity: ≥150–300 min/week moderate-intensity aerobic activity + resistance training
- Behavioral therapy: Goal setting, self-monitoring, stimulus control, cognitive restructuring

**Pharmacotherapy (adjunct to lifestyle, for BMI ≥30 or ≥27 with comorbidities):**
- GLP-1 receptor agonists: Semaglutide (up to 15% weight loss), liraglutide
- Tirzepatide (GIP/GLP-1 dual agonist): Up to 20% weight loss
- Orlistat: Lipase inhibitor
- Naltrexone/bupropion, phentermine/topiramate

**Bariatric (metabolic) surgery (BMI ≥40 or ≥35 with comorbidities):**
- Roux-en-Y gastric bypass, sleeve gastrectomy — most effective long-term treatment (25–35% sustained weight loss)
- Also improves or resolves type 2 diabetes, hypertension, OSA, and dyslipidemia

## Prevention
- Promote breastfeeding in infancy
- Encourage healthy dietary patterns from early childhood (fruits, vegetables, whole grains, limit sugar-sweetened beverages and ultra-processed foods)
- Regular physical activity (children: ≥60 min/day; adults: ≥150 min/week)
- Limit sedentary screen time
- Policy interventions: Sugar taxes, food labeling, marketing restrictions, urban planning for walkability, school nutrition programs
- Address social determinants: Improve access to affordable healthy food and safe physical activity spaces

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if your BMI is ≥30, or ≥25 with associated health conditions (diabetes, hypertension, sleep apnea, joint pain). Seek evaluation for rapid unexplained weight gain, symptoms of secondary causes (fatigue, cold intolerance, moon face), or if obesity is significantly affecting daily function, mobility, or mental health. Children and adolescents with excessive weight gain should be evaluated by a pediatrician.
