# Malnutrition

## Key Facts
- Malnutrition refers to deficiencies, excesses, or imbalances in a person's intake of energy and/or nutrients.
- Malnutrition encompasses undernutrition (wasting, stunting, underweight), micronutrient deficiencies, and overweight/obesity.
- In 2022, an estimated 148.1 million children under 5 were stunted and 45 million were wasted.
- Micronutrient deficiencies affect approximately 2 billion people worldwide.
- Malnutrition in all its forms is associated with increased morbidity, mortality, and healthcare costs.
- The WHO double burden of malnutrition recognizes that undernutrition and overweight/obesity often coexist within the same countries, communities, and households.

## Overview
Malnutrition is a broad term covering several distinct conditions:

- **Undernutrition:**
  - **Wasting** (low weight-for-height): Indicates recent/acute weight loss or failure to gain weight; often caused by acute food shortage or illness
  - **Stunting** (low height-for-age): Reflects chronic undernutrition; associated with long-term poverty, maternal malnutrition, recurrent infections, inadequate early nutrition
  - **Underweight** (low weight-for-age): Can reflect wasting, stunting, or both

- **Micronutrient deficiencies:** Insufficient intake of essential vitamins and minerals — iron, vitamin A, iodine, zinc, and folate are the most significant globally ("hidden hunger")

- **Overweight and obesity:** Excessive energy intake relative to expenditure (covered separately in the Obesity file)

Malnutrition and infectious disease interact in a vicious cycle: malnutrition weakens the immune system, increasing susceptibility to infections, while infections worsen nutritional status.

## Symptoms
**Undernutrition:**
- Weight loss, muscle wasting, visible emaciation (wasting)
- Failure to grow (stunting), short stature for age
- Fatigue, weakness, lethargy
- Edema (bilateral pitting edema — hallmark of kwashiorkor)
- Dry skin, hair changes (sparse, discolored — "flag sign")
- Impaired wound healing, recurrent infections
- Cognitive and developmental delays in children

**Micronutrient deficiencies:**
- **Iron deficiency (anemia):** Fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath, reduced cognitive performance
- **Vitamin A deficiency:** Night blindness, xerophthalmia, increased infection risk
- **Iodine deficiency:** Goiter, hypothyroidism, cretinism (intellectual disability in children)
- **Zinc deficiency:** Growth retardation, impaired immunity, diarrhea, skin lesions
- **Folate deficiency:** Megaloblastic anemia, neural tube defects in newborns

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Insufficient food intake:** Poverty, food insecurity, famine, conflict, displacement
- **Poor dietary quality:** Monotonous diet lacking diversity, reliance on starchy staples
- **Disease:** Diarrhea, malaria, HIV, tuberculosis, helminth infections — increase nutrient losses and requirements
- **Maternal factors:** Maternal undernutrition, adolescent pregnancy, short birth spacing
- **Infant and young child feeding:** Suboptimal breastfeeding practices, inadequate complementary feeding
- **Water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH):** Poor WASH contributes to diarrheal disease and environmental enteropathy
- **Social determinants:** Poverty, low education, gender inequality, limited access to healthcare
- **Climate change and environmental disasters:** Disruption of food systems

## Diagnosis
**Anthropometry (primary tool):**
- **Weight-for-height (WHZ):** <−2 SD = wasting; <−3 SD = severe wasting
- **Height-for-age (HAZ):** <−2 SD = stunting
- **Weight-for-age (WAZ):** <−2 SD = underweight
- **Mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC):** <115 mm (children 6–59 months) = severe acute malnutrition (SAM); <125 mm = moderate acute malnutrition (MAM)
- **BMI-for-age:** Used for older children and adolescents
- **Bilateral pitting edema:** Defines kwashiorkor (always classified as SAM regardless of other anthropometry)

**Laboratory (for micronutrient deficiencies):**
- Hemoglobin, ferritin, serum iron (iron deficiency)
- Serum retinol (vitamin A)
- Urinary iodine concentration (iodine)
- Serum zinc
- Serum folate, vitamin B12
- Albumin, prealbumin (protein status — limited utility)

## Treatment
**Severe acute malnutrition (SAM):**
- **WHO protocol:** Stabilization phase (treat hypoglycemia, hypothermia, dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, infections) → rehabilitation phase (high-energy therapeutic feeding: F-75, then F-100 formula, or ready-to-use therapeutic food — RUTF such as Plumpy'Nut)
- **Community-based management of acute malnutrition (CMAM):** Outpatient treatment with RUTF for SAM without medical complications; inpatient care for complicated SAM

**Moderate acute malnutrition (MAM):**
- Supplementary feeding programs with fortified blended foods or ready-to-use supplementary food (RUSF)
- Nutrition counseling and dietary diversification

**Micronutrient deficiencies:**
- Iron supplementation (+ folic acid for pregnant women)
- Vitamin A supplementation (biannual high-dose for children 6–59 months in endemic areas)
- Iodine: Universal salt iodization
- Zinc supplementation (during diarrhea episodes)
- Multiple micronutrient powders (MNPs) for home fortification

**Stunting:**
- No direct treatment to reverse established stunting
- Prevention through the 1000-day window (conception to age 2) is critical

## Prevention
- **Promote optimal breastfeeding:** Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months, continued breastfeeding with complementary foods to 2 years and beyond
- **Adequate complementary feeding:** Diverse, nutrient-rich foods from 6 months of age
- **Maternal nutrition:** Iron-folic acid supplementation during pregnancy, adequate dietary intake
- **Food fortification:** Salt iodization, iron fortification of staple foods, biofortification
- **Micronutrient supplementation programs** for high-risk populations
- **WASH interventions:** Clean water, sanitation, hygiene education
- **Nutrition-sensitive interventions:** Social safety nets, women's empowerment, agricultural diversification, education
- **WHO Essential Nutrition Actions:** Evidence-based package for the first 1000 days

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek immediate medical care for a child with visible severe wasting, bilateral swelling (edema), refusal to eat, high fever, or severe diarrhea/vomiting. Consult a healthcare provider for any child with poor weight gain, growth faltering, or signs of micronutrient deficiency (pallor, night blindness, goiter). Pregnant women with poor weight gain or symptoms of anemia should seek evaluation promptly.
