# Peptic Ulcer Disease

## Key Facts
- Peptic ulcer disease (PUD) refers to open sores that develop on the inner lining of the stomach (gastric ulcer) or the upper portion of the small intestine (duodenal ulcer).
- *Helicobacter pylori* infection is the most common cause, responsible for 60–80% of gastric ulcers and up to 95% of duodenal ulcers.
- Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are the second most common cause of peptic ulcers.
- Approximately 5–10% of the global population will develop a peptic ulcer at some point in their lifetime.
- Complications include gastrointestinal bleeding, perforation, and gastric outlet obstruction.
- Effective treatment is available: eradication of *H. pylori* with antibiotics and acid suppression with proton pump inhibitors (PPIs).

## Overview
Peptic ulcer disease encompasses gastric and duodenal ulcers — defects in the gastrointestinal mucosa that extend through the muscularis mucosae. These ulcers result from an imbalance between aggressive factors (gastric acid, pepsin, *H. pylori*, NSAIDs) and protective factors (mucus, bicarbonate, prostaglandins, mucosal blood flow).

*Helicobacter pylori* is a gram-negative, spiral-shaped bacterium that colonizes the gastric mucosa. Approximately 50% of the world's population is infected with *H. pylori*, although only 10–20% of infected individuals develop peptic ulcer disease. The prevalence of *H. pylori* is higher in low- and middle-income countries due to overcrowding, poor sanitation, and lack of clean water.

## Symptoms
- Burning or gnawing epigastric pain (upper abdominal pain), often described as "hunger pain"
- Duodenal ulcer pain typically occurs 2–3 hours after meals and at night, relieved by eating or antacids
- Gastric ulcer pain may worsen with eating
- Nausea, vomiting, bloating, and belching
- Loss of appetite and unintentional weight loss
- Feeling of fullness after small meals (early satiety)

**Alarm symptoms (indicating complications):**
- Hematemesis (vomiting blood — bright red or "coffee ground" appearance)
- Melena (black, tarry stools indicating gastrointestinal bleeding)
- Sudden, severe, diffuse abdominal pain (suggesting perforation)
- Signs of anemia: pallor, fatigue, dizziness

Some ulcers are "silent" and cause no symptoms until a complication occurs.

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Primary causes:**
- *Helicobacter pylori* infection (most common cause worldwide)
- NSAID use (aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac) — particularly chronic or high-dose use

**Contributing risk factors:**
- Smoking (impairs mucosal healing and increases acid secretion)
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Physiological stress (critical illness, major surgery, burns — "stress ulcers")
- Zollinger-Ellison syndrome (gastrin-secreting tumor causing excessive acid production — rare)
- Advanced age
- History of prior peptic ulcer disease
- Concurrent use of NSAIDs with corticosteroids, anticoagulants, or selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)

## Diagnosis
- **Upper gastrointestinal endoscopy (esophagogastroduodenoscopy — EGD):** Gold standard. Directly visualizes the ulcer, assesses severity, and allows biopsy for *H. pylori* testing and exclusion of malignancy (especially for gastric ulcers).
- **H. pylori testing:**
  - Non-invasive: Urea breath test (UBT) — most accurate non-invasive test; stool antigen test; serology (IgG antibodies — indicates exposure but not active infection)
  - Invasive (during endoscopy): Rapid urease test (CLO test), histology, culture
- **Blood tests:** Complete blood count (to detect anemia from chronic bleeding), serum gastrin (if Zollinger-Ellison syndrome suspected).
- **Barium swallow X-ray:** Less commonly used; can identify ulcer crater but has lower sensitivity than endoscopy.

## Treatment
**H. pylori eradication (if H. pylori positive):**
- Standard triple therapy (14 days): PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin (or metronidazole)
- Bismuth quadruple therapy (14 days): PPI + bismuth subsalicylate + metronidazole + tetracycline (preferred in areas with high clarithromycin resistance)
- Confirm eradication with urea breath test or stool antigen test at least 4 weeks after completing therapy

**Acid suppression:**
- Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs): omeprazole, lansoprazole, pantoprazole, esomeprazole — mainstay of treatment, taken for 4–8 weeks
- H2-receptor antagonists: ranitidine, famotidine — less potent alternative

**NSAID-related ulcers:**
- Discontinue NSAIDs if possible
- If NSAIDs must continue: use the lowest effective dose, co-prescribe a PPI for gastroprotection
- Consider switching to a COX-2 selective inhibitor (celecoxib) with lower GI risk

**Complication management:** Endoscopic hemostasis for bleeding ulcers, surgical repair for perforation, endoscopic dilation for strictures.

## Prevention
- Test and treat *H. pylori* infection in individuals with dyspepsia or known ulcer history
- Use NSAIDs judiciously — shortest duration, lowest dose; co-prescribe PPI for high-risk patients
- Quit smoking and limit alcohol consumption
- Avoid combining NSAIDs with corticosteroids or anticoagulants without gastroprotection
- Maintain good hygiene (handwashing, clean water, safe food preparation) to reduce *H. pylori* transmission
- Regular follow-up for patients with a history of peptic ulcer disease

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek immediate medical care if you experience vomiting blood (bright red or "coffee grounds"), passing black or tarry stools, sudden severe abdominal pain, or signs of shock (rapid heartbeat, cold/clammy skin, dizziness). Consult a healthcare provider for persistent upper abdominal pain, nausea, unexplained weight loss, or symptoms not responding to over-the-counter antacids.
