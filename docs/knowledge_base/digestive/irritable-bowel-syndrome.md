# Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

## Key Facts
- Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) is a common functional gastrointestinal disorder characterized by recurrent abdominal pain and altered bowel habits.
- IBS affects an estimated 5–10% of the global population, making it one of the most common gastrointestinal conditions.
- It is more common in women than men (approximately 2:1 ratio) and typically develops before age 50.
- IBS is classified into subtypes based on predominant bowel pattern: IBS-C (constipation), IBS-D (diarrhea), IBS-M (mixed), and IBS-U (unclassified).
- IBS is a disorder of gut-brain interaction; there is no structural or biochemical abnormality detectable with standard tests.
- While not life-threatening, IBS significantly affects quality of life and is associated with increased healthcare utilization.

## Overview
Irritable bowel syndrome is a chronic functional disorder of the gastrointestinal tract characterized by abdominal pain or discomfort associated with defecation or a change in bowel habits. The diagnosis is based on symptom criteria (Rome IV criteria) after exclusion of organic diseases. IBS is classified as a disorder of gut-brain interaction, reflecting abnormalities in the bidirectional communication between the gut and the central nervous system.

The pathophysiology of IBS is multifactorial and includes visceral hypersensitivity, altered gastrointestinal motility, intestinal permeability changes, low-grade mucosal inflammation, dysbiosis of the gut microbiome, and psychosocial factors. Post-infectious IBS may develop after an episode of acute gastroenteritis.

## Symptoms
**Core symptoms (Rome IV criteria):**
- Recurrent abdominal pain, on average at least 1 day per week in the last 3 months, associated with two or more of:
  - Related to defecation (improved or worsened)
  - Associated with a change in stool frequency
  - Associated with a change in stool form (appearance)

**By subtype:**
- **IBS-C (constipation-predominant):** Hard or lumpy stools ≥25% of bowel movements; loose stools <25%
- **IBS-D (diarrhea-predominant):** Loose or watery stools ≥25%; hard stools <25%
- **IBS-M (mixed):** Both hard and loose stools ≥25% each

**Other common symptoms:**
- Bloating and abdominal distension
- Mucus in stool
- Urgency or straining during bowel movements
- Sensation of incomplete evacuation
- Symptoms often worsened by stress, certain foods, or menstruation

## Causes / Risk Factors
The exact cause of IBS is not known. Contributing factors include:

- **Visceral hypersensitivity:** Increased sensitivity of the gut nerves to stimuli such as gas, distension, and normal digestive processes.
- **Altered gut motility:** Abnormal contractions of the intestinal muscles — too fast (diarrhea) or too slow (constipation).
- **Gut-brain axis dysfunction:** Dysregulation of communication between the central nervous system and the enteric nervous system.
- **Gut microbiome dysbiosis:** Alterations in the composition and function of intestinal bacteria.
- **Post-infectious IBS:** Following acute bacterial, viral, or parasitic gastroenteritis (reported in 10–15% of cases).
- **Psychosocial factors:** Anxiety, depression, psychological trauma, and chronic stress are strongly associated with IBS.
- **Food intolerances:** Sensitivity to fermentable carbohydrates (FODMAPs), lactose, gluten, or certain food additives.
- **Genetics:** Family clustering suggests a genetic component.

## Diagnosis
IBS is a clinical diagnosis based on symptom criteria after excluding organic pathology:

- **Rome IV criteria:** Recurrent abdominal pain ≥1 day/week for ≥3 months, with onset ≥6 months before diagnosis, associated with defecation, change in stool frequency, or change in stool form.
- **Limited investigations:** Directed by clinical presentation to exclude organic disease:
  - Complete blood count and C-reactive protein (CRP) — to exclude inflammation/anemia
  - Serological testing for celiac disease (anti-tTG IgA)
  - Stool calprotectin — to distinguish IBS from inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)
  - Thyroid function tests (if clinically indicated)
- **Colonoscopy:** Not routinely required; indicated for patients with alarm features (rectal bleeding, weight loss, iron deficiency anemia, family history of colorectal cancer, onset >50 years).
- **Breath tests:** Hydrogen breath test for lactose or fructose intolerance, or small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO).

## Treatment
Treatment is individualized based on symptom predominance and severity:

**Dietary modifications:**
- Low FODMAP diet: Restriction of fermentable oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides, and polyols for 4–6 weeks, followed by systematic reintroduction. Evidence supports symptom improvement in 50–80% of patients.
- Adequate fiber intake (soluble fiber — psyllium — preferred over insoluble fiber)
- Regular meal patterns; avoid skipping meals
- Limit caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods, and fatty foods

**Pharmacotherapy:**
- **IBS-D:** Loperamide for diarrhea, bile acid sequestrants (cholestyramine), rifaximin (non-absorbed antibiotic), eluxadoline
- **IBS-C:** Osmotic laxatives (polyethylene glycol), linaclotide, lubiprostone, plecanatide
- **Abdominal pain:** Antispasmodics (hyoscine butylbromide, peppermint oil capsules), low-dose tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline 10–25 mg at night), or SSRIs
- **Probiotics:** Certain strains (e.g., *Bifidobacterium infantis* 35624) may provide modest symptom relief

**Psychological therapies:**
- Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) — strongest evidence among psychological interventions
- Gut-directed hypnotherapy
- Mindfulness-based stress reduction

## Prevention
- There is no proven way to prevent IBS, but symptom management can prevent flares:
- Identify and avoid individual dietary triggers (food diary may help)
- Manage stress through regular exercise, relaxation techniques, and adequate sleep
- Maintain regular eating habits and adequate hydration
- Treat gastrointestinal infections promptly to reduce risk of post-infectious IBS
- Regular physical activity (≥30 minutes/day) improves gut motility and reduces symptoms

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if you experience persistent changes in bowel habits, recurrent abdominal pain affecting daily activities, or symptoms that do not respond to dietary changes. Seek prompt evaluation for alarm symptoms: unintentional weight loss, rectal bleeding, iron deficiency anemia, onset of symptoms after age 50, family history of colorectal cancer or inflammatory bowel disease, or severe/worsening symptoms.
