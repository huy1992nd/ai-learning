# Colorectal Cancer

## Key Facts
- Colorectal cancer (CRC) is cancer of the colon or rectum; it is the third most commonly diagnosed cancer worldwide.
- In 2022, there were approximately 1.9 million new cases and 900,000 deaths from CRC globally (GLOBOCAN 2022).
- Incidence is highest in high-income countries but is rising rapidly in low- and middle-income countries undergoing economic transition.
- Most colorectal cancers develop slowly from precancerous polyps (adenomatous polyps) over 10–15 years.
- Screening and early detection through colonoscopy and fecal occult blood testing can significantly reduce CRC incidence and mortality.
- Treatment outcomes are strongly related to stage at diagnosis; 5-year survival exceeds 90% for localized disease but drops below 15% for metastatic disease.

## Overview
Colorectal cancer develops in the lining of the large intestine (colon) or rectum. Most CRCs arise from adenomatous polyps — benign growths that undergo a series of genetic mutations over years, progressing through the adenoma-carcinoma sequence. This slow progression provides a window of opportunity for screening and early intervention.

CRC is staged using the TNM system: Stage I (tumor limited to submucosa/muscularis), Stage II (penetrates through muscularis), Stage III (lymph node involvement), and Stage IV (distant metastases, commonly to liver and lungs). Hereditary syndromes such as Lynch syndrome (hereditary non-polyposis colorectal cancer, HNPCC) and familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) account for approximately 5–10% of all CRC cases.

## Symptoms
Early-stage CRC is often asymptomatic, which underscores the importance of screening. When symptoms develop, they may include:

- Change in bowel habits (diarrhea, constipation, or narrowing of stool) lasting more than a few days
- Rectal bleeding or blood in stool (bright red or dark)
- Persistent abdominal discomfort (cramps, gas, pain)
- Sensation of incomplete bowel evacuation
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fatigue and weakness (from iron deficiency anemia due to chronic occult bleeding)
- Nausea and vomiting (if bowel obstruction occurs)

**Right-sided colon cancer** often presents with iron deficiency anemia and occult bleeding. **Left-sided and rectal cancer** more commonly presents with visible rectal bleeding, change in stool caliber, and obstructive symptoms.

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Non-modifiable risk factors:**
- Age (≥50 years; risk increases substantially with age)
- Personal history of colorectal polyps or CRC
- Family history of CRC (first-degree relative increases risk 2–3 fold)
- Hereditary syndromes: Lynch syndrome, FAP, MYH-associated polyposis
- Inflammatory bowel disease (ulcerative colitis, Crohn's disease) — long duration increases risk
- Type 2 diabetes

**Modifiable risk factors:**
- Processed and red meat consumption (classified as Group 1 and Group 2A carcinogens by IARC)
- Low fiber, fruit, and vegetable intake
- Physical inactivity
- Overweight and obesity
- Tobacco smoking
- Heavy alcohol consumption (>2 drinks/day)
- Low calcium and vitamin D intake

## Diagnosis
- **Colonoscopy:** Gold standard for diagnosis. Allows direct visualization of the entire colon, biopsy of suspicious lesions, and removal of polyps (polypectomy). Recommended as first-line after positive screening tests.
- **Fecal occult blood test (FOBT) / Fecal immunochemical test (FIT):** Non-invasive screening tests that detect blood in stool. Annual or biennial FIT reduces CRC mortality by 15–33%. Positive results require follow-up colonoscopy.
- **Flexible sigmoidoscopy:** Examines the distal colon and rectum; used in some screening programs.
- **CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy):** Non-invasive imaging alternative for patients unable to undergo colonoscopy.
- **Histopathology:** Biopsy confirms adenocarcinoma type and grade. Molecular testing (MSI, KRAS, NRAS, BRAF, HER2) guides treatment decisions.
- **Staging:** CT of chest, abdomen, and pelvis; MRI pelvis (for rectal cancer); CEA (carcinoembryonic antigen) tumor marker.

## Treatment
Treatment depends on the stage and location of the cancer:

- **Stage 0 / Polyps:** Endoscopic polypectomy or local excision. Surveillance colonoscopy at defined intervals.
- **Stage I–III (localized and regional):** Surgical resection (colectomy with en-bloc lymph node dissection). Laparoscopic surgery preferred when feasible.
  - **Adjuvant chemotherapy:** Recommended for Stage III (FOLFOX: 5-fluorouracil + leucovorin + oxaliplatin, or capecitabine + oxaliplatin). Consider for high-risk Stage II.
  - **Rectal cancer:** Neoadjuvant chemoradiation (before surgery) for locally advanced disease, followed by total mesorectal excision (TME).
- **Stage IV (metastatic):** Combination chemotherapy (FOLFOX, FOLFIRI, FOLFOXIRI) with or without targeted therapy (bevacizumab — anti-VEGF, cetuximab/panitumumab — anti-EGFR for RAS wild-type tumors). Immunotherapy (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) for MSI-high / dMMR tumors. Resection of liver or lung metastases if feasible.
- **Palliative care:** Symptom management for advanced, unresectable disease.

## Prevention
- **Screening:** Regular CRC screening starting at age 45–50 for average-risk individuals. Options include annual FIT, colonoscopy every 10 years, or flexible sigmoidoscopy every 5 years. Earlier and more frequent screening for high-risk groups.
- **Polyp removal:** Colonoscopic polypectomy prevents progression of adenomas to cancer.
- **Diet:** High-fiber diet rich in whole grains, fruits, and vegetables; limit red and processed meat.
- **Physical activity:** Regular exercise (≥150 minutes moderate-intensity per week) reduces CRC risk by 20–30%.
- **Healthy weight:** Maintain a BMI within the normal range (18.5–24.9).
- **Limit alcohol and quit smoking**
- **Aspirin:** Low-dose aspirin may reduce CRC risk in selected individuals (consult a healthcare provider regarding benefits and risks).

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider for persistent changes in bowel habits, rectal bleeding or blood in stool, unexplained abdominal pain, unintentional weight loss, or fatigue with iron deficiency anemia. Seek immediate care for signs of bowel obstruction (severe abdominal pain, distension, vomiting, inability to pass gas or stool). Begin screening discussions with your doctor at age 45, or earlier if you have a family history of CRC or hereditary syndromes.
