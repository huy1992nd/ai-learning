# Cirrhosis of the Liver

## Key Facts
- Cirrhosis is the end stage of chronic liver disease, characterized by irreversible fibrosis and replacement of normal liver tissue with scar tissue.
- Cirrhosis affects an estimated 1–2% of the global population and is responsible for over 1.3 million deaths annually worldwide.
- The leading causes are chronic hepatitis B and C infections, alcohol-related liver disease, and metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD, formerly NAFLD).
- Cirrhosis is the 11th most common cause of death globally.
- Early-stage (compensated) cirrhosis may be asymptomatic; decompensated cirrhosis carries high morbidity and mortality.
- Liver transplantation is the definitive treatment for end-stage cirrhosis.

## Overview
Cirrhosis represents the final common pathway of chronic liver injury from various causes. The liver undergoes progressive fibrosis — deposition of extracellular matrix (collagen) — that disrupts normal hepatic architecture, forming regenerative nodules and ultimately impairing liver function.

Cirrhosis is classified as **compensated** (liver function is maintained; patient may be asymptomatic) or **decompensated** (development of clinical complications: ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, or jaundice). The transition from compensated to decompensated cirrhosis occurs at a rate of approximately 5–7% per year. Once decompensation occurs, median survival drops significantly without liver transplantation.

## Symptoms
**Compensated cirrhosis (early stage):**
- Often asymptomatic for years
- Fatigue and weakness
- Loss of appetite, nausea
- Unintentional weight loss
- Spider angiomata (spider-like blood vessels visible on the skin)
- Palmar erythema (reddening of the palms)

**Decompensated cirrhosis (advanced stage):**
- Ascites (accumulation of fluid in the abdominal cavity)
- Jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes)
- Variceal hemorrhage (bleeding from enlarged veins in the esophagus or stomach — life-threatening)
- Hepatic encephalopathy (confusion, altered consciousness, asterixis — "liver flap")
- Peripheral edema (leg swelling)
- Easy bruising and prolonged bleeding (coagulopathy)
- Hepatorenal syndrome (kidney failure secondary to liver disease)
- Susceptibility to bacterial infections (spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Common causes:**
- **Chronic hepatitis B and C:** Leading causes globally, especially in Asia, Africa, and Eastern Europe
- **Alcohol-related liver disease:** Chronic heavy alcohol consumption (>3 drinks/day for men, >2 for women for 10+ years)
- **Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD):** Associated with obesity, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome — rapidly becoming the most common cause in Western countries
- **Autoimmune hepatitis:** Immune-mediated liver inflammation
- **Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC) and primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC):** Chronic cholestatic liver diseases
- **Hereditary conditions:** Hemochromatosis (iron overload), Wilson's disease (copper overload), alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
- **Drug-induced liver injury:** Chronic exposure to hepatotoxic medications

**Risk factors:** Excessive alcohol consumption, obesity, unprotected sex (for hepatitis B/C), injection drug use, metabolic syndrome.

## Diagnosis
- **Liver function tests (LFTs):** Elevated AST, ALT (often AST > ALT in alcoholic liver disease), elevated bilirubin, reduced albumin, prolonged prothrombin time/INR.
- **Liver stiffness measurement (FibroScan/transient elastography):** Non-invasive method to assess liver fibrosis; values >12.5 kPa suggest cirrhosis.
- **Abdominal ultrasound with Doppler:** Evaluates liver morphology (nodular surface, coarse echotexture), portal vein flow, spleen size, and presence of ascites.
- **CT or MRI:** For further assessment and hepatocellular carcinoma surveillance.
- **Liver biopsy:** Histological gold standard for diagnosis; provides information on etiology and severity but is invasive and rarely needed when non-invasive methods are conclusive.
- **Serology/etiology workup:** Hepatitis B/C markers, autoantibodies, iron studies, ceruloplasmin, alpha-1 antitrypsin level.
- **Prognostic scores:** Child-Pugh score and MELD (Model for End-Stage Liver Disease) score to assess severity and guide transplant listing.

## Treatment
**Management of underlying cause:**
- Hepatitis B: Antiviral therapy (tenofovir, entecavir) — can halt or reverse early fibrosis
- Hepatitis C: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) — cure rates >95%, may reverse early cirrhosis
- Alcohol cessation: Most critical intervention for alcohol-related cirrhosis
- Weight loss and metabolic management for MASLD-related cirrhosis

**Management of complications:**
- **Ascites:** Sodium restriction (<2 g/day), diuretics (spironolactone + furosemide), large-volume paracentesis, transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS) for refractory ascites
- **Variceal hemorrhage:** Non-selective beta-blockers (propranolol, carvedilol) for primary prophylaxis; endoscopic band ligation for secondary prophylaxis; emergency management with vasoactive drugs + urgent endoscopy
- **Hepatic encephalopathy:** Lactulose (osmotic laxative), rifaximin (antibiotic to reduce ammonia-producing bacteria)
- **Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis:** Empiric antibiotics (cefotaxime); antibiotic prophylaxis for high-risk patients

**Liver transplantation:** Definitive treatment for decompensated cirrhosis; indicated when MELD score or clinical status warrants it.

**HCC surveillance:** Ultrasound ± alpha-fetoprotein every 6 months for all cirrhosis patients to detect hepatocellular carcinoma early.

## Prevention
- Vaccinate against hepatitis B (universal infant vaccination + catch-up for at-risk adults)
- Screen and treat hepatitis C (test-and-treat strategy)
- Limit alcohol consumption to safe levels or abstain entirely
- Maintain a healthy weight through balanced diet and regular exercise
- Manage metabolic risk factors (diabetes, dyslipidemia)
- Avoid hepatotoxic substances and unnecessary medications
- Practice safe injection and sexual practices to prevent hepatitis B/C

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if you experience persistent fatigue, jaundice, abdominal swelling, unexplained weight loss, easy bruising, or dark urine. Seek emergency medical care for vomiting blood, black tarry stools, sudden confusion or disorientation, or rapidly increasing abdominal distension. If you have risk factors for liver disease (chronic hepatitis, heavy alcohol use, obesity), discuss liver health screening with your doctor.
