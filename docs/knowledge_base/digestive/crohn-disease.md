# Crohn's Disease

## Key Facts
- Crohn's disease is a chronic inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) that can affect any part of the gastrointestinal tract from mouth to anus, but most commonly involves the terminal ileum and colon.
- The global incidence of Crohn's disease has been rising, particularly in newly industrialized countries, with an estimated 3–20 cases per 100,000 people annually.
- Peak age of onset is 15–35 years, with a second smaller peak at 50–70 years.
- The exact cause is unknown, but it involves a dysregulated immune response to intestinal bacteria in genetically susceptible individuals.
- Crohn's disease is characterized by transmural inflammation (affecting all layers of the bowel wall) with a relapsing-remitting course.
- There is no cure; treatment focuses on inducing and maintaining remission, preventing complications, and improving quality of life.

## Overview
Crohn's disease is one of the two main forms of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), the other being ulcerative colitis. Unlike ulcerative colitis, which is limited to the colon and affects only the mucosal layer, Crohn's disease can involve any segment of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract and is characterized by transmural inflammation (full-thickness involvement of the bowel wall). This can lead to complications including strictures, fistulas, and abscesses.

The inflammation in Crohn's disease is typically discontinuous, with affected segments ("skip lesions") separated by normal bowel. The disease follows a chronic relapsing-remitting course, with periods of active inflammation (flares) alternating with periods of remission. Crohn's disease significantly impacts quality of life and may require multiple surgeries over a patient's lifetime.

## Symptoms
Symptoms depend on the location and severity of inflammation:

- Chronic or recurrent diarrhea (often non-bloody, unlike ulcerative colitis)
- Abdominal pain and cramping (often in the right lower quadrant for ileocecal disease)
- Fatigue and malaise
- Unintentional weight loss and malnutrition
- Fever (during active inflammation)
- Blood in stool (if colon is involved)
- Mouth ulcers (aphthous stomatitis)
- Perianal disease: fissures, fistulas, abscesses (occur in up to 30% of patients)

**Extraintestinal manifestations (affect up to 25% of patients):**
- Joint pain (peripheral arthritis, sacroiliitis, ankylosing spondylitis)
- Skin conditions (erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum)
- Eye inflammation (uveitis, episcleritis)
- Liver conditions (primary sclerosing cholangitis)
- Osteoporosis

## Causes / Risk Factors
The etiology is multifactorial:

- **Genetics:** Over 200 genetic loci have been associated with IBD. NOD2/CARD15 gene mutations are the strongest genetic risk factor. Family history of IBD increases risk (first-degree relatives have a 5–20 fold increased risk).
- **Immune dysregulation:** An inappropriate, chronic immune response to commensal gut bacteria in genetically predisposed individuals.
- **Environmental factors:** Cigarette smoking (doubles the risk and worsens disease course — unlike ulcerative colitis where smoking is protective), diet (Western diet high in processed foods), NSAID use, prior appendectomy, antibiotic exposure.
- **Gut microbiome:** Reduced microbial diversity and altered composition (dysbiosis) have been consistently observed.
- **Geography:** Higher incidence in industrialized nations, northern latitudes, and urban areas.

## Diagnosis
- **Ileocolonoscopy with biopsies:** First-line investigation. Reveals discontinuous inflammation, cobblestone mucosa, deep ulcerations, and skip lesions. Biopsies show non-caseating granulomas (pathognomonic but present in only 15–36% of biopsies).
- **Upper GI endoscopy:** To evaluate upper GI tract involvement.
- **MRI enterography (MRE):** Preferred imaging for small bowel evaluation; detects inflammation, strictures, fistulas, and abscesses without radiation.
- **CT enterography:** Alternative to MRI; useful for acute complications.
- **Capsule endoscopy:** For suspected small bowel Crohn's when endoscopy and imaging are inconclusive (after ruling out strictures).
- **Laboratory tests:** C-reactive protein (CRP), erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), complete blood count, serum albumin, fecal calprotectin (correlates with intestinal inflammation).

## Treatment
**Induction of remission (active disease):**
- Corticosteroids: Prednisolone or budesonide for moderate flares (not for long-term use)
- Exclusive enteral nutrition (EEN): First-line for inducing remission in pediatric Crohn's disease
- Biologic therapies: Anti-TNF agents (infliximab, adalimumab) for moderate-to-severe disease; anti-integrin (vedolizumab); anti-IL-12/23 (ustekinumab)

**Maintenance of remission:**
- Immunomodulators: Azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine, or methotrexate
- Biologic therapies: Continued anti-TNF, vedolizumab, or ustekinumab
- Combination therapy: Biologic + immunomodulator (superior efficacy for some patients)
- 5-ASA (mesalazine): Limited role in Crohn's disease (mainly for mild colonic disease)

**Surgical management:**
- Surgery is required in up to 50% of Crohn's patients within 10 years of diagnosis
- Indications: strictures, fistulas, abscesses, refractory disease, or malignancy
- Resection of affected bowel segments (conservative resection to preserve bowel length)
- Strictureplasty for short strictures
- Perianal surgery: seton drainage, fistulotomy, advancement flaps

**Supportive care:** Nutritional support, iron supplementation, vitamin D and calcium, smoking cessation counseling, psychological support.

## Prevention
- There is no known way to prevent Crohn's disease.
- **For patients with established disease:**
  - Quit smoking (most important modifiable factor to reduce flares and surgical recurrence)
  - Adhere to maintenance therapy as prescribed
  - Regular monitoring with clinical assessment, biomarkers (CRP, calprotectin), and endoscopy
  - Vaccinations: ensure up-to-date immunizations before starting immunosuppressive therapy (especially live vaccines)
  - Colorectal cancer surveillance: colonoscopy every 1–2 years for patients with longstanding colonic Crohn's disease

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if you experience persistent diarrhea (>4 weeks), abdominal pain, unexplained weight loss, or blood in stool. Seek urgent care for severe abdominal pain, high fever, signs of intestinal obstruction (vomiting, abdominal distension, inability to pass gas or stool), perianal abscess (painful swelling near the anus), or symptoms of dehydration.
