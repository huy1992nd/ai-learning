# Osteoarthritis

## Key Facts
- Osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common form of arthritis and the most prevalent joint disease worldwide.
- An estimated 528 million people were living with OA globally in 2019, a 113% increase since 1990.
- OA most commonly affects the knees, hips, hands, and spine.
- It is characterized by progressive degradation of articular cartilage, subchondral bone changes, and synovial inflammation.
- OA is a leading cause of disability in older adults, significantly impacting mobility and quality of life.
- There is no cure; management focuses on pain relief, maintaining function, and slowing disease progression.

## Overview
Osteoarthritis is a degenerative joint disease involving the entire joint as an organ — including articular cartilage, subchondral bone, synovium, ligaments, capsule, and periarticular muscles. The disease is characterized by cartilage breakdown, osteophyte (bone spur) formation, subchondral bone sclerosis, and mild synovial inflammation.

OA was previously considered a simple "wear and tear" condition, but it is now understood to involve complex biological processes including mechanical stress, inflammatory pathways, and metabolic factors. OA prevalence increases with age and is more common in women, particularly after menopause.

## Symptoms
- Joint pain that worsens with activity and improves with rest (in early stages); may become persistent in advanced disease
- Morning stiffness lasting less than 30 minutes (unlike inflammatory arthritis where stiffness exceeds 30–60 minutes)
- Joint stiffness after periods of inactivity ("gelling phenomenon")
- Reduced range of motion
- Joint swelling (mild effusion)
- Crepitus (crackling or grinding sensation during joint movement)
- Joint instability or "giving way" (especially knee OA)
- Bony enlargement (Heberden's nodes at DIP joints, Bouchard's nodes at PIP joints — in hand OA)
- Functional impairment: difficulty walking, climbing stairs, gripping objects

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Age:** The strongest risk factor; prevalence increases sharply after age 50
- **Obesity:** Excess body weight increases mechanical load on weight-bearing joints (knee OA risk increases 36% for every 5 kg/m² increase in BMI); also contributes through systemic inflammation
- **Sex:** Women have higher prevalence, especially after age 50 (hormonal factors)
- **Joint injury:** Previous trauma, meniscal tears, ACL injury, repetitive occupational stress
- **Genetics:** Heritability of OA is estimated at 40–65%
- **Joint malalignment:** Varus (bow-legged) or valgus (knock-kneed) alignment increases focal joint loading
- **Muscle weakness:** Quadriceps weakness is both a risk factor and consequence of knee OA
- **Metabolic factors:** Diabetes, metabolic syndrome may contribute to OA pathogenesis

## Diagnosis
- **Clinical diagnosis:** Based on history and physical examination. ACR criteria for knee OA: age ≥50, knee pain, morning stiffness <30 minutes, crepitus, bony tenderness, bony enlargement, no palpable warmth.
- **Radiography (X-ray):** Kellgren-Lawrence grading (0–4). Findings include joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis, and subchondral cysts. Note: radiographic severity does not always correlate with symptom severity.
- **MRI:** More sensitive than X-ray; detects early cartilage changes, bone marrow lesions, meniscal pathology, and synovitis. Not routinely required for diagnosis.
- **Laboratory tests:** Usually normal. ESR, CRP, and RF are typically normal (helps distinguish from inflammatory arthritis). Synovial fluid analysis: non-inflammatory (WBC <2000/mm³).

## Treatment
**Non-pharmacological (first-line):**
- Exercise: Strengthening exercises (quadriceps for knee OA), aerobic exercise, flexibility training — the most effective non-drug intervention
- Weight management: Weight loss of ≥5% body weight significantly reduces pain and improves function in overweight patients
- Physical therapy: Tailored exercise programs, manual therapy, aquatic therapy
- Assistive devices: Canes, walkers, knee braces, shoe insoles
- Patient education and self-management programs

**Pharmacological:**
- Topical NSAIDs (e.g., diclofenac gel) — recommended first-line for hand and knee OA
- Oral NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, celecoxib) — effective but use lowest dose for shortest duration due to GI and cardiovascular risks
- Paracetamol (acetaminophen) — limited efficacy; no longer first-line per updated guidelines
- Intra-articular corticosteroid injections — short-term pain relief for flares
- Duloxetine — for patients with central sensitization or contraindications to NSAIDs
- Topical capsaicin — adjunctive option

**Surgical:**
- Arthroscopic surgery: Limited role; not recommended for routine OA
- Joint replacement (arthroplasty): Total knee or hip replacement for severe OA with significant functional impairment refractory to conservative management — highly effective with >90% good outcomes

## Prevention
- Maintain a healthy body weight throughout life
- Engage in regular physical activity (low-impact exercises: walking, swimming, cycling)
- Prevent joint injuries (warm-up before exercise, use protective equipment in sports)
- Strengthen muscles around joints (quadriceps strengthening for knee OA prevention)
- Correct biomechanical abnormalities (orthotics, footwear)
- Manage occupational risks (ergonomic modifications, avoiding repetitive heavy lifting)
- Early treatment of joint injuries (meniscal tears, ACL injuries)

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if you experience persistent joint pain, stiffness, or swelling that limits daily activities. Seek evaluation if joint symptoms are progressively worsening, if you have difficulty walking or performing routine tasks, or if over-the-counter pain medications provide inadequate relief. Consider referral to an orthopedic surgeon if conservative treatments fail after 3–6 months of consistent effort.
