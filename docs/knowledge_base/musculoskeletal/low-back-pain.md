# Low Back Pain

## Key Facts
- Low back pain (LBP) is the leading cause of disability worldwide, affecting an estimated 619 million people in 2020.
- LBP can occur at any age but prevalence peaks between ages 50 and 55.
- Approximately 90% of LBP cases are non-specific (no identifiable pathological cause).
- Most acute LBP episodes resolve within 6 weeks, but recurrence is common (up to 70% within one year).
- LBP is a major contributor to healthcare utilization, work absenteeism, and reduced quality of life globally.
- WHO published its first guidelines on the management of chronic LBP in 2023.

## Overview
Low back pain refers to pain, muscle tension, or stiffness localized between the lower rib margins and the gluteal folds. It is classified by duration as acute (<6 weeks), subacute (6–12 weeks), or chronic (>12 weeks).

Non-specific LBP — where no specific spinal pathology can be identified — accounts for approximately 90% of cases. The remaining ~10% may be attributed to specific causes including disc herniation, spinal stenosis, vertebral fractures, or (rarely) serious underlying conditions (infection, malignancy, cauda equina syndrome).

LBP involves a complex interplay of biological, psychological, and social factors (biopsychosocial model). Psychosocial factors such as fear-avoidance behavior, catastrophizing, depression, and job dissatisfaction are strong predictors of chronicity.

## Symptoms
- Pain in the lumbar region, which may radiate to the buttocks or thighs
- Muscle stiffness and spasm
- Reduced range of motion of the lumbar spine
- Pain worsened by certain movements, prolonged sitting, or standing
- **Radiculopathy (nerve root involvement):** Sharp, shooting pain radiating below the knee (sciatica), numbness, tingling, or weakness in a dermatomal distribution
- **Red flag symptoms (require urgent evaluation):**
  - Cauda equina syndrome: saddle anesthesia, urinary retention or incontinence, fecal incontinence, bilateral leg weakness
  - Progressive neurological deficit
  - Unexplained weight loss, fever, night pain (suspicion for malignancy or infection)
  - History of significant trauma or osteoporosis (suspicion for fracture)

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Non-specific:** No identifiable structural cause; involves muscular, ligamentous, and disc-related pain generators
- **Specific causes:** Lumbar disc herniation, spinal stenosis, spondylolisthesis, vertebral compression fractures, spinal infections, tumors
- **Risk factors:**
  - Physical: Heavy lifting, prolonged sitting, repetitive bending/twisting, vibration exposure, obesity, poor physical fitness
  - Psychosocial: Stress, anxiety, depression, fear-avoidance beliefs, low job satisfaction, catastrophizing
  - Other: Smoking, increasing age, previous episodes of LBP, sedentary lifestyle

## Diagnosis
- **Clinical assessment:** History and physical examination are sufficient for most patients. Focus on identifying red flags and neurological deficits.
- **Neurological examination:** Straight leg raise test (sensitivity ~90% for L5/S1 disc herniation), motor strength, sensation, reflexes
- **Imaging — NOT routinely recommended for non-specific LBP:**
  - MRI: Indicated when red flags are present, neurological deficits persist >6 weeks, or surgical intervention is considered. Note: disc degeneration, bulges, and protrusions are extremely common incidental findings in asymptomatic individuals.
  - X-ray: For suspected fracture, spondylolisthesis, or inflammatory spondyloarthropathy
- **Laboratory:** ESR, CRP, CBC — when infection, malignancy, or inflammatory disease suspected

## Treatment
**WHO 2023 guideline recommendations for chronic LBP (non-surgical):**

**Non-pharmacological (first-line — strongly recommended):**
- Structured exercise programs (core strengthening, aerobic exercise, yoga, Pilates)
- Spinal manipulative therapy
- Massage therapy
- Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)
- Multidisciplinary rehabilitation (combining physical and psychological approaches)
- Patient education: Reassurance about benign nature, encouragement to stay active, avoidance of bed rest

**Pharmacological (second-line — when non-pharmacological approaches insufficient):**
- NSAIDs: Shortest effective duration (ibuprofen, naproxen)
- Muscle relaxants: Short-term use only
- Duloxetine: For chronic LBP with neuropathic component
- **NOT recommended:** Opioids for chronic LBP (risk of dependence, limited long-term efficacy), paracetamol alone (insufficient evidence of efficacy)

**Interventional / surgical (for specific causes):**
- Epidural steroid injections: Short-term relief for radiculopathy (limited long-term benefit)
- Surgery: Discectomy for persistent radiculopathy from disc herniation; spinal fusion for instability/spondylolisthesis; decompression for symptomatic spinal stenosis

## Prevention
- Regular physical activity and exercise (strengthening core muscles, maintaining flexibility)
- Maintain healthy body weight
- Proper lifting techniques (bend knees, keep load close to body)
- Ergonomic workplace modifications (adjustable chair, monitor height, standing desk options)
- Avoid prolonged static postures (take regular breaks from sitting)
- Smoking cessation
- Address psychosocial risk factors early (stress management, workplace support)

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek immediate medical attention if back pain is accompanied by loss of bladder or bowel control, saddle area numbness, progressive leg weakness, or fever with unexplained weight loss. Consult a healthcare provider if back pain persists beyond 6 weeks, progressively worsens, or is associated with significant leg pain, numbness, or weakness below the knee. Self-care measures including staying active and using short-term NSAIDs are appropriate for most acute episodes.
