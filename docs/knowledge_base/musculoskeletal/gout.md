# Gout

## Key Facts
- Gout is the most common form of inflammatory arthritis, caused by deposition of monosodium urate (MSU) crystals in joints and soft tissues.
- Global prevalence is estimated at 1–4% of the adult population, with rising trends linked to aging populations and dietary changes.
- Gout is 3–4 times more common in men than women; women are predominantly affected after menopause.
- Gout is directly caused by chronic hyperuricemia (serum uric acid >6.8 mg/dL / >404 µmol/L).
- It is one of the few rheumatic diseases that can be effectively cured with appropriate long-term urate-lowering therapy.
- Gout is strongly associated with cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, metabolic syndrome, and diabetes.

## Overview
Gout results from the deposition of monosodium urate crystals in and around joints due to sustained hyperuricemia. When serum uric acid exceeds its saturation point (6.8 mg/dL), urate crystals can precipitate in synovial fluid, cartilage, and soft tissues, triggering an intense inflammatory response.

The natural history of gout progresses through stages: asymptomatic hyperuricemia → acute gouty arthritis (flares) → intercritical periods (between flares) → chronic tophaceous gout (if untreated). With appropriate urate-lowering therapy, crystal deposits can be dissolved and gout can be effectively cured.

## Symptoms
- **Acute gout flare:** Sudden onset of excruciating joint pain, swelling, warmth, redness, and exquisite tenderness — typically reaching peak severity within 12–24 hours. The first metatarsophalangeal joint (big toe) is affected in ~50% of first attacks (podagra).
- **Common joints:** First MTP, midfoot, ankle, knee, wrist, fingers, elbows
- **Intercritical gout:** Asymptomatic intervals between flares (early disease); without treatment, flares become more frequent and polyarticular
- **Chronic tophaceous gout:** Visible tophi (chalky deposits of urate crystals) in soft tissues — ears, fingers, elbows, Achilles tendon. Chronic joint pain and deformity. Joint destruction.
- **Renal complications:** Uric acid nephrolithiasis (kidney stones), urate nephropathy

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Hyperuricemia** — the necessary precursor (though not all hyperuricemic individuals develop gout):
  - Underexcretion of uric acid (~90% of cases): Genetic factors, chronic kidney disease, medications (thiazide diuretics, loop diuretics, low-dose aspirin, cyclosporine)
  - Overproduction of uric acid (~10%): High-purine diet, genetic enzyme defects, myeloproliferative disorders, tumor lysis syndrome
- **Diet:** Red meat, organ meats (liver, kidney), shellfish, fructose-sweetened beverages, alcohol (especially beer and spirits)
- **Obesity and metabolic syndrome:** Strong association
- **Male sex:** Men develop gout earlier (peak onset 40–60); women after menopause (estrogen promotes uric acid excretion)
- **Genetics:** Urate transporter gene variants (SLC2A9, ABCG2)
- **Comorbidities:** Hypertension, chronic kidney disease, diabetes, cardiovascular disease

## Diagnosis
- **Gold standard:** Identification of negatively birefringent MSU crystals in synovial fluid or tophus aspirate under polarized light microscopy
- **Clinical diagnosis:** Often made without joint aspiration when typical presentation (acute monoarthritis of first MTP, rapid onset, self-limiting) + hyperuricemia. 2015 ACR/EULAR classification criteria available (score-based).
- **Serum uric acid:** Often elevated but can be normal or low during acute flares (not reliable for diagnosis during a flare)
- **Inflammatory markers:** Elevated ESR, CRP, WBC during acute flares
- **Imaging:**
  - X-ray: Normal in early disease; "punched-out" erosions with overhanging edges (Martel sign) and preserved joint space in chronic gout
  - Ultrasound: "Double contour sign" (hyperechoic line on cartilage surface) — highly specific
  - Dual-energy CT (DECT): Detects urate crystal deposits with high sensitivity and specificity; useful for equivocal cases
- **Exclude septic arthritis:** Joint aspiration with Gram stain and culture — critical in acute monoarthritis (gout and septic arthritis can coexist)

## Treatment
**Acute flare management:**
- NSAIDs: Naproxen 500 mg twice daily or indomethacin (first-line if no contraindications)
- Colchicine: Most effective within 12 hours of flare onset (1.2 mg then 0.6 mg one hour later; low-dose regimen)
- Corticosteroids: Oral prednisone, intra-articular injection, or IM triamcinolone — for patients with NSAID/colchicine contraindications
- IL-1 inhibitors (anakinra, canakinumab): For refractory flares

**Urate-lowering therapy (ULT) — long-term management:**
- **Indications:** Recurrent flares (≥2/year), tophi, urate arthropathy, renal stones
- **Target:** Serum uric acid <6 mg/dL (<360 µmol/L); <5 mg/dL for severe/tophaceous gout
- **Xanthine oxidase inhibitors (first-line):** Allopurinol (start low 100 mg/day, titrate up; HLA-B*5801 screening recommended in high-risk populations), febuxostat
- **Uricosurics:** Probenecid, lesinurad — for patients intolerant to xanthine oxidase inhibitors or with underexcretion
- **Pegloticase:** IV uricase for severe refractory tophaceous gout
- **Flare prophylaxis during ULT initiation:** Low-dose colchicine (0.5–0.6 mg daily) or low-dose NSAID for 3–6 months

## Prevention
- Dietary modifications: Limit red meat, organ meats, shellfish, and fructose-sweetened beverages; moderate alcohol consumption (avoid beer)
- Maintain healthy body weight (gradual weight loss; avoid crash diets which can precipitate flares)
- Stay well hydrated (≥2 L water/day)
- Review medications that increase uric acid (substitute alternatives when possible)
- Cherry consumption and vitamin C supplementation may modestly reduce uric acid levels
- Adherence to urate-lowering therapy for lifelong crystal dissolution and flare prevention
- Manage cardiovascular risk factors and comorbidities

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek prompt medical attention for a sudden, intensely painful, swollen, red joint — especially if accompanied by fever (to exclude septic arthritis). Consult a healthcare provider if you experience recurrent episodes of joint inflammation, visible lumps near joints (tophi), or kidney stones. Patients diagnosed with gout should follow up regularly to ensure serum uric acid is at target and to monitor for comorbidities.
