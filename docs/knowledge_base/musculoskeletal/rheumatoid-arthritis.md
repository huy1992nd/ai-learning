# Rheumatoid Arthritis

## Key Facts
- Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic, systemic autoimmune inflammatory disease that primarily affects the joints.
- RA affects approximately 18 million people worldwide (2019 estimates).
- It is 2–3 times more common in women than men.
- Typical onset occurs between ages 30 and 60, though it can develop at any age.
- Without adequate treatment, RA causes progressive joint destruction, deformity, and disability.
- Early diagnosis and aggressive treatment with disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs) can achieve disease remission and prevent joint damage.

## Overview
Rheumatoid arthritis is a chronic autoimmune disease characterized by persistent synovial inflammation leading to progressive joint destruction, functional impairment, and systemic complications. The disease primarily affects the small joints of the hands and feet symmetrically but can involve any synovial joint.

RA is driven by immune dysregulation, with autoreactive T cells and B cells producing autoantibodies (rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP antibodies) and inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1) that perpetuate synovial inflammation, pannus formation, and erosion of cartilage and bone.

## Symptoms
- **Joint symptoms:** Symmetric polyarthritis — pain, swelling, warmth, and tenderness of multiple joints, especially MCP, PIP, and wrist joints. Morning stiffness lasting >60 minutes (hallmark of inflammatory arthritis).
- **Joint deformities (advanced disease):** Swan-neck deformity, boutonnière deformity, ulnar deviation, Z-thumb
- **Constitutional symptoms:** Fatigue, malaise, low-grade fever, weight loss
- **Extra-articular manifestations:**
  - Rheumatoid nodules (subcutaneous, over bony prominences)
  - Pulmonary: interstitial lung disease, pleural effusion
  - Cardiovascular: accelerated atherosclerosis, pericarditis
  - Ocular: scleritis, episcleritis, keratoconjunctivitis sicca
  - Hematologic: anemia of chronic disease, Felty syndrome (RA + splenomegaly + neutropenia)
  - Vasculitis (rare, severe RA)

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Autoimmune etiology:** Immune-mediated attack on synovial membrane
- **Genetics:** HLA-DR4 (shared epitope) — strongest genetic risk; heritability ~50–60%
- **Sex:** Female predominance (2–3:1); hormonal influences
- **Smoking:** The strongest modifiable risk factor; increases risk 1.5–2× and worsens disease severity, especially in genetically susceptible individuals (shared epitope positive)
- **Infections:** Periodontitis (Porphyromonas gingivalis), EBV, and other infections may trigger RA in genetically predisposed individuals
- **Obesity:** Modestly increases risk
- **Silica dust exposure:** Occupational risk factor

## Diagnosis
- **2010 ACR/EULAR Classification Criteria:** Score ≥6/10 based on joint involvement, serology (RF, anti-CCP), acute-phase reactants (ESR, CRP), and symptom duration (≥6 weeks).
- **Laboratory:**
  - Rheumatoid factor (RF): Positive in ~70–80% but not specific
  - Anti-CCP antibodies: High specificity (~95%) for RA; positive in ~60–70%
  - ESR and CRP: Elevated during active disease
  - CBC: Anemia of chronic disease, thrombocytosis
- **Imaging:**
  - X-ray (hands and feet): Periarticular osteopenia, joint space narrowing, marginal erosions (may be normal early)
  - Ultrasound: Detects synovitis, tenosynovitis, and early erosions with high sensitivity
  - MRI: Most sensitive for early erosions and synovitis
- **Exclude other causes:** Synovial fluid analysis (inflammatory: WBC 2000–75,000/mm³), rule out crystal arthropathy, SLE, psoriatic arthritis

## Treatment
**Treat-to-target strategy** — aim for remission or low disease activity:

**First-line DMARD:**
- Methotrexate (MTX): Cornerstone of RA treatment; started early, typically 15–25 mg/week with folic acid supplementation

**Combination DMARDs (if inadequate response):**
- MTX + sulfasalazine + hydroxychloroquine (triple therapy)
- MTX + leflunomide

**Biologic DMARDs (if conventional DMARD failure):**
- TNF inhibitors: adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab, certolizumab, golimumab
- IL-6 inhibitors: tocilizumab, sarilumab
- T-cell co-stimulation inhibitor: abatacept
- B-cell depleting: rituximab
- JAK inhibitors (targeted synthetic DMARDs): tofacitinib, baricitinib, upadacitinib

**Symptomatic treatment:**
- NSAIDs and low-dose corticosteroids for bridging symptom control (corticosteroids tapered as soon as possible)

**Non-pharmacological:**
- Physiotherapy, occupational therapy, joint protection strategies, patient education
- Surgery: Synovectomy, joint replacement for severely damaged joints

## Prevention
- There is no known way to prevent RA.
- Smoking cessation reduces risk, particularly in genetically susceptible individuals.
- Maintaining good oral health (periodontal disease treatment) may reduce risk.
- Early detection and prompt initiation of DMARDs within the "window of opportunity" (first 3–6 months of symptoms) dramatically improves long-term outcomes.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek medical evaluation if you experience persistent joint swelling, pain, and stiffness (especially morning stiffness >30 minutes) lasting more than 6 weeks, particularly if it affects the small joints of the hands and feet symmetrically. Early referral to a rheumatologist is critical for timely diagnosis and treatment. Urgent evaluation is needed for severe joint flares, suspected infections, or new systemic symptoms.
