# Osteoporosis

## Key Facts
- Osteoporosis is a systemic skeletal disease characterized by low bone mass and microarchitectural deterioration, leading to increased bone fragility and fracture risk.
- An estimated 200 million people worldwide are affected by osteoporosis.
- Osteoporotic fractures occur in 1 in 3 women and 1 in 5 men over age 50.
- Hip fractures are the most serious consequence, with mortality rates of 20–30% within one year in elderly patients.
- Osteoporosis is often called the "silent disease" because bone loss occurs without symptoms until a fracture happens.
- Prevention and early detection through bone density screening are essential.

## Overview
Osteoporosis results from an imbalance between bone resorption and bone formation, leading to net bone loss. Bone density peaks in the late 20s to early 30s (peak bone mass) and gradually declines with age. In women, bone loss accelerates dramatically during the 5–7 years following menopause due to estrogen deficiency.

The most common osteoporotic fractures occur at the hip, vertebral spine, and distal radius (wrist). Vertebral fractures are the most frequent but often go undiagnosed. Hip fractures carry the greatest morbidity and mortality.

## Symptoms
- Osteoporosis itself is asymptomatic until a fracture occurs
- **Vertebral compression fractures:** Acute or chronic back pain, loss of height, progressive thoracic kyphosis ("dowager's hump")
- **Hip fracture:** Sudden severe hip/groin pain after a fall, inability to bear weight
- **Wrist fracture (Colles' fracture):** Pain and deformity after falling onto outstretched hand
- **Other fragility fractures:** Humerus, pelvis, ribs — occurring with minimal trauma (e.g., a fall from standing height or less)

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Non-modifiable:**
- Age (risk increases sharply after 50)
- Female sex (postmenopausal estrogen deficiency)
- Family history of osteoporosis or hip fracture
- Ethnicity (highest in Caucasian and Asian populations)
- Small body frame

**Modifiable:**
- Low calcium and vitamin D intake
- Physical inactivity / sedentary lifestyle
- Smoking
- Excessive alcohol consumption (>3 units/day)
- Low body weight (BMI <20)

**Secondary causes:**
- Medications: Long-term glucocorticoids (most common drug-induced cause), aromatase inhibitors, anticonvulsants, proton pump inhibitors
- Endocrine: Hypogonadism, hyperthyroidism, hyperparathyroidism, Cushing syndrome
- Other: Rheumatoid arthritis, celiac disease, chronic kidney disease, anorexia nervosa

## Diagnosis
- **Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA):** Gold standard for measuring bone mineral density (BMD). Measured at hip and lumbar spine. Results reported as T-scores:
  - Normal: T-score ≥ −1.0
  - Osteopenia: T-score −1.0 to −2.5
  - Osteoporosis: T-score ≤ −2.5
  - Severe osteoporosis: T-score ≤ −2.5 with fragility fracture
- **FRAX® tool:** WHO-endorsed fracture risk assessment tool; estimates 10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture and hip fracture based on clinical risk factors ± BMD.
- **Laboratory:** Calcium, phosphate, 25-hydroxyvitamin D, PTH, TSH, renal and liver function, CBC — to identify secondary causes. Bone turnover markers (CTX, P1NP) for monitoring treatment response.
- **Vertebral fracture assessment (VFA):** Lateral spine imaging by DXA or X-ray to detect prevalent vertebral fractures.

## Treatment
**Non-pharmacological:**
- Calcium: 1000–1200 mg/day (preferably from diet)
- Vitamin D: 800–2000 IU/day to maintain serum 25(OH)D ≥30 ng/mL
- Weight-bearing and resistance exercise
- Fall prevention: Home safety modifications, balance training, vision correction, medication review

**Pharmacological (for patients with osteoporosis or high fracture risk):**
- **Bisphosphonates (first-line):** Alendronate, risedronate (oral, weekly), zoledronic acid (IV, annually) — inhibit osteoclast-mediated bone resorption
- **Denosumab:** Anti-RANKL monoclonal antibody (SC every 6 months); effective alternative to bisphosphonates
- **Anabolic agents (for severe osteoporosis):** Teriparatide (PTH analog), romosozumab (anti-sclerostin — builds bone and reduces resorption)
- **Selective estrogen receptor modulators (SERMs):** Raloxifene — reduces vertebral fracture risk
- **Hormone replacement therapy:** Effective but reserved for menopausal symptom management due to other risks

Treatment duration: Bisphosphonates typically 3–5 years, then reassess ("drug holiday" for moderate-risk patients).

## Prevention
- Ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D intake from childhood through old age
- Regular weight-bearing and muscle-strengthening exercise throughout life
- Avoid smoking and excessive alcohol consumption
- Build strong peak bone mass during adolescence and early adulthood
- Screen postmenopausal women ≥65 years and men ≥70 years with DXA
- Screen younger adults with risk factors (glucocorticoid use, fragility fracture)
- Implement fall prevention strategies in elderly populations
- Manage secondary causes promptly

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if you have risk factors for osteoporosis (postmenopausal women, age >50, long-term corticosteroid use, family history of hip fracture). Seek evaluation for any fracture occurring with minimal trauma, unexplained back pain, or noticeable height loss. All adults ≥50 with a fragility fracture should have a bone density assessment and fracture risk evaluation.
