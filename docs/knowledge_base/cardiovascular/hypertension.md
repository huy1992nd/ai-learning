# Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

## Key Facts
- Hypertension is defined as persistently elevated blood pressure with systolic ≥140 mmHg and/or diastolic ≥90 mmHg.
- An estimated 1.28 billion adults aged 30–79 years worldwide have hypertension, with two-thirds living in low- and middle-income countries.
- Hypertension is the leading modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease and premature death globally.
- Approximately 46% of adults with hypertension are unaware of their condition.
- Only about 21% of adults with hypertension have it under control.
- Lifestyle modification and affordable medications can effectively control blood pressure and reduce cardiovascular risk.

## Overview
Hypertension, commonly known as high blood pressure, is a chronic medical condition in which the blood pressure in the arteries is persistently elevated. Blood pressure is recorded as two numbers: systolic pressure (pressure when the heart beats) over diastolic pressure (pressure when the heart rests between beats), measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg).

Hypertension is classified as primary (essential) hypertension, which has no identifiable cause and accounts for 90–95% of cases, or secondary hypertension, which results from an underlying condition such as kidney disease, endocrine disorders, or medications. Hypertension is often called "the silent killer" because it typically causes no symptoms until significant organ damage has occurred.

## Symptoms
Most people with hypertension have no symptoms, even when blood pressure readings are dangerously high. Occasionally, some individuals may experience:

- Headaches (usually only in hypertensive crisis: systolic >180 mmHg)
- Shortness of breath
- Nosebleeds
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Chest pain
- Visual changes
- Blood in urine

**Hypertensive crisis** (systolic >180 mmHg and/or diastolic >120 mmHg) may cause severe headache, anxiety, chest pain, blurred vision, nausea, and vomiting. This requires emergency medical treatment.

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Non-modifiable risk factors:**
- Age (risk increases with age)
- Family history of hypertension
- Ethnicity (higher prevalence in people of African descent)
- Genetic predisposition

**Modifiable risk factors:**
- Excessive sodium intake (>5 g salt/day)
- Low potassium intake (insufficient fruits and vegetables)
- Physical inactivity
- Overweight and obesity (BMI ≥25)
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Tobacco use (smoking and smokeless tobacco)
- Chronic stress
- Comorbidities: diabetes, kidney disease, sleep apnea

## Diagnosis
- **Blood pressure measurement:** Using a validated, calibrated device. Blood pressure should be measured on at least two separate occasions to confirm hypertension.
- **Office blood pressure:** Standard measurement performed by a healthcare professional.
- **Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM):** 24-hour monitoring; considered the gold standard for diagnosis, detecting white-coat and masked hypertension.
- **Home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM):** Self-measurement at home; average of multiple readings over several days.
- **Additional investigations:** Urinalysis, blood glucose, serum creatinine, electrolytes, lipid profile, ECG — to assess target organ damage and cardiovascular risk.

## Treatment
Treatment aims to reduce blood pressure to target levels (<140/90 mmHg for most adults; <130/80 mmHg for high-risk groups):

- **Lifestyle modifications (first-line for all patients):**
  - Reduce dietary sodium to <5 g salt per day
  - Increase potassium-rich foods (fruits, vegetables)
  - Regular physical activity (at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week)
  - Maintain a healthy body weight (BMI 18.5–24.9)
  - Limit alcohol consumption
  - Quit smoking

- **Pharmacotherapy:** Initiated when lifestyle measures alone are insufficient, or immediately for higher-risk patients:
  - ACE inhibitors (e.g., enalapril, lisinopril) or ARBs (e.g., losartan, valsartan)
  - Calcium channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine, nifedipine)
  - Thiazide diuretics (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone)
  - Beta-blockers (e.g., bisoprolol) for specific indications (heart failure, post-MI)
  - Combination therapy is often required to achieve target blood pressure

## Prevention
- Adopt a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy (DASH diet pattern)
- Reduce salt intake to less than 5 grams per day
- Engage in regular physical activity
- Maintain a healthy weight
- Avoid tobacco and limit alcohol consumption
- Manage stress through healthy coping mechanisms
- Regular blood pressure screening (at least annually for adults ≥18 years)

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Have your blood pressure checked regularly by a healthcare provider. Seek immediate emergency care if you experience a blood pressure reading above 180/120 mmHg accompanied by symptoms such as severe headache, chest pain, shortness of breath, visual changes, confusion, or difficulty speaking, as this may indicate a hypertensive crisis or stroke.
