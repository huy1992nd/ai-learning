# Heart Failure

## Key Facts
- Heart failure is a clinical syndrome in which the heart is unable to pump blood efficiently enough to meet the body's metabolic demands.
- An estimated 64 million people worldwide are living with heart failure.
- Heart failure is a leading cause of hospitalization in adults over 65 years of age.
- The most common causes are coronary heart disease, hypertension, valvular heart disease, and cardiomyopathy.
- Heart failure is a chronic, progressive condition, but symptoms can be managed and outcomes improved with medications, lifestyle changes, and in some cases, devices or surgery.
- Five-year survival after heart failure diagnosis is approximately 50%, highlighting the need for early detection and treatment.

## Overview
Heart failure (HF) is a complex clinical syndrome that results from any structural or functional impairment of ventricular filling or ejection of blood. It is not a single disease but the end result of many cardiovascular conditions. Heart failure is classified by the left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF):

- **HF with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF):** LVEF ≤40%; the heart muscle is weakened and cannot contract effectively.
- **HF with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF):** LVEF ≥50%; the heart muscle is stiff and cannot relax and fill properly.
- **HF with mildly reduced ejection fraction (HFmrEF):** LVEF 41–49%.

Heart failure can be classified as left-sided (most common), right-sided, or biventricular. It may develop acutely (e.g., after myocardial infarction) or gradually over months to years.

## Symptoms
Symptoms vary depending on which side of the heart is affected and the severity:

**Left-sided heart failure:**
- Shortness of breath (dyspnea), initially on exertion, progressing to rest
- Orthopnea (breathlessness when lying flat)
- Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (waking up at night gasping for air)
- Fatigue and weakness
- Reduced exercise tolerance
- Persistent cough or wheezing (sometimes with pink, frothy sputum)

**Right-sided heart failure:**
- Swelling (edema) in feet, ankles, legs, or abdomen (ascites)
- Weight gain from fluid retention
- Loss of appetite and nausea
- Jugular venous distension

**Advanced heart failure:** Severe breathlessness at rest, confusion, rapid or irregular heartbeat, inability to perform daily activities.

The New York Heart Association (NYHA) classification grades heart failure severity from Class I (no limitation of physical activity) to Class IV (symptoms at rest).

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Common causes:**
- Coronary artery disease / myocardial infarction (most common cause of HFrEF)
- Hypertension (leading cause of HFpEF)
- Valvular heart disease (aortic stenosis, mitral regurgitation)
- Cardiomyopathy (dilated, hypertrophic, restrictive)
- Myocarditis (inflammatory)
- Congenital heart disease

**Risk factors:**
- Age (>65 years), diabetes, obesity, chronic kidney disease, atrial fibrillation, excessive alcohol use, cardiotoxic chemotherapy (anthracyclines), sleep apnea, family history of cardiomyopathy.

## Diagnosis
- **Clinical assessment:** History of dyspnea, orthopnea, edema; physical examination revealing elevated jugular venous pressure, pulmonary crackles, peripheral edema, hepatomegaly.
- **BNP / NT-proBNP:** Natriuretic peptide blood tests — elevated levels support the diagnosis of heart failure (BNP >100 pg/mL or NT-proBNP >300 pg/mL).
- **Echocardiography:** Essential for assessing cardiac structure and function, measuring ejection fraction, evaluating valvular disease, and identifying the underlying cause.
- **Chest X-ray:** May show cardiomegaly, pulmonary congestion, pleural effusions.
- **ECG:** May reveal arrhythmias, left ventricular hypertrophy, or ischaemic changes.
- **Additional tests:** Coronary angiography (if ischaemic cause suspected), cardiac MRI (for cardiomyopathy evaluation), blood tests (renal function, liver function, thyroid function, iron studies).

## Treatment
**HFrEF (guideline-directed medical therapy):**
- ACE inhibitors or ARBs (e.g., enalapril, valsartan) — or ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan, preferred)
- Beta-blockers (carvedilol, bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate)
- Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (spironolactone, eplerenone)
- SGLT2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) — now a pillar of HF therapy
- Diuretics (furosemide) for fluid overload and symptom relief
- Hydralazine + isosorbide dinitrate (alternative for patients intolerant to ACE/ARB)

**Device therapy:**
- Implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD) for prevention of sudden cardiac death in selected patients
- Cardiac resynchronization therapy (CRT) for patients with wide QRS complex and reduced EF

**Advanced therapies:** Left ventricular assist device (LVAD) or heart transplantation for end-stage heart failure refractory to medical therapy.

**HFpEF:** SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin), diuretics for congestion, management of comorbidities (hypertension, atrial fibrillation, obesity, diabetes).

## Prevention
- Control hypertension and diabetes
- Treat coronary artery disease and valvular heart disease promptly
- Maintain a healthy weight and exercise regularly
- Limit alcohol intake; avoid excessive consumption
- Do not use illicit drugs (cocaine and amphetamines are cardiotoxic)
- Quit smoking
- Seek medical follow-up if you have a family history of cardiomyopathy

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek medical attention if you notice progressive shortness of breath, new or worsening swelling in your legs or abdomen, rapid unexplained weight gain (>2 kg in 2–3 days), inability to sleep flat, or persistent cough. Seek emergency care for sudden severe breathlessness, chest pain, fainting, or rapid/irregular heartbeat.
