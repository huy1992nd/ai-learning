# Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident)

## Key Facts
- Stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is interrupted or reduced, depriving brain tissue of oxygen and nutrients.
- Stroke is the second leading cause of death globally, responsible for approximately 6.6 million deaths in 2019.
- Stroke is also the third leading cause of disability worldwide.
- There are two main types: ischaemic stroke (about 85%) caused by a blood clot, and haemorrhagic stroke (about 15%) caused by bleeding.
- Up to 80% of strokes are preventable through management of key risk factors: hypertension, tobacco use, diabetes, and physical inactivity.
- Rapid recognition and treatment (within hours) can significantly reduce brain damage and improve outcomes.

## Overview
A stroke, also known as a cerebrovascular accident (CVA), occurs when blood flow to a region of the brain is suddenly interrupted, causing brain cells to die from oxygen deprivation. Stroke is a medical emergency — every minute of delay in treatment results in the loss of approximately 1.9 million neurons.

**Ischaemic stroke** is caused by a blockage (usually a blood clot) in an artery supplying the brain. The clot may form locally (thrombotic stroke) or travel from elsewhere in the body (embolic stroke, often from the heart in atrial fibrillation).

**Haemorrhagic stroke** results from the rupture of a blood vessel in or around the brain, leading to intracerebral haemorrhage or subarachnoid haemorrhage. Haemorrhagic strokes are less common but have higher mortality rates.

**Transient ischaemic attack (TIA)** is a temporary episode of neurological dysfunction caused by brief ischaemia without permanent brain damage. TIA is a warning sign of future stroke.

## Symptoms
Stroke symptoms appear suddenly. The **FAST** acronym helps recognize stroke:

- **F — Face drooping:** One side of the face droops or feels numb. Ask the person to smile — is it uneven?
- **A — Arm weakness:** One arm is weak or numb. Ask the person to raise both arms — does one drift downward?
- **S — Speech difficulty:** Speech is slurred or the person has difficulty speaking or understanding.
- **T — Time to call emergency services:** If any of these signs are observed, call for emergency help immediately.

Other symptoms may include: sudden severe headache with no known cause, sudden confusion, sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes, sudden dizziness or loss of balance, and sudden numbness in the face, arm, or leg (especially on one side of the body).

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Medical risk factors:**
- Hypertension (the single most important modifiable risk factor)
- Atrial fibrillation (increases stroke risk 5-fold)
- Diabetes mellitus
- Dyslipidemia (high cholesterol)
- Carotid artery stenosis
- Previous stroke or TIA
- Sickle cell disease

**Lifestyle risk factors:**
- Tobacco smoking
- Physical inactivity
- Unhealthy diet (high sodium, low fruits/vegetables)
- Obesity
- Excessive alcohol consumption

**Non-modifiable risk factors:** Age (risk doubles each decade after 55), male sex (slightly higher risk), family history, ethnicity (higher in people of African descent).

## Diagnosis
- **CT scan of the brain (non-contrast):** First-line emergency imaging to distinguish between ischaemic and haemorrhagic stroke. Must be performed urgently before treatment decisions.
- **MRI (diffusion-weighted imaging):** More sensitive than CT for detecting early ischaemic changes and small infarcts.
- **CT angiography (CTA):** Identifies large vessel occlusion for potential mechanical thrombectomy.
- **ECG and cardiac monitoring:** Detects atrial fibrillation or other cardiac sources of embolism.
- **Blood tests:** Glucose, coagulation studies, complete blood count, renal function.
- **Carotid ultrasound:** Assesses carotid artery stenosis as a potential source of thromboembolism.

## Treatment
**Acute ischaemic stroke (time-critical):**
- **Intravenous thrombolysis:** Alteplase (tPA) administered within 4.5 hours of symptom onset to dissolve the clot.
- **Mechanical thrombectomy:** Endovascular removal of large vessel occlusions, effective up to 24 hours after onset in selected patients.
- **Antiplatelet therapy:** Aspirin initiated within 24–48 hours for ischaemic stroke (after excluding haemorrhage).

**Haemorrhagic stroke:**
- Blood pressure control to reduce further bleeding
- Reversal of anticoagulation if applicable
- Surgical intervention (craniotomy, decompressive surgery) in selected cases
- Management of raised intracranial pressure

**Post-stroke care:**
- Stroke unit admission (improves outcomes)
- Secondary prevention: antihypertensives, statins, antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapy (for atrial fibrillation)
- Rehabilitation: physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy — commenced early
- Management of complications: dysphagia, deep vein thrombosis, depression

## Prevention
- Control blood pressure (target <140/90 mmHg; <130/80 for high-risk groups)
- Treat atrial fibrillation with anticoagulation therapy
- Quit smoking
- Manage diabetes and maintain glycaemic control
- Maintain a healthy diet (reduce salt, increase fruits and vegetables)
- Exercise regularly (≥150 minutes moderate-intensity activity per week)
- Maintain a healthy weight
- Limit alcohol consumption
- Take prescribed antiplatelet or anticoagulant medications as directed

## When to Seek Medical Attention
**Stroke is a medical emergency.** Call emergency services immediately (do NOT drive yourself) if you or someone nearby suddenly develops facial drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty, severe headache, confusion, or loss of vision or balance. Note the time symptoms started — this is critical for treatment decisions. Remember: "Time is brain."
