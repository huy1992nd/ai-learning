# Rheumatic Heart Disease

## Key Facts
- Rheumatic heart disease (RHD) results from damage to heart valves caused by one or more episodes of acute rheumatic fever (ARF), an autoimmune response to group A streptococcal throat infection.
- An estimated 40.5 million people were living with RHD globally in 2019, with approximately 306,000 deaths annually.
- RHD is the most common acquired heart disease in children and young adults in low- and middle-income countries.
- The disease disproportionately affects populations in poverty, overcrowded living conditions, and with limited access to healthcare.
- RHD is preventable through prompt antibiotic treatment of streptococcal pharyngitis and secondary prophylaxis with penicillin.
- Advanced RHD often requires surgical intervention (valve repair or replacement).

## Overview
Rheumatic heart disease is a chronic cardiac condition resulting from acute rheumatic fever, which is an inflammatory disease triggered by an autoimmune response to group A *Streptococcus* (GAS) pharyngitis (strep throat). When strep throat is not treated with antibiotics, the body's immune response can attack its own tissues, particularly the heart valves, joints, skin, and brain.

Repeated episodes of ARF lead to progressive, cumulative valve damage (rheumatic carditis). The mitral valve is most commonly affected, followed by the aortic valve. Valve damage leads to stenosis (narrowing), regurgitation (leaking), or both, eventually causing heart failure if left untreated. RHD remains a significant public health problem in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and the Pacific Islands.

## Symptoms
**Acute rheumatic fever (initial episode):**
- Migratory polyarthritis (joint pain and swelling moving from one joint to another)
- Carditis (inflammation of the heart): chest pain, new heart murmur, heart failure
- Erythema marginatum (painless, non-itchy rash with pink rings)
- Subcutaneous nodules (painless, firm lumps under the skin)
- Sydenham chorea (involuntary, jerky movements of face, hands, and feet)
- Fever

**Chronic rheumatic heart disease:**
- Shortness of breath (especially on exertion and lying down)
- Fatigue
- Heart palpitations
- Chest pain
- Swelling of feet and ankles
- Fainting episodes

Symptoms of chronic RHD depend on which valves are affected and the severity of damage. Many patients are asymptomatic for years before decompensation occurs.

## Causes / Risk Factors
The causal pathway begins with group A streptococcal pharyngitis:

1. Untreated or inadequately treated GAS pharyngitis
2. Autoimmune cross-reaction (molecular mimicry between GAS antigens and human cardiac tissue)
3. Acute rheumatic fever (1–5 weeks after strep throat)
4. Recurrent ARF episodes cause cumulative valve damage → RHD

**Risk factors:**
- Age 5–15 years (peak age for ARF)
- Poverty and overcrowded living conditions
- Limited access to healthcare and antibiotics
- Family history of ARF/RHD
- Indigenous and marginalized populations

## Diagnosis
**Acute rheumatic fever:**
- Jones criteria (revised): Major and minor criteria plus evidence of preceding GAS infection (positive throat culture, rapid antigen test, or elevated streptococcal antibodies — ASO or anti-DNase B).

**Rheumatic heart disease:**
- **Echocardiography:** The primary diagnostic tool; identifies valvular stenosis, regurgitation, and morphological changes (thickening, restricted leaflet mobility, commissural fusion). Echocardiographic screening programs have identified subclinical RHD in endemic populations.
- **Cardiac auscultation:** Heart murmurs (characteristic murmur of mitral stenosis is a low-pitched diastolic rumble).
- **ECG:** May show atrial enlargement, atrial fibrillation, or ventricular hypertrophy.
- **Chest X-ray:** Cardiomegaly, left atrial enlargement, pulmonary congestion.

## Treatment
**Acute rheumatic fever:**
- Antibiotic therapy: Benzathine penicillin G (single intramuscular injection) or oral penicillin V for 10 days to eradicate GAS. Alternatives: amoxicillin, or macrolides for penicillin-allergic patients.
- Anti-inflammatory therapy: Aspirin or naproxen for arthritis and mild carditis; corticosteroids for severe carditis with heart failure.
- Management of chorea: Supportive; carbamazepine or valproic acid for severe cases.

**Secondary prophylaxis (prevention of recurrence):**
- Benzathine penicillin G intramuscular injection every 3–4 weeks — the most effective measure to prevent recurrent ARF and progressive valve damage.
- Duration: minimum 10 years after last ARF episode, or until age 21 (whichever is longer); lifelong for patients with established RHD.

**Chronic RHD:**
- Medical management: Diuretics, ACE inhibitors for heart failure; anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation; management of complications.
- Surgical intervention: Valve repair (preferred when feasible) or valve replacement (mechanical or bioprosthetic) for severe symptomatic valve disease. Access to cardiac surgery remains a major barrier in endemic regions.
- Infective endocarditis prophylaxis: Antibiotic prophylaxis before dental procedures in patients with prosthetic valves.

## Prevention
- **Primary prevention:** Prompt diagnosis and antibiotic treatment of streptococcal pharyngitis (sore throat) — "treat strep, prevent ARF."
- **Secondary prevention:** Long-term benzathine penicillin G prophylaxis for individuals with a history of ARF or established RHD to prevent recurrences.
- **Primordial prevention:** Improving living conditions, reducing overcrowding, and ensuring access to healthcare in endemic communities.
- **Screening programs:** Echocardiographic screening in high-risk populations (school-age children in endemic areas) to detect subclinical RHD.
- **Health education:** Raising awareness about sore throat treatment and the importance of adherence to secondary prophylaxis.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider for any sore throat with fever lasting more than 2–3 days, especially in children aged 5–15 years, to rule out streptococcal infection. Seek medical attention if you develop joint pain and swelling, chest pain, breathlessness, or unusual involuntary movements following a sore throat episode. Patients with known RHD should seek immediate care for worsening breathlessness, palpitations, or signs of heart failure.
