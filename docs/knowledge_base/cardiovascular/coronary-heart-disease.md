# Coronary Heart Disease (Ischaemic Heart Disease)

## Key Facts
- Coronary heart disease (CHD) is the most common type of cardiovascular disease and the leading cause of death worldwide.
- CHD caused approximately 8.9 million deaths in 2019, representing 16% of all global deaths.
- It results from atherosclerotic plaque buildup in the coronary arteries, reducing blood flow to the heart muscle.
- Major modifiable risk factors include tobacco use, unhealthy diet, physical inactivity, and harmful use of alcohol.
- Acute manifestations include unstable angina and myocardial infarction (heart attack).
- CHD is largely preventable through addressing lifestyle risk factors and managing conditions such as hypertension, diabetes, and dyslipidemia.

## Overview
Coronary heart disease, also known as ischaemic heart disease or coronary artery disease, occurs when atherosclerotic plaques (deposits of fat, cholesterol, calcium, and other substances) build up inside the coronary arteries, which supply oxygen-rich blood to the heart muscle. This process, called atherosclerosis, develops gradually over decades, progressively narrowing the arteries and reducing myocardial blood flow.

When a plaque ruptures, it can trigger the formation of a blood clot (thrombus) that may partially or completely block the artery, leading to acute coronary syndromes: unstable angina or myocardial infarction. CHD encompasses a spectrum from stable angina pectoris to acute myocardial infarction and sudden cardiac death.

## Symptoms
**Stable angina:** Chest pain or discomfort (pressure, squeezing, heaviness) typically triggered by physical exertion or emotional stress and relieved by rest or nitroglycerin. Pain may radiate to the left arm, neck, jaw, shoulder, or back.

**Acute coronary syndrome (unstable angina / myocardial infarction):**
- Severe chest pain or pressure, often described as "an elephant sitting on the chest"
- Pain radiating to the left arm, both arms, neck, jaw, or back
- Shortness of breath
- Cold sweating, nausea, or vomiting
- Lightheadedness or fainting
- Anxiety or a sense of impending doom

**Atypical presentations:** Women, elderly patients, and individuals with diabetes may present with atypical symptoms such as fatigue, dyspnea, nausea, or abdominal discomfort without classic chest pain.

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Non-modifiable risk factors:**
- Age (men ≥45 years; women ≥55 years or post-menopausal)
- Male sex (higher risk, though women's risk increases after menopause)
- Family history of premature CHD (first-degree relative with CHD before age 55 in men or 65 in women)

**Modifiable risk factors:**
- Tobacco smoking (including secondhand smoke)
- Hypertension
- Dyslipidemia (elevated LDL cholesterol, low HDL cholesterol)
- Diabetes mellitus
- Overweight and obesity
- Physical inactivity
- Unhealthy diet (high in saturated fat, trans fat, sodium, and added sugars)
- Harmful alcohol consumption
- Psychosocial stress

## Diagnosis
- **Electrocardiogram (ECG):** Detects ischemic changes, ST-segment elevation (STEMI) or depression, T-wave inversions, and arrhythmias.
- **Cardiac biomarkers:** Troponin I or T levels — elevated in myocardial infarction; the primary diagnostic marker for acute MI.
- **Exercise stress test:** Evaluates cardiac response to physical exertion; detects exercise-induced ischemia.
- **Echocardiography:** Assesses ventricular function and wall motion abnormalities.
- **Coronary angiography (cardiac catheterization):** Gold standard for visualizing coronary artery anatomy and identifying the location and severity of stenoses.
- **CT coronary angiography:** Non-invasive imaging alternative for ruling out CHD in intermediate-risk patients.
- **Coronary calcium score:** CT-based assessment of calcified plaque burden for cardiovascular risk stratification.

## Treatment
**Acute management (myocardial infarction):**
- Emergency reperfusion therapy: primary percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) — stenting — or thrombolytic therapy (fibrinolysis) if PCI is not available within 120 minutes.
- Aspirin and P2Y12 inhibitor (dual antiplatelet therapy)
- Anticoagulation (heparin), supplemental oxygen, pain management (morphine if needed)

**Long-term medical therapy:**
- Antiplatelet agents (aspirin, clopidogrel)
- Statins (atorvastatin, rosuvastatin) for lipid-lowering
- ACE inhibitors or ARBs (especially if hypertension, diabetes, or reduced ejection fraction)
- Beta-blockers (post-MI, heart failure)
- Nitroglycerin for angina symptom relief

**Revascularization:**
- Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent placement
- Coronary artery bypass graft surgery (CABG) for severe multi-vessel disease or left main coronary artery disease

**Cardiac rehabilitation:** Structured exercise program, dietary counseling, smoking cessation support, psychological support.

## Prevention
- Do not smoke; avoid secondhand smoke
- Eat a heart-healthy diet (rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, fish, nuts; low in saturated fat, trans fat, and sodium)
- Engage in regular physical activity (at least 150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity exercise)
- Maintain a healthy weight
- Control blood pressure (<140/90 mmHg)
- Manage blood glucose levels (HbA1c <7% for diabetics)
- Manage cholesterol (LDL-C targets per cardiovascular risk)
- Limit alcohol intake
- Manage stress and seek support for mental health

## When to Seek Medical Attention
**Call emergency services immediately** if you experience chest pain or pressure lasting more than a few minutes, especially if accompanied by pain radiating to the arm/jaw/back, shortness of breath, cold sweating, nausea, or lightheadedness. Time is critical — "time is muscle" — and early treatment of myocardial infarction significantly improves survival.
