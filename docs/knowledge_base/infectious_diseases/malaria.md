# Malaria

## Key Facts
- Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by *Plasmodium* parasites transmitted through the bites of infected female *Anopheles* mosquitoes.
- In 2022, there were an estimated 249 million malaria cases and 608,000 malaria deaths in 85 countries.
- Children under 5 years of age are the most vulnerable group, accounting for approximately 80% of all malaria deaths in Africa.
- *Plasmodium falciparum* is the deadliest malaria parasite and the most prevalent on the African continent.
- Malaria is preventable and curable with timely diagnosis and effective treatment.
- Insecticide-treated nets (ITNs) and indoor residual spraying (IRS) are the two core vector control interventions.

## Overview
Malaria is an acute febrile illness caused by *Plasmodium* parasites, which are spread to humans through the bites of infected female *Anopheles* mosquitoes (known as "malaria vectors"). There are five parasite species that cause malaria in humans: *P. falciparum*, *P. vivax*, *P. malariae*, *P. ovale*, and *P. knowlesi*. Of these, *P. falciparum* and *P. vivax* pose the greatest threat.

The WHO African Region carries a disproportionately high burden of malaria. In 2022, the region was home to about 94% of malaria cases and 95% of malaria deaths. Malaria remains a major cause of childhood illness and death in sub-Saharan Africa, despite significant progress in reducing incidence and mortality over the past two decades.

## Symptoms
Symptoms typically appear 10–15 days after the infective mosquito bite. Initial symptoms are often mild and may include:

- Fever, headache, and chills (classic malaria triad)
- Sweating, fatigue, and general malaise
- Nausea, vomiting, and body aches
- Muscle pain and diarrhea (in some cases)

If not treated within 24 hours, *P. falciparum* malaria can progress to severe illness, including: severe anemia, respiratory distress (metabolic acidosis), cerebral malaria (seizures, coma), multi-organ failure, and death. In *P. vivax* and *P. ovale* infections, dormant liver-stage parasites (hypnozoites) can reactivate weeks to months later, causing relapses.

## Causes / Risk Factors
Malaria is caused by *Plasmodium* parasites transmitted via the bite of infected female *Anopheles* mosquitoes, which bite predominantly between dusk and dawn. Transmission can also occur through blood transfusion, organ transplantation, shared needles, or from mother to child (congenital malaria).

Risk factors include living in or traveling to endemic areas (sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, South America), young age (under 5), pregnancy, HIV/AIDS, lack of immunity (travelers from non-endemic areas), and limited access to preventive measures.

## Diagnosis
- **Rapid Diagnostic Tests (RDTs):** Detect malaria antigens in blood; provide results in 15–30 minutes. Widely used in resource-limited settings.
- **Microscopy:** Examination of thick and thin blood smears under a microscope; remains the gold standard. Identifies parasite species and quantifies parasitemia.
- **Molecular tests (PCR):** High sensitivity for detecting low-level parasitemia; used in research and reference laboratories.

Early and accurate diagnosis is essential for effective disease management and malaria surveillance.

## Treatment
Malaria is treated with antimalarial medications. The choice of drug depends on the species of parasite and the severity of disease:

- **Uncomplicated P. falciparum malaria:** Artemisinin-based Combination Therapy (ACT) is the recommended first-line treatment (e.g., artemether-lumefantrine, artesunate-amodiaquine).
- **Severe malaria:** Intravenous or intramuscular artesunate is the treatment of choice, followed by a full course of ACT once the patient can take oral medication.
- **P. vivax / P. ovale:** Chloroquine (where still effective) or ACT, plus a 14-day course of primaquine to eliminate hypnozoites and prevent relapse (after testing for G6PD deficiency).
- **Supportive care:** Blood transfusion for severe anemia, antipyretics for fever, and management of complications.

Drug resistance is a recurring challenge; WHO monitors antimalarial drug efficacy globally.

## Prevention
- **Insecticide-treated mosquito nets (ITNs):** Sleeping under ITNs provides a physical and chemical barrier against mosquito bites. This is the most widely used and effective prevention method.
- **Indoor residual spraying (IRS):** Spraying the interior walls of dwellings with insecticide kills mosquitoes that rest on sprayed surfaces.
- **Chemoprevention:** Seasonal malaria chemoprevention (SMC) for children in high-transmission areas; intermittent preventive treatment in pregnancy (IPTp).
- **Vaccination:** The RTS,S/AS01 (Mosquirix) and R21/Matrix-M vaccines are recommended by WHO for children in areas of moderate to high *P. falciparum* transmission.
- **Personal protection:** Use of mosquito repellents, wearing long-sleeved clothing during peak biting hours.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek medical attention immediately if you develop a fever during or after traveling to a malaria-endemic area. Symptoms such as high fever, chills, severe headache, confusion, difficulty breathing, persistent vomiting, or dark-colored urine require urgent evaluation. Delay in treatment of *P. falciparum* malaria can lead to rapid deterioration and death within hours.
