# Dengue

## Key Facts
- Dengue is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the dengue virus (DENV), transmitted primarily by *Aedes aegypti* mosquitoes.
- An estimated 100–400 million infections occur each year, with about 3.9 billion people at risk in over 129 countries.
- Most dengue infections are asymptomatic or produce mild illness; however, severe dengue can be life-threatening.
- The global incidence of dengue has grown dramatically in recent decades, with cases reported to WHO increasing from 505,430 in 2000 to over 5 million in 2023.
- There is no specific antiviral treatment for dengue; early detection and appropriate medical care reduce fatality rates below 1%.
- A dengue vaccine (Dengvaxia) is available but recommended only for individuals with confirmed prior infection.

## Overview
Dengue is the most common and rapidly spreading mosquito-borne viral disease in the world. It is caused by four closely related serotypes of the dengue virus (DENV-1 through DENV-4), belonging to the genus *Flavivirus*, family *Flaviviridae*. Infection with one serotype provides lifelong immunity to that serotype but only short-term cross-immunity to the others. Sequential infection with a different serotype increases the risk of severe dengue.

Dengue is found in tropical and subtropical climates worldwide, mostly in urban and semi-urban areas. The disease has expanded its geographic range significantly due to urbanization, international travel, and climate change.

## Symptoms
Dengue infection may be asymptomatic or may cause a wide spectrum of clinical illness:

**Dengue Fever (mild):** High fever (40°C / 104°F) accompanied by at least two of the following: severe headache, retro-orbital pain (pain behind the eyes), muscle and joint pain, nausea, vomiting, swollen glands, and rash. Symptoms typically last 2–7 days after an incubation period of 4–10 days.

**Severe Dengue (dengue hemorrhagic fever):** A potentially fatal complication that develops 3–7 days after initial symptom onset. Warning signs include severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, rapid breathing, bleeding gums or nose, blood in vomit or stool, fatigue, restlessness, and fluid accumulation. Severe dengue can cause plasma leakage, severe bleeding, and organ failure.

## Causes / Risk Factors
Dengue virus is transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female *Aedes* mosquitoes, primarily *Aedes aegypti* and to a lesser extent *Aedes albopictus*. These mosquitoes are day-biting and breed in stagnant water in containers around human dwellings.

Risk factors include living in or traveling to tropical/subtropical regions, prior dengue infection (increases severe dengue risk), and lack of vector control measures. Young children and individuals with a second dengue infection are at higher risk for severe disease.

## Diagnosis
- **NS1 antigen test:** Detects dengue virus antigen in blood during the first 5 days of illness (high sensitivity in early phase).
- **Serological tests (ELISA):** Detect IgM (acute infection, positive from day 5) and IgG (past infection or secondary infection) antibodies.
- **RT-PCR:** Molecular detection of viral RNA; most specific but requires laboratory equipment.
- **Complete blood count:** Thrombocytopenia (low platelet count) and rising hematocrit are warning indicators.

## Treatment
There is no specific antiviral medication for dengue. Treatment is supportive:

- **Fluid management:** Adequate hydration (oral rehydration salts or intravenous fluids in severe cases) is the cornerstone of dengue treatment.
- **Fever management:** Paracetamol (acetaminophen) for fever and pain relief. Aspirin and NSAIDs (ibuprofen) must be avoided as they increase bleeding risk.
- **Monitoring:** Patients with warning signs require close monitoring, ideally in a healthcare facility, for signs of plasma leakage and shock.
- **Severe dengue:** Requires hospitalization with aggressive intravenous fluid therapy and close monitoring of vital signs and hematocrit.

## Prevention
- **Vector control:** Eliminate mosquito breeding sites by emptying, cleaning, or covering water storage containers. Use insecticide-treated bed nets, window screens, and indoor residual spraying.
- **Personal protection:** Wear long-sleeved clothing, use mosquito repellents containing DEET or icaridin, and use mosquito coils or vaporizers.
- **Community engagement:** Raise awareness about dengue prevention, especially during rainy seasons.
- **Vaccination:** Dengvaxia (CYD-TDV) is licensed in some countries for individuals aged 9–45 years with confirmed prior dengue infection. It is not recommended for dengue-naive individuals.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek immediate medical care if any of the following warning signs appear (typically around days 3–7 of illness): severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, rapid breathing, bleeding gums or nose, blood in vomit or stool, extreme fatigue or restlessness, pale or cold skin. These may indicate progression to severe dengue, which requires emergency treatment.
