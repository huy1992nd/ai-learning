# HIV/AIDS

## Key Facts
- Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) attacks the immune system and, if untreated, leads to Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS).
- An estimated 39.0 million people were living with HIV globally at the end of 2022, with 1.3 million new infections that year.
- HIV caused approximately 630,000 deaths in 2022; a significant decline from 2.0 million in 2004.
- Antiretroviral therapy (ART) does not cure HIV but suppresses viral replication, allowing people with HIV to live long and healthy lives.
- As of the end of 2022, 29.8 million people living with HIV were accessing ART globally.
- There is no vaccine for HIV, but effective prevention tools include condoms, pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP), and harm reduction for people who inject drugs.

## Overview
HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a retrovirus that infects cells of the immune system, primarily CD4+ T lymphocytes. The virus progressively destroys immune function, leaving the body vulnerable to opportunistic infections and cancers. Without treatment, HIV infection advances through three stages: acute HIV infection, chronic HIV infection (clinical latency), and AIDS.

AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome) is the most advanced stage of HIV infection, defined by a CD4 cell count below 200 cells/mm³ or the development of certain opportunistic infections or cancers. With early diagnosis and lifelong ART, progression to AIDS can be prevented, and people with HIV can achieve an undetectable viral load, meaning they cannot transmit the virus sexually (Undetectable = Untransmittable, or U=U).

## Symptoms
**Acute HIV infection (2–4 weeks after exposure):** Many people develop flu-like symptoms including fever, headache, rash, sore throat, swollen lymph nodes, muscle and joint pain, fatigue, and mouth ulcers. This stage is highly infectious.

**Chronic HIV infection (clinical latency):** May last several years with few or no symptoms. The virus continues to replicate at low levels. Without ART, this stage progresses to AIDS typically within 10 years.

**AIDS:** Rapid weight loss, recurring fever, profuse night sweats, extreme fatigue, prolonged swelling of lymph glands, chronic diarrhea, pneumonia, memory loss, and skin blotches. Opportunistic infections include *Pneumocystis* pneumonia, tuberculosis, cryptococcal meningitis, and Kaposi's sarcoma.

## Causes / Risk Factors
HIV is transmitted through contact with certain body fluids from a person with detectable HIV viral load: blood, semen, pre-seminal fluid, rectal fluids, vaginal fluids, and breast milk.

Common routes of transmission include: unprotected sexual intercourse (vaginal or anal), sharing needles or syringes, mother-to-child transmission during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding, and occupational exposure (needlestick injuries).

Risk factors include: unprotected sex with multiple partners, presence of other sexually transmitted infections (which facilitate transmission), injection drug use, and lack of access to prevention services.

## Diagnosis
- **Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs):** Detect HIV antibodies in blood or oral fluid; provide same-day results. WHO recommends RDTs as the primary testing strategy.
- **Laboratory-based immunoassays:** Fourth-generation assays detect both HIV antibodies and p24 antigen, enabling earlier detection (as early as 2 weeks post-exposure).
- **HIV self-testing:** Allows individuals to test themselves in private using oral fluid or blood-based kits.
- **Nucleic acid testing (NAT):** Detects viral RNA; used for early infant diagnosis and confirmatory testing.
- WHO recommends a testing strategy using sequential reactive tests (at least two different assays) before confirming HIV-positive status.

## Treatment
HIV is treated with antiretroviral therapy (ART), which involves taking a combination of HIV medicines daily:

- **First-line ART:** WHO recommends dolutegravir-based regimens (e.g., TLD: tenofovir + lamivudine + dolutegravir) for most adults and adolescents.
- **Treatment initiation:** WHO recommends starting ART as soon as possible after diagnosis, regardless of CD4 count ("treat all" approach).
- **Viral suppression:** The goal of ART is to achieve and maintain an undetectable viral load (below 50 copies/mL), which preserves immune function and prevents transmission.
- **Adherence:** Lifelong adherence to ART is critical. Missed doses can lead to drug resistance and treatment failure.
- **Monitoring:** Regular viral load testing (at least annually) is the preferred approach for monitoring treatment response.

## Prevention
- **Condoms:** Consistent and correct use of male or female condoms during sexual intercourse.
- **Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP):** Daily oral PrEP or long-acting injectable cabotegravir for HIV-negative individuals at substantial risk.
- **Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP):** ART started within 72 hours of potential exposure, taken for 28 days.
- **Harm reduction:** Needle and syringe programs, opioid substitution therapy for people who inject drugs.
- **Prevention of mother-to-child transmission (PMTCT):** ART for HIV-positive pregnant and breastfeeding women effectively reduces transmission to below 5%.
- **Voluntary medical male circumcision (VMMC):** Reduces the risk of female-to-male sexual transmission by approximately 60%.
- **Testing and treatment:** Early HIV diagnosis and ART initiation to achieve viral suppression (U=U).

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Get tested for HIV if you have had unprotected sexual contact, shared injection equipment, or been exposed to blood or body fluids from a person whose HIV status is unknown. Seek immediate medical attention if you experience symptoms of acute HIV infection (flu-like illness 2–4 weeks after potential exposure) or if you need post-exposure prophylaxis (within 72 hours of exposure). All pregnant women should be tested for HIV.
