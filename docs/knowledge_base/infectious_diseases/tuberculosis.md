# Tuberculosis (TB)

## Key Facts
- Tuberculosis (TB) is caused by the bacterium *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* and most commonly affects the lungs (pulmonary TB).
- TB is the world's second leading infectious killer after COVID-19; in 2022, 1.3 million people died from TB globally.
- An estimated 10.6 million people fell ill with TB in 2022, including 5.8 million men, 3.5 million women, and 1.3 million children.
- About one-quarter of the global population is estimated to have been infected with TB bacteria (latent TB infection).
- TB is preventable and curable; about 85% of people who develop TB disease can be successfully treated with a 4–6 month drug regimen.
- Multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) remains a public health crisis, with about 410,000 estimated cases in 2022.

## Overview
Tuberculosis is a communicable disease caused by the bacillus *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*. It typically affects the lungs (pulmonary TB) but can affect other organs (extrapulmonary TB), including lymph nodes, bones, kidneys, and the central nervous system. TB spreads from person to person through the air when people with pulmonary TB cough, sneeze, or spit.

A person needs to inhale only a small number of bacilli to become infected. Not everyone infected with TB bacteria becomes sick. Latent TB infection (LTBI) means the bacteria are present but dormant, and the person is not infectious. About 5–10% of people with LTBI will progress to active TB disease at some point in their lives, with the risk being much higher among immunocompromised individuals.

## Symptoms
**Pulmonary TB (active disease):**
- Persistent cough lasting more than 2–3 weeks (often productive, may include blood-tinged sputum)
- Chest pain and difficulty breathing
- Unexplained weight loss and loss of appetite
- Fever (often low-grade), night sweats, and chills
- Fatigue and general malaise

**Extrapulmonary TB:** Symptoms depend on the organ affected. Lymph node TB presents as painless swelling; skeletal TB causes bone pain; meningeal TB causes headache, confusion, and neck stiffness.

**Latent TB infection:** No symptoms and not infectious. Detected by tuberculin skin test (TST) or interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA).

## Causes / Risk Factors
TB is caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* and transmitted through airborne droplet nuclei generated when an infectious person coughs or sneezes. Prolonged close contact with an infectious person increases transmission risk.

Risk factors for developing active TB include: HIV co-infection (the strongest risk factor), diabetes, malnutrition, tobacco smoking, alcohol use disorder, silicosis, immunosuppressive therapy, age (very young and very old), and living in crowded or poorly ventilated conditions.

## Diagnosis
- **Sputum smear microscopy:** Rapid and inexpensive; detects acid-fast bacilli (AFB) but has limited sensitivity.
- **GeneXpert MTB/RIF (Xpert):** Molecular test recommended by WHO as the initial diagnostic test; detects TB and rifampicin resistance within 2 hours.
- **Culture:** Gold standard for TB diagnosis; allows drug susceptibility testing but takes 2–8 weeks.
- **Chest X-ray:** Used for screening and assessing disease extent in pulmonary TB.
- **Tuberculin skin test (TST) / IGRA:** Used to detect latent TB infection; cannot distinguish latent from active TB.

## Treatment
TB is treated with a standardized regimen of antimicrobial drugs:

- **Drug-susceptible TB:** Standard first-line treatment is a 6-month regimen: 2 months of isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol (intensive phase), followed by 4 months of isoniazid and rifampicin (continuation phase).
- **MDR-TB / RR-TB:** Requires longer treatment (9–18 months) with second-line drugs, including bedaquiline, pretomanid, and linezolid (BPaL regimen recommended by WHO).
- **Latent TB treatment:** Shorter preventive regimens include 3 months of weekly isoniazid-rifapentine (3HP) or 4 months of daily rifampicin.
- **Directly Observed Therapy (DOT):** WHO recommends treatment support and patient-centered approaches to ensure adherence and completion.

## Prevention
- **BCG vaccination:** The Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine is given to infants in countries with high TB burden; it provides protection against severe childhood TB (miliary TB, TB meningitis) but has variable efficacy against pulmonary TB in adults.
- **Infection control:** Adequate ventilation, respiratory hygiene, and use of masks in healthcare settings and congregate environments.
- **Latent TB treatment:** Treating LTBI in high-risk groups (HIV-positive individuals, close contacts of TB patients, immunosuppressed persons) to prevent progression to active disease.
- **Active case finding:** Early detection and treatment of infectious cases to break the chain of transmission.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if you experience a persistent cough lasting more than 2 weeks, especially if accompanied by blood in sputum, unexplained weight loss, fever, or night sweats. Seek immediate evaluation if you have been in close contact with someone diagnosed with TB or if you are HIV-positive and develop respiratory symptoms.
