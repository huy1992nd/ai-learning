# Hepatitis B

## Key Facts
- Hepatitis B is a viral infection caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV) that attacks the liver and can cause both acute and chronic disease.
- An estimated 254 million people were living with chronic hepatitis B infection in 2022.
- Hepatitis B caused an estimated 1.1 million deaths in 2022, mostly from cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma (liver cancer).
- Hepatitis B is transmitted through contact with blood and other body fluids of an infected person, particularly from mother to child at birth.
- A safe and effective vaccine is available and provides 98–100% protection against hepatitis B.
- Chronic hepatitis B can be treated with antiviral agents (e.g., tenofovir, entecavir), reducing the risk of liver cancer and improving long-term survival.

## Overview
Hepatitis B is an infection of the liver caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV), a small DNA virus belonging to the *Hepadnaviridae* family. The infection can be acute (short-term, resolving within 6 months) or chronic (long-term, persisting beyond 6 months). Chronic hepatitis B is a major global health problem due to its potential to cause liver cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma.

The risk of developing chronic infection depends on the age at which a person becomes infected: approximately 80–90% of infants infected during the first year of life develop chronic infection, compared to less than 5% of adults. The WHO African and Western Pacific regions account for the majority of the global burden.

## Symptoms
**Acute hepatitis B:** Most newly infected adults are asymptomatic. When symptoms occur (typically 1–4 months after infection), they may include: jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes), dark urine, extreme fatigue, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (especially in the upper right quadrant), and joint pain. Acute hepatitis B is rarely fatal, but approximately 1% of cases may develop fulminant hepatitis.

**Chronic hepatitis B:** Most chronically infected people are asymptomatic for decades until complications develop. Symptoms of advanced liver disease include fatigue, jaundice, ascites (abdominal swelling), spider angiomata, and hepatic encephalopathy.

## Causes / Risk Factors
HBV is transmitted through percutaneous or mucosal exposure to infected blood or body fluids. The most common transmission routes are:

- **Mother-to-child transmission (MTCT):** The most significant route globally, occurring at birth.
- **Horizontal transmission:** Contact with infected blood through unsafe injection practices, needlestick injuries, tattooing, or sharing razors.
- **Sexual transmission:** Unprotected sexual contact with an infected person.

Risk factors include: birth to an HBV-positive mother, healthcare workers with occupational exposure, people who inject drugs, household contacts of infected persons, and individuals with multiple sexual partners.

## Diagnosis
- **Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg):** The hallmark of HBV infection. Persistence beyond 6 months defines chronic infection.
- **Anti-HBs antibody:** Indicates immunity (from vaccination or resolved infection).
- **HBV DNA (viral load):** Quantifies viral replication; guides treatment decisions.
- **Hepatitis B e antigen (HBeAg):** Marker of high viral replication and infectivity.
- **Liver function tests (ALT/AST):** Elevated levels indicate liver inflammation.
- **FibroScan or liver biopsy:** Assesses degree of liver fibrosis.

## Treatment
Not all people with chronic hepatitis B require treatment. Treatment is indicated for those with significant liver disease (fibrosis/cirrhosis) or high viral load with liver inflammation:

- **First-line antivirals:** Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF), tenofovir alafenamide (TAF), or entecavir (ETV) — taken orally, once daily. These are well-tolerated with a high barrier to resistance.
- **Treatment duration:** Usually lifelong, as antivirals suppress but rarely eliminate the virus (functional cure is rare).
- **Pegylated interferon-alpha:** An alternative finite-duration treatment (48 weeks) for selected patients, but with more side effects.
- **Monitoring:** Regular viral load and ALT monitoring; liver cancer surveillance with ultrasound every 6 months for at-risk individuals.

## Prevention
- **Vaccination:** The hepatitis B vaccine is the mainstay of prevention. WHO recommends that all infants receive the first dose within 24 hours of birth (birth dose), followed by 2–3 additional doses. The vaccine provides long-lasting protection (20+ years, possibly lifelong).
- **Prevention of mother-to-child transmission:** Hepatitis B birth-dose vaccination, plus antiviral prophylaxis (tenofovir) for pregnant women with high viral load.
- **Blood safety:** Screening all donated blood for HBV.
- **Injection safety:** Use of sterile needles and syringes; safe injection practices in healthcare settings.
- **Harm reduction:** Needle and syringe programs for people who inject drugs.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek medical evaluation if you experience jaundice, persistent fatigue, dark urine, or abdominal pain. Get tested for hepatitis B if you were born in a high-prevalence region, have a household or sexual contact with an HBV-infected person, are a healthcare worker, inject drugs, or are pregnant. Early detection of chronic hepatitis B enables monitoring and timely treatment to prevent liver damage.
