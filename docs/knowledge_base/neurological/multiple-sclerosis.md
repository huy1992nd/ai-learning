# Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

## Key Facts
- Multiple sclerosis is a chronic autoimmune, inflammatory, and neurodegenerative disease of the central nervous system (CNS).
- An estimated 2.8 million people worldwide were living with MS in 2020.
- MS is the most common non-traumatic disabling neurological disease in young adults, with typical onset between ages 20 and 40.
- It is 2–3 times more common in women than men.
- MS is characterized by demyelination and axonal damage in the brain and spinal cord, leading to diverse neurological symptoms.
- Disease-modifying therapies (DMTs) can significantly reduce relapse rates and slow disability progression.

## Overview
Multiple sclerosis is an immune-mediated disease in which the body's immune system attacks the myelin sheath — the protective covering of nerve fibers in the brain and spinal cord. This demyelination disrupts the transmission of nerve signals, leading to a wide range of neurological symptoms. Over time, the disease can also cause axonal damage and neurodegeneration.

MS is classified into several clinical phenotypes:
- **Relapsing-remitting MS (RRMS):** Most common form (~85% at onset); characterized by clearly defined relapses (new or worsening symptoms) followed by partial or complete remissions.
- **Secondary progressive MS (SPMS):** Follows RRMS in many patients; characterized by gradual worsening of disability with or without superimposed relapses.
- **Primary progressive MS (PPMS):** ~10–15% of patients; gradual accumulation of disability from onset without distinct relapses.

## Symptoms
MS symptoms are highly variable depending on the location of CNS lesions:

- **Visual:** Optic neuritis (painful vision loss, usually unilateral — often an early symptom), diplopia (double vision), nystagmus
- **Motor:** Weakness, spasticity, gait difficulties, incoordination (ataxia)
- **Sensory:** Numbness, tingling, burning sensations, Lhermitte's sign (electric shock sensation down the spine on neck flexion)
- **Bladder and bowel:** Urgency, frequency, incontinence, constipation
- **Cognitive:** Difficulty with concentration, memory, information processing speed (affects 40–70% of patients)
- **Fatigue:** The most common MS symptom, often the most disabling; not proportional to physical activity
- **Pain:** Neuropathic pain, trigeminal neuralgia, musculoskeletal pain
- **Mood:** Depression, anxiety (common comorbidities)
- **Other:** Sexual dysfunction, heat sensitivity (Uhthoff's phenomenon), vertigo, speech difficulties

## Causes / Risk Factors
The exact cause of MS is unknown but involves a complex interplay of genetic and environmental factors:

- **Immune dysregulation:** Autoreactive T cells and B cells attack CNS myelin, triggering inflammation and demyelination.
- **Genetics:** Not directly inherited, but genetic susceptibility exists. HLA-DRB1*15:01 allele is the strongest genetic risk factor. Having a first-degree relative with MS increases risk to 2–5%.
- **Vitamin D deficiency / limited sun exposure:** Strong epidemiological association; higher MS prevalence at higher latitudes.
- **Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infection:** Nearly all MS patients have evidence of past EBV infection; recent evidence strongly suggests EBV as a necessary (but not sufficient) cause of MS.
- **Smoking:** Increases risk of MS by 50% and accelerates disease progression.
- **Obesity in adolescence:** Associated with increased MS risk.
- **Geography:** Prevalence increases with distance from the equator (latitude gradient).

## Diagnosis
MS diagnosis follows the McDonald Criteria (revised 2017), requiring demonstration of dissemination in space (DIS) and dissemination in time (DIT):

- **Brain and spinal cord MRI:** The most important diagnostic tool. Reveals T2-hyperintense demyelinating lesions in characteristic locations (periventricular, cortical/juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord). Gadolinium-enhancing lesions indicate active inflammation. DIS requires ≥1 T2 lesion in ≥2 characteristic areas; DIT requires simultaneous presence of enhancing and non-enhancing lesions OR a new T2/enhancing lesion on follow-up MRI.
- **Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis:** Oligoclonal bands (present in >95% of MS patients but not in serum) and elevated IgG index support the diagnosis. CSF can substitute for DIT.
- **Evoked potentials:** Visual evoked potentials (VEP) can detect subclinical optic nerve demyelination.
- **Blood tests:** To exclude MS mimics (neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder — AQP4 and MOG antibodies, sarcoidosis, SLE, vitamin B12 deficiency, infections).

## Treatment
**Acute relapse management:**
- High-dose intravenous methylprednisolone (1 g/day for 3–5 days) to reduce inflammation and hasten recovery.
- Plasma exchange (plasmapheresis) for severe relapses not responding to corticosteroids.

**Disease-modifying therapies (DMTs) — to reduce relapses and slow disability progression:**
- **Moderate efficacy:** Interferon beta (1a, 1b), glatiramer acetate, teriflunomide, dimethyl fumarate
- **High efficacy:** Natalizumab (anti-integrin), ocrelizumab (anti-CD20 — also approved for PPMS), ofatumumab, fingolimod, siponimod (for SPMS), cladribine, alemtuzumab

Treatment strategy increasingly favors early initiation of high-efficacy therapy to prevent irreversible neurological damage.

**Symptom management:**
- Fatigue: Exercise, amantadine, modafinil
- Spasticity: Physiotherapy, baclofen, tizanidine, nabiximols (cannabis-based spray)
- Neuropathic pain: Gabapentin, pregabalin, duloxetine
- Bladder dysfunction: Anticholinergics, intermittent self-catheterization
- Depression: SSRIs, cognitive behavioral therapy
- Cognitive rehabilitation: Structured programs and compensatory strategies

**Rehabilitation:** Physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy are integral to comprehensive MS care.

## Prevention
- There is no known way to prevent MS.
- Emerging evidence suggests that maintaining adequate vitamin D levels, avoiding smoking, maintaining a healthy weight during adolescence, and early treatment of EBV infection (vaccine research ongoing) may reduce risk.
- For patients with MS: adherence to DMTs, smoking cessation, regular exercise, and vitamin D supplementation are recommended to optimize outcomes and potentially slow disease progression.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a neurologist if you experience episodes of unexplained vision loss, numbness or weakness (especially on one side of the body), prolonged fatigue, difficulty walking or balance problems, or electric shock sensations in the spine. Seek urgent evaluation for sudden severe vision loss, acute limb weakness, or urinary retention. If you have MS, report new or worsening neurological symptoms to your neurologist promptly, as early relapse treatment improves outcomes.
