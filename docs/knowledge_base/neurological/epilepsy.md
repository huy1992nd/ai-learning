# Epilepsy

## Key Facts
- Epilepsy is a chronic neurological disorder characterized by recurrent, unprovoked seizures resulting from excessive electrical activity in the brain.
- An estimated 50 million people worldwide have epilepsy, making it one of the most common neurological diseases globally.
- Nearly 80% of people with epilepsy live in low- and middle-income countries.
- Up to 70% of people with epilepsy could become seizure-free with appropriate antiseizure medication.
- About 25% of epilepsy cases are potentially preventable (head injury, perinatal complications, CNS infections).
- Epilepsy is associated with significant stigma and discrimination, particularly in resource-limited settings.

## Overview
Epilepsy is a brain disorder characterized by an enduring predisposition to generate epileptic seizures and by the neurobiological, cognitive, psychological, and social consequences of this condition. A diagnosis of epilepsy requires at least two unprovoked (or reflex) seizures occurring more than 24 hours apart, OR one unprovoked seizure with a high probability of further seizures (≥60% recurrence risk over the next 10 years).

Seizures result from sudden, abnormal, excessive electrical discharges in a group of brain neurons. The clinical manifestation depends on where in the brain the discharge occurs, how it spreads, how much of the brain is affected, and how long the discharge lasts. Epilepsy can occur at any age but onset is most common in childhood and in older adults.

## Symptoms
**Seizure types:**

**Focal (partial) seizures — originating in one brain region:**
- Focal aware seizures: consciousness preserved; may involve motor (jerking of one limb), sensory (tingling, visual disturbances), or autonomic symptoms
- Focal impaired awareness seizures: altered consciousness; may involve automatisms (lip-smacking, hand movements, wandering)
- Focal to bilateral tonic-clonic seizures: begins focally then generalizes

**Generalized seizures — involving both brain hemispheres from onset:**
- Tonic-clonic (grand mal): Loss of consciousness, body stiffening (tonic phase), followed by rhythmic jerking (clonic phase), postictal confusion
- Absence (petit mal): Brief (5–30 seconds) episodes of staring, unresponsiveness; common in children
- Myoclonic: Brief, shock-like jerks of a muscle or group of muscles
- Atonic (drop attacks): Sudden loss of muscle tone causing falls

**Postictal state:** Following a seizure, patients may experience confusion, drowsiness, headache, nausea, and temporary weakness (Todd's paralysis).

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Structural causes:** Brain tumors, traumatic brain injury, cerebrovascular disease (stroke), cortical malformations, hippocampal sclerosis.

**Genetic causes:** Inherited channelopathies and genetic epilepsy syndromes (e.g., Dravet syndrome, juvenile myoclonic epilepsy).

**Infectious causes:** Neurocysticercosis (the most common preventable cause of epilepsy globally), meningitis, encephalitis, cerebral malaria, HIV-related CNS infections.

**Metabolic causes:** Electrolyte imbalances, inborn errors of metabolism, mitochondrial disorders.

**Perinatal causes:** Birth asphyxia, neonatal hypoglycemia, intracranial hemorrhage.

**Risk factors:** Family history of epilepsy, head trauma, CNS infections, cerebrovascular disease, neurodegenerative diseases (Alzheimer's), premature birth.

## Diagnosis
- **Clinical history:** Detailed description of the seizure (from patient and witnesses) — the most important diagnostic tool. Includes semiology, duration, frequency, triggers, postictal features.
- **Electroencephalography (EEG):** Records brain electrical activity; may reveal epileptiform discharges (spikes, sharp waves). A normal interictal EEG does not exclude epilepsy. Prolonged or video-EEG monitoring increases diagnostic yield.
- **Brain MRI:** High-resolution MRI is the imaging modality of choice to identify structural lesions (hippocampal sclerosis, tumors, cortical dysplasia, vascular malformations).
- **Blood tests:** Glucose, electrolytes, calcium, magnesium, complete blood count, liver/renal function — to exclude metabolic causes and as baseline before starting medication.
- **Neuropsychological testing:** Assesses cognitive function and helps localize epileptogenic zone in surgical candidates.

## Treatment
**Antiseizure medications (ASMs) — mainstay of treatment:**
- First-line broad-spectrum: Levetiracetam, lamotrigine, valproic acid (avoid in women of childbearing potential due to teratogenicity)
- Focal seizures: Carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, lacosamide
- Absence seizures: Ethosuximide, valproic acid, lamotrigine
- Principles: Start monotherapy at low dose, titrate slowly, aim for seizure freedom with minimal side effects. If first ASM fails, try a second monotherapy before considering combination therapy.

**Surgical treatment:**
- For drug-resistant epilepsy (failure of ≥2 appropriate ASMs at adequate doses)
- Resective surgery (temporal lobectomy) achieves seizure freedom in 60–80% of selected patients
- Requires comprehensive presurgical evaluation (video-EEG, MRI, neuropsychological testing)

**Other therapies:**
- Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS): Implanted device for drug-resistant epilepsy not suitable for surgery
- Ketogenic diet: High-fat, low-carbohydrate diet; effective in some drug-resistant childhood epilepsies
- Responsive neurostimulation (RNS) and deep brain stimulation (DBS)

**Seizure first aid:** Keep the person safe; cushion the head; do NOT put anything in the mouth; do NOT restrain; turn on their side (recovery position); time the seizure; call emergency services if the seizure lasts >5 minutes or if the person does not regain consciousness.

## Prevention
- Prevent head injuries (helmets, seatbelts, fall prevention)
- Adequate perinatal care to reduce birth-related brain injuries
- Prevention and treatment of CNS infections (neurocysticercosis prevention through improved sanitation; vaccination for meningitis)
- Stroke prevention (blood pressure control, healthy lifestyle)
- Avoid known seizure triggers (sleep deprivation, excessive alcohol, flashing lights for photosensitive epilepsy)
- Medication adherence: do not stop ASMs abruptly

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek medical evaluation after a first seizure. Call emergency services if a seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes (status epilepticus — a medical emergency), if the person does not regain consciousness, if a second seizure occurs shortly after the first, if the person is injured during the seizure, if the person has difficulty breathing after the seizure, or if the seizure occurs in water. Consult a neurologist if seizures are not well-controlled despite medication.
