# Parkinson's Disease

## Key Facts
- Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder primarily affecting the dopaminergic neurons of the substantia nigra in the brain.
- An estimated 8.5 million people were living with Parkinson's disease globally in 2019, with prevalence more than doubling over the past 25 years.
- PD caused approximately 329,000 deaths in 2019, an increase of over 100% since 2000.
- The cardinal motor features are bradykinesia, resting tremor, rigidity, and postural instability.
- Non-motor symptoms (depression, cognitive decline, sleep disturbances, autonomic dysfunction) are common and significantly affect quality of life.
- There is no cure, but symptomatic treatment with levodopa and other dopaminergic medications can provide significant benefit for many years.

## Overview
Parkinson's disease is the second most common neurodegenerative disorder after Alzheimer's disease. It is caused by the progressive loss of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta of the midbrain. The resulting dopamine deficiency in the basal ganglia leads to the characteristic motor symptoms. Additionally, the presence of Lewy bodies (intracellular aggregates of alpha-synuclein protein) in the brain is the pathological hallmark of PD.

The disease typically begins insidiously, often with unilateral symptoms, and progresses over years. While motor symptoms are the most recognizable features, PD is now understood to be a multisystem disorder with prominent non-motor manifestations that may precede motor symptoms by years or decades.

## Symptoms
**Motor symptoms (cardinal features):**
- **Bradykinesia:** Slowness of movement; reduced amplitude and speed of repetitive movements; difficulty initiating movement
- **Resting tremor:** "Pill-rolling" tremor of the hand at rest (4–6 Hz); typically begins unilaterally
- **Rigidity:** Increased resistance to passive movement; "cogwheel" rigidity (ratchet-like resistance)
- **Postural instability:** Impaired balance and coordination; increased fall risk (usually develops in later stages)

Other motor features: Shuffling gait (festination), reduced arm swing, micrographia (small handwriting), hypomimia (reduced facial expression — "masked face"), dysarthria (soft, monotonous speech), dysphagia.

**Non-motor symptoms:**
- **Neuropsychiatric:** Depression (affects 40–50%), anxiety, apathy, cognitive impairment, dementia (in later stages), hallucinations
- **Autonomic:** Constipation, urinary dysfunction, orthostatic hypotension, excessive sweating
- **Sleep:** REM sleep behavior disorder (may precede diagnosis by years), insomnia, excessive daytime sleepiness
- **Sensory:** Anosmia (loss of smell — often the earliest symptom), pain
- **Other:** Fatigue, seborrheic dermatitis

## Causes / Risk Factors
The exact cause of PD is unknown in most cases. It likely results from a combination of genetic susceptibility and environmental exposures:

- **Age:** The strongest risk factor; prevalence increases sharply after age 60
- **Genetics:** Approximately 10–15% of cases are familial. Known genetic mutations include LRRK2, GBA, SNCA, PINK1, Parkin, DJ-1. GBA mutations are the most common genetic risk factor
- **Environmental factors:** Pesticide exposure (rotenone, paraquat), rural living, well-water consumption, and head trauma have been associated with increased risk
- **Protective factors:** Caffeine consumption, smoking (epidemiological association only — NOT a recommendation), and physical exercise are associated with reduced risk

## Diagnosis
PD is primarily a clinical diagnosis:

- **Movement Disorder Society (MDS) Clinical Diagnostic Criteria:** Requires bradykinesia PLUS resting tremor and/or rigidity. Supportive criteria include unilateral onset, progressive course, clear beneficial response to dopaminergic therapy, and presence of levodopa-induced dyskinesia.
- **DaTscan (dopamine transporter imaging):** SPECT imaging that shows reduced dopamine transporter uptake in the striatum; supports diagnosis when clinical features are uncertain.
- **Brain MRI:** Usually normal in PD but used to exclude structural causes (vascular parkinsonism, normal pressure hydrocephalus, tumors).
- **Levodopa challenge test:** A significant motor improvement after levodopa administration supports the diagnosis.
- **Neuropsychological assessment:** Evaluates cognitive function; important for monitoring and management.

## Treatment
**Pharmacological therapy:**
- **Levodopa (+ carbidopa or benserazide):** The most effective symptomatic treatment; gold standard for motor symptoms. Long-term use may lead to motor fluctuations (wearing-off) and dyskinesia.
- **Dopamine agonists:** Pramipexole, ropinirole, rotigotine (patch) — may delay levodopa-related complications; used as initial therapy in younger patients.
- **MAO-B inhibitors:** Rasagiline, selegiline, safinamide — mild symptomatic benefit; may be used as monotherapy in early disease or as adjunct.
- **COMT inhibitors:** Entacapone, opicapone — used with levodopa to extend its effect and reduce "off" time.
- **Amantadine:** For levodopa-induced dyskinesia.
- **Anticholinergics:** Trihexyphenidyl — limited role; may help tremor in younger patients but has cognitive side effects.

**Surgical therapy:**
- **Deep brain stimulation (DBS):** Implanted electrodes in the subthalamic nucleus or globus pallidus interna; effective for motor fluctuations and tremor refractory to medical therapy.

**Supportive therapies:**
- Physiotherapy (gait training, balance exercises, fall prevention)
- Occupational therapy (daily activity adaptation)
- Speech therapy (Lee Silverman Voice Treatment — LSVT for hypophonia)
- Psychological support and cognitive rehabilitation
- Nutritional advice (protein timing for levodopa optimization)

## Prevention
- There is no proven way to prevent Parkinson's disease.
- Emerging evidence suggests that regular physical exercise may reduce the risk or slow disease progression.
- Avoiding pesticide exposure and head trauma may lower risk.
- Research into neuroprotective strategies (alpha-synuclein targeting, GLP-1 agonists, anti-inflammatory agents) is ongoing.
- For patients with PD: regular exercise, medication adherence, fall prevention, and social engagement help maintain quality of life.

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if you notice a persistent tremor at rest, slowness of movement, muscle stiffness, changes in handwriting, a shuffling gait, or reduced facial expression. Seek medical advice for new loss of sense of smell, REM sleep behavior disorder (acting out dreams), persistent constipation, or unexplained depression — which may be early non-motor signs. Patients already diagnosed with PD should seek urgent care for sudden worsening of symptoms, hallucinations, falls causing injury, or difficulty swallowing.
