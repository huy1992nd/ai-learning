# Alzheimer's Disease

## Key Facts
- Alzheimer's disease (AD) is the most common cause of dementia, accounting for 60–70% of all dementia cases.
- An estimated 55 million people worldwide are living with dementia, with nearly 10 million new cases each year.
- Dementia is the seventh leading cause of death globally and a major cause of disability and dependency among older people.
- Alzheimer's disease is characterized by progressive cognitive decline, memory loss, and behavioral changes.
- The disease typically affects people over 65 years, though early-onset Alzheimer's (before age 65) accounts for up to 5–10% of cases.
- There is currently no cure, but treatments can temporarily improve symptoms, and risk reduction strategies may delay onset.

## Overview
Alzheimer's disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the accumulation of amyloid-beta plaques (extracellular) and neurofibrillary tangles of hyperphosphorylated tau protein (intracellular) in the brain. These pathological changes lead to synaptic dysfunction, neuroinflammation, and progressive neuronal loss, particularly in the hippocampus and cerebral cortex.

The disease progresses through stages: preclinical (pathological changes without symptoms), mild cognitive impairment (MCI) due to Alzheimer's, and dementia due to Alzheimer's (mild, moderate, and severe stages). The clinical course typically spans 8–12 years from symptom onset to death, though this varies considerably.

## Symptoms
**Early stage (mild Alzheimer's):**
- Forgetting recent events and conversations
- Difficulty finding words (anomia)
- Losing or misplacing belongings
- Difficulty planning or organizing
- Impaired judgment and decision-making
- Apathy, withdrawal from social activities
- Mood changes (depression, anxiety)

**Middle stage (moderate Alzheimer's — longest stage):**
- Increasing memory loss (may forget personal history and family members)
- Confusion about time and place
- Behavioral and personality changes (agitation, suspicion, wandering)
- Difficulty with daily activities (dressing, bathing, toileting)
- Sleep disturbances (sundowning — increased confusion in the evening)
- Repetitive speech and actions

**Late stage (severe Alzheimer's):**
- Severe memory loss (unable to recognize family)
- Loss of ability to communicate verbally
- Complete dependence on caregivers
- Loss of mobility, dysphagia, incontinence
- Increased vulnerability to infections (especially pneumonia)

## Causes / Risk Factors
The exact cause of Alzheimer's disease is not fully understood. Current evidence points to a multifactorial etiology:

**Non-modifiable risk factors:**
- Age (greatest risk factor; risk doubles every 5 years after age 65)
- Genetics: APOE ε4 allele (strongest genetic risk factor for late-onset AD); autosomal dominant mutations in APP, PSEN1, PSEN2 (cause early-onset familial AD — rare, <1% of cases)
- Family history of dementia
- Down syndrome (trisomy 21 — the APP gene is on chromosome 21)

**Potentially modifiable risk factors (Lancet Commission 2020 identified 12 modifiable risk factors accounting for ~40% of dementia cases):**
- Low education, hearing loss, traumatic brain injury, hypertension, excessive alcohol, obesity, smoking, depression, social isolation, physical inactivity, diabetes, air pollution

## Diagnosis
- **Clinical assessment:** Comprehensive history from patient and informant, cognitive evaluation, neurological examination, and assessment of functional abilities.
- **Cognitive testing:** Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE), Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA), clock drawing test — to quantify cognitive impairment.
- **Neuroimaging:**
  - Brain MRI: Shows hippocampal and medial temporal lobe atrophy; excludes other causes (stroke, tumor, normal pressure hydrocephalus).
  - Amyloid PET scan: Detects amyloid-beta plaques in the brain (research/specialist use).
  - FDG-PET: Shows temporoparietal hypometabolism.
- **Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) biomarkers:** Low Aβ42, elevated total tau and phospho-tau — supports AD diagnosis.
- **Blood-based biomarkers:** Plasma Aβ42/40 ratio and phospho-tau (emerging as screening tools).
- **Exclusion of reversible causes:** Thyroid function, vitamin B12, folate, syphilis serology, HIV testing (as appropriate).

## Treatment
**Cholinesterase inhibitors (symptomatic treatment for mild-to-moderate AD):**
- Donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine — modestly improve cognition and function by inhibiting acetylcholinesterase.

**NMDA receptor antagonist:**
- Memantine — for moderate-to-severe AD; may be combined with cholinesterase inhibitors.

**Emerging disease-modifying therapies:**
- Anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies: Lecanemab, donanemab — have shown modest slowing of cognitive decline in early AD in clinical trials (FDA-approved under accelerated/full approval pathways). Require amyloid-PET or CSF confirmation and MRI monitoring for amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA).

**Non-pharmacological interventions:**
- Cognitive stimulation therapy and cognitive rehabilitation
- Physical exercise programs
- Music therapy, art therapy
- Structured daily routines and environmental modifications
- Caregiver education, support, and respite care

**Behavioral and psychological symptoms (BPSD):**
- Non-pharmacological approaches first (de-escalation, addressing triggers, environmental modifications)
- Pharmacological: Low-dose atypical antipsychotics (risperidone, quetiapine) for severe agitation or psychosis — use cautiously due to increased cerebrovascular risk

## Prevention
While there is no guaranteed way to prevent Alzheimer's disease, addressing modifiable risk factors may reduce or delay onset:

- Engage in lifelong learning and cognitively stimulating activities
- Maintain regular physical exercise (≥150 minutes moderate-intensity per week)
- Treat and manage cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, obesity)
- Address hearing loss with hearing aids
- Maintain social engagement and avoid social isolation
- Avoid excessive alcohol consumption and quit smoking
- Prevent head injuries (helmets, seatbelts)
- Treat depression and maintain mental health
- Ensure adequate sleep
- Follow a healthy diet (Mediterranean diet has the strongest evidence)

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if you or a family member experience persistent memory problems that interfere with daily life, difficulty performing familiar tasks, confusion with time or place, problems with language or decision-making, withdrawal from social activities, or personality and mood changes. Early evaluation allows for proper diagnosis, exclusion of treatable causes of cognitive decline, and timely access to support services and advance care planning.
