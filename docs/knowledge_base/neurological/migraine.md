# Migraine

## Key Facts
- Migraine is a primary headache disorder characterized by recurrent episodes of moderate to severe, often unilateral, pulsating headache lasting 4–72 hours.
- Migraine affects over 1 billion people worldwide and is the second leading cause of years lived with disability globally.
- It is approximately three times more common in women than men, partly due to hormonal factors.
- Migraine with aura (preceded by visual, sensory, or speech disturbances) occurs in about 25–30% of migraine sufferers.
- Migraine is associated with significant functional impairment, affecting work productivity, social activities, and quality of life.
- Effective acute and preventive treatments are available, yet migraine remains underdiagnosed and undertreated worldwide.

## Overview
Migraine is a complex neurological disorder involving recurrent attacks of headache accompanied by neurological, gastrointestinal, and autonomic symptoms. It is classified as a primary headache disorder (not caused by an underlying disease). The International Classification of Headache Disorders (ICHD-3) distinguishes between migraine without aura and migraine with aura.

The pathophysiology of migraine involves activation of the trigeminovascular system, release of neuropeptides (calcitonin gene-related peptide — CGRP), cortical spreading depression (the basis of aura), and central sensitization. Migraine is increasingly recognized as a spectrum disorder with episodic and chronic forms (chronic migraine is defined as ≥15 headache days per month for ≥3 months, with migraine features on ≥8 days).

## Symptoms
**Migraine attack phases:**

1. **Prodrome (hours to days before):** Mood changes, food cravings, neck stiffness, increased yawning, fluid retention, frequent urination.

2. **Aura (5–60 minutes, if present):** Fully reversible visual symptoms (flashing lights, zigzag lines, blind spots), sensory symptoms (tingling, numbness), or speech disturbances.

3. **Headache phase (4–72 hours):** Moderate to severe, often unilateral, pulsating/throbbing headache. Associated with nausea, vomiting, photophobia (light sensitivity), and phonophobia (sound sensitivity). Worsened by routine physical activity.

4. **Postdrome (hours to days after):** Fatigue, difficulty concentrating, mood changes, residual head discomfort.

## Causes / Risk Factors
The exact cause of migraine is not fully understood. It involves genetic predisposition and environmental triggers:

**Trigger factors (vary among individuals):**
- Stress and emotional changes
- Hormonal fluctuations (menstruation, oral contraceptives, menopause)
- Sleep disturbances (too little or too much sleep)
- Dietary factors (alcohol — especially red wine, aged cheese, chocolate, caffeine withdrawal, fasting/skipping meals)
- Sensory stimuli (bright/flickering lights, strong smells, loud sounds)
- Weather and barometric pressure changes
- Physical exertion

**Risk factors for chronic migraine:**
- Medication overuse (analgesics, triptans ≥10–15 days/month)
- Obesity
- Depression and anxiety
- Sleep disorders
- High baseline attack frequency

## Diagnosis
Migraine is a clinical diagnosis based on history and headache characteristics:

- **ICHD-3 criteria for migraine without aura:** ≥5 attacks lasting 4–72 hours, with at least two of: unilateral location, pulsating quality, moderate/severe intensity, aggravation by routine physical activity; AND at least one of: nausea and/or vomiting, photophobia and phonophobia.
- **Migraine with aura:** ≥2 attacks with fully reversible visual, sensory, or speech/language symptoms, developing over ≥5 minutes, lasting 5–60 minutes, with headache following within 60 minutes.
- **Neuroimaging (MRI brain):** Not routinely required but indicated for atypical presentations, new neurological findings, or red flags (thunderclap headache, seizures, progressive worsening).
- **Headache diary:** Recommended for tracking frequency, triggers, severity, and medication use.

## Treatment
**Acute treatment (to abort an attack):**
- Analgesics: Ibuprofen (400 mg), naproxen (500 mg), or aspirin (1000 mg) — most effective when taken early
- Triptans: Sumatriptan (oral, nasal spray, or subcutaneous injection), rizatriptan, zolmitriptan — first-line for moderate-to-severe attacks
- Anti-emetics: Metoclopramide or domperidone for associated nausea
- Gepants (CGRP receptor antagonists): Ubrogepant, rimegepant — newer oral options
- Ditans (5-HT1F agonists): Lasmiditan — alternative for patients who cannot use triptans

**Preventive treatment (reduce attack frequency):**
- Indicated when ≥4 migraine days/month or significant disability
- Beta-blockers: Propranolol, metoprolol
- Antiepileptics: Topiramate, valproic acid
- Antidepressants: Amitriptyline (low-dose)
- CGRP monoclonal antibodies: Erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab — monthly or quarterly injections; highly effective with minimal side effects
- OnabotulinumtoxinA (Botox): For chronic migraine (≥15 days/month)

**Lifestyle management:** Regular sleep schedule, regular meals, stress management, moderate exercise, trigger avoidance, adequate hydration.

## Prevention
- Identify and avoid personal triggers using a headache diary
- Maintain regular sleep patterns (consistent bedtime and wake time)
- Exercise regularly (at least 30 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise, 3–5 times per week)
- Manage stress through relaxation techniques, mindfulness, or cognitive behavioral therapy
- Avoid medication overuse (limit acute medications to ≤2–3 days per week)
- Stay hydrated and do not skip meals
- Consider preventive medication if attacks are frequent or disabling

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if headaches are becoming more frequent or severe, if current treatments are not effective, or if headaches significantly impact daily life. Seek emergency care for: sudden, severe headache ("thunderclap" — worst headache of your life), headache with fever and stiff neck, headache after head injury, headache with confusion, weakness, vision loss, or seizure, or a new type of headache after age 50.
