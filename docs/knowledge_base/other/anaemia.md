# Anaemia

## Key Facts
- Anaemia affects approximately 1.8 billion people worldwide (2019), making it one of the most common global health conditions.
- It is defined as a condition in which the number of red blood cells or their oxygen-carrying capacity is insufficient to meet physiological needs.
- Iron deficiency is the most common cause of anaemia globally, accounting for approximately 50% of cases.
- Anaemia disproportionately affects women of reproductive age (29.9%) and children under 5 (39.8%).
- Anaemia during pregnancy is associated with increased maternal and perinatal mortality, preterm delivery, and low birth weight.
- WHO considers anaemia a public health problem when prevalence exceeds 20% in a population.

## Overview
Anaemia is defined by haemoglobin concentration below WHO-established thresholds:
- Men: <13 g/dL (130 g/L)
- Non-pregnant women: <12 g/dL (120 g/L)
- Pregnant women: <11 g/dL (110 g/L)
- Children 6–59 months: <11 g/dL (110 g/L)

Anaemia is not a diagnosis but a sign of an underlying condition. It is classified by mechanism (decreased production, increased destruction, blood loss) and by red cell morphology (microcytic, normocytic, macrocytic).

The most common causes globally are iron deficiency (nutritional and from chronic blood loss), infections (malaria, hookworm, schistosomiasis, HIV), other nutritional deficiencies (folate, vitamin B12), haemoglobinopathies (sickle cell disease, thalassemia), and chronic disease.

## Symptoms
- Fatigue and weakness (most common)
- Pallor (skin, conjunctivae, nail beds)
- Shortness of breath, especially on exertion
- Dizziness, lightheadedness
- Tachycardia, palpitations
- Headache
- Cold hands and feet
- Difficulty concentrating, impaired cognitive function
- Irritability (especially in children)
- Pica (craving non-food substances — associated with iron deficiency)
- Koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails — severe iron deficiency)
- Angular stomatitis, glossitis (iron, B12, or folate deficiency)
- Jaundice (haemolytic anaemia)

Mild anaemia may be asymptomatic. Symptoms depend on severity, speed of onset, and underlying cause.

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Decreased RBC production:**
- Iron deficiency (dietary insufficiency, malabsorption — celiac disease, chronic blood loss — menstruation, GI bleeding)
- Folate and vitamin B12 deficiency
- Chronic kidney disease (decreased erythropoietin)
- Anaemia of chronic disease/inflammation (cancer, infections, autoimmune disease)
- Bone marrow disorders (aplastic anaemia, myelodysplastic syndrome, leukemia)

**Increased RBC destruction (haemolytic anaemia):**
- Haemoglobinopathies (sickle cell disease, thalassemia)
- G6PD deficiency
- Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia
- Malaria
- Mechanical destruction (prosthetic heart valves, TTP/HUS)

**Blood loss:**
- Acute: Trauma, surgery, postpartum haemorrhage
- Chronic: Heavy menstruation, GI bleeding (peptic ulcer, colorectal cancer, hookworm)

**Risk factors:** Female sex, pregnancy, poverty, malaria-endemic areas, vegetarian/vegan diet (B12), chronic disease

## Diagnosis
- **Complete blood count (CBC):** Haemoglobin, haematocrit, MCV (mean corpuscular volume), MCH, MCHC, RDW, reticulocyte count
- **Classification by MCV:**
  - Microcytic (MCV <80 fL): Iron deficiency, thalassemia, chronic disease
  - Normocytic (MCV 80–100 fL): Chronic disease, acute blood loss, haemolysis, bone marrow failure
  - Macrocytic (MCV >100 fL): Folate/B12 deficiency, myelodysplasia, alcohol, liver disease, hypothyroidism
- **Iron studies:** Serum ferritin (best single test for iron deficiency — <15 µg/L diagnostic; <30 µg/L with inflammation), serum iron, TIBC, transferrin saturation
- **Peripheral blood smear:** Morphology, sickle cells, target cells, spherocytes, schistocytes
- **Additional tests:** Reticulocyte count, vitamin B12, folate, LDH, haptoglobin, bilirubin (haemolysis workup), haemoglobin electrophoresis, Coombs test, EPO level, bone marrow biopsy (if indicated)

## Treatment
**Iron deficiency anaemia (most common):**
- Oral iron: Ferrous sulfate 325 mg (65 mg elemental iron) 1–3 times daily. Alternate-day dosing may improve absorption. Take on empty stomach with vitamin C.
- IV iron: For intolerance, malabsorption, severe anaemia, or ongoing losses (ferric carboxymaltose, iron sucrose)
- Treat underlying cause (GI bleeding, heavy menses)

**Folate deficiency:** Folic acid 1–5 mg/day orally
**Vitamin B12 deficiency:** IM cyanocobalamin or high-dose oral B12 (1000–2000 µg/day); lifelong if pernicious anaemia

**Anaemia of chronic disease:** Treat underlying condition; EPO-stimulating agents if CKD-related

**Haemolytic anaemia:** Treat underlying cause; corticosteroids for autoimmune haemolysis; folic acid supplementation

**Severe/symptomatic anaemia:** Red blood cell transfusion (Hb <7 g/dL or symptomatic at higher levels)

**Sickle cell disease/thalassemia:** Specialist management (hydroxyurea, transfusion programs, iron chelation, stem cell transplant)

## Prevention
- **Iron supplementation:** WHO recommends intermittent iron-folic acid supplementation for menstruating women in populations where anaemia prevalence is ≥20%
- **Dietary diversification:** Iron-rich foods (meat, fish, legumes, dark leafy greens); vitamin C to enhance non-heme iron absorption
- **Food fortification:** Iron fortification of wheat flour, rice, and other staples
- **Malaria prevention:** ITNs, IPTp (intermittent preventive treatment in pregnancy) in endemic areas
- **Deworming:** Periodic anthelminthic treatment in hookworm-endemic areas
- **Antenatal care:** Iron-folic acid supplementation throughout pregnancy
- **Delayed cord clamping:** At birth (≥1–3 minutes) — increases infant iron stores
- **Newborn screening:** For sickle cell disease and thalassemia in high-prevalence populations

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek medical evaluation for persistent fatigue, unexplained pallor, shortness of breath, dizziness, or palpitations. Urgent evaluation is needed for severe symptoms: chest pain, rapid heart rate at rest, fainting, or signs of active bleeding. Pregnant women should have haemoglobin checked at first antenatal visit and at 28 weeks. Children with poor growth, pallor, or pica should be evaluated for anaemia.
