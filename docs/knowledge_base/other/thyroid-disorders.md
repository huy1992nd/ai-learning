# Thyroid Disorders

## Key Facts
- Thyroid disorders are among the most common endocrine diseases worldwide.
- An estimated 200 million people globally have some form of thyroid disease.
- Iodine deficiency is the most common preventable cause of thyroid disorders worldwide, affecting approximately 2 billion people.
- Hypothyroidism affects 2–5% of the general population; subclinical hypothyroidism up to 10%.
- Hyperthyroidism affects approximately 1–2% of the population.
- Thyroid cancer is the most common endocrine malignancy, with rapidly rising incidence (partly due to increased detection).

## Overview
The thyroid gland produces thyroid hormones (T4 — thyroxine, and T3 — triiodothyronine) that regulate metabolism, growth, and development. Thyroid disorders include:

- **Hypothyroidism:** Insufficient thyroid hormone production. Most common cause in iodine-sufficient areas: Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune). In iodine-deficient regions: iodine deficiency.
- **Hyperthyroidism:** Excess thyroid hormone production. Most common cause: Graves' disease (autoimmune). Other causes: toxic multinodular goiter, toxic adenoma.
- **Goiter:** Enlargement of the thyroid gland. May be diffuse or nodular. Endemic goiter is due to iodine deficiency.
- **Thyroid nodules:** Very common (palpable in 5% of adults; detected by ultrasound in 50–70%). Most are benign; ~5–10% are malignant.
- **Thyroid cancer:** Papillary (80–85%), follicular (10–15%), medullary (3–5%), anaplastic (1–2%).

## Symptoms
**Hypothyroidism:**
- Fatigue, lethargy, cold intolerance
- Weight gain (modest), difficulty losing weight
- Constipation, dry skin, hair loss (diffuse)
- Bradycardia, puffy face and periorbital edema (myxedema)
- Depression, cognitive slowing, poor memory
- Menstrual irregularities (menorrhagia), infertility
- Myalgias, muscle weakness, elevated CK
- Hoarse voice, delayed relaxation of deep tendon reflexes

**Hyperthyroidism:**
- Weight loss despite increased appetite
- Heat intolerance, excessive sweating
- Anxiety, irritability, tremor, insomnia
- Tachycardia, palpitations, atrial fibrillation
- Diarrhea, frequent bowel movements
- Menstrual irregularities (oligomenorrhea/amenorrhea)
- Muscle weakness (proximal myopathy)
- **Graves' disease specific:** Diffuse goiter, ophthalmopathy (exophthalmos, lid retraction, periorbital edema), pretibial myxedema

**Thyroid nodules/cancer:**
- Often asymptomatic; discovered incidentally
- Palpable neck mass
- Hoarseness, dysphagia, or dyspnea (if large or invasive)
- Cervical lymphadenopathy (thyroid cancer with metastasis)

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Hypothyroidism:**
- Hashimoto's thyroiditis (most common in iodine-sufficient areas)
- Iodine deficiency (most common cause globally)
- Post-thyroidectomy, post-radioactive iodine therapy
- Medications: Lithium, amiodarone, checkpoint inhibitors
- Postpartum thyroiditis
- Central hypothyroidism (pituitary/hypothalamic — rare)

**Hyperthyroidism:**
- Graves' disease (autoimmune — most common)
- Toxic multinodular goiter, toxic adenoma
- Thyroiditis (subacute, painless — transient hyperthyroidism)
- Excessive iodine intake (Jod-Basedow effect)
- Medications: Amiodarone

**Thyroid cancer risk factors:**
- Radiation exposure (childhood head/neck radiation — strongest risk factor)
- Family history, genetic syndromes (MEN2 for medullary thyroid cancer)
- Female sex (3:1 female predominance)
- Iodine deficiency (follicular cancer); iodine excess (papillary cancer — debated)

## Diagnosis
**Thyroid function tests:**
- **TSH:** First-line screening test. Elevated in hypothyroidism; suppressed in hyperthyroidism.
- **Free T4 (fT4):** Low in hypothyroidism; elevated in hyperthyroidism.
- **Free T3:** Elevated in hyperthyroidism (especially T3 thyrotoxicosis).

**Autoantibodies:**
- Anti-TPO, anti-thyroglobulin: Hashimoto's thyroiditis
- TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb/TSI): Graves' disease

**Thyroid imaging:**
- Ultrasound: First-line for thyroid nodule evaluation. ACR TI-RADS classification for risk stratification.
- Radioactive iodine uptake (RAIU) and scan: Differentiates causes of hyperthyroidism (diffuse uptake in Graves'; focal in toxic adenoma; low in thyroiditis).
- Fine-needle aspiration biopsy (FNAB): For nodules meeting size/risk criteria per TI-RADS. Bethesda classification for cytology.

**Additional:** Thyroglobulin (tumor marker for differentiated thyroid cancer), calcitonin (medullary thyroid cancer), urinary iodine concentration (population iodine status).

## Treatment
**Hypothyroidism:**
- Levothyroxine (L-T4): Standard treatment. Dose adjusted to normalize TSH (typically 1.6 µg/kg/day for full replacement). Take on empty stomach, 30–60 min before breakfast.
- Monitor TSH every 6–8 weeks until stable, then annually.

**Hyperthyroidism:**
- **Antithyroid drugs (ATDs):** Methimazole (preferred) or propylthiouracil (PTU — preferred in first trimester of pregnancy). Treatment duration 12–18 months for Graves' disease; ~50% remission rate.
- **Radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy:** Definitive treatment for Graves' disease and toxic nodular goiter. Most patients become hypothyroid and require lifelong levothyroxine. Contraindicated in pregnancy and moderate-severe Graves' ophthalmopathy.
- **Thyroidectomy:** For large goiters, suspected malignancy, severe ophthalmopathy, or ATD intolerance/failure.
- **Beta-blockers:** Propranolol or atenolol for symptomatic relief (tachycardia, tremor) while awaiting ATD effect.

**Thyroid nodules/cancer:**
- Observation with serial ultrasound for low-risk nodules
- Surgery (thyroidectomy) for confirmed or suspected malignancy
- Radioactive iodine ablation for differentiated thyroid cancer post-surgery (selected cases)
- TSH suppression with levothyroxine (for high-risk differentiated cancer)
- External beam radiation, targeted therapy (lenvatinib, sorafenib) for advanced/refractory cases

## Prevention
- **Universal salt iodization (USI):** The most effective strategy for preventing iodine deficiency disorders — WHO recommends 20–40 mg iodine per kg of salt
- Adequate iodine intake: 150 µg/day for adults; 250 µg/day during pregnancy/lactation
- Newborn screening for congenital hypothyroidism (TSH heel-prick test) — enables early treatment and prevents intellectual disability
- Avoid unnecessary radiation exposure to the head and neck region, especially in children
- Regular thyroid function screening in high-risk groups (family history, autoimmune disease, post-radiation, pregnancy)

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider for symptoms of thyroid dysfunction: unexplained fatigue, weight changes, heat/cold intolerance, mood changes, or menstrual irregularities. Seek evaluation for any palpable neck mass or swelling, hoarseness, or difficulty swallowing. Urgent evaluation is needed for thyroid storm (severe hyperthyroidism with fever, tachycardia, altered consciousness) or myxedema coma (severe hypothyroidism with hypothermia, altered consciousness) — both are medical emergencies.
