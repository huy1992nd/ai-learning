# Vision Impairment

## Key Facts
- At least 2.2 billion people globally have a near or distance vision impairment, of whom at least 1 billion have a condition that could have been prevented or has yet to be addressed.
- The leading causes of vision impairment and blindness are uncorrected refractive errors and cataracts.
- Vision impairment disproportionately affects people in low- and middle-income countries, older adults, and women.
- Globally, at least 1 billion people have a near vision impairment (presbyopia) that could be corrected with reading glasses.
- The prevalence of vision impairment is projected to increase significantly due to population aging and lifestyle factors.
- 90% of vision impairment is preventable or treatable.

## Overview
Vision impairment ranges from mild visual difficulty to complete blindness. WHO defines:
- **Mild vision impairment:** Visual acuity worse than 6/12 to 6/18
- **Moderate vision impairment:** Worse than 6/18 to 6/60
- **Severe vision impairment:** Worse than 6/60 to 3/60
- **Blindness:** Worse than 3/60 or visual field <10° around central fixation

The major global causes of vision impairment are:
1. Uncorrected refractive errors (myopia, hyperopia, astigmatism) — most common cause
2. Cataracts — leading cause of blindness
3. Age-related macular degeneration (AMD)
4. Glaucoma
5. Diabetic retinopathy
6. Corneal opacity
7. Trachoma (leading infectious cause of blindness)

## Symptoms
Symptoms depend on the underlying condition:

- **Refractive errors:** Blurred vision (distance or near), eye strain, headaches, squinting
- **Cataracts:** Gradual painless blurring of vision, glare sensitivity, faded colors, difficulty with night driving, frequent prescription changes
- **Glaucoma:** Often asymptomatic until advanced (chronic open-angle); peripheral vision loss ("tunnel vision"); acute angle-closure: sudden severe eye pain, headache, nausea, halos around lights, red eye — ophthalmic emergency
- **Age-related macular degeneration:** Central vision loss, distortion (metamorphopsia — straight lines appear wavy), difficulty reading and recognizing faces
- **Diabetic retinopathy:** Often asymptomatic in early stages; floaters, blurred vision, dark areas in visual field, vision loss
- **Trachoma:** Recurrent conjunctivitis, eyelid inflammation, trichiasis (inturned eyelashes), corneal scarring, progressive vision loss

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Age:** The strongest risk factor — cataract, AMD, glaucoma, presbyopia all increase with age
- **Genetics:** Family history of glaucoma, AMD, refractive errors
- **Diabetes:** Diabetic retinopathy is the leading cause of blindness in working-age adults; risk increases with duration and poor glycemic control
- **UV light exposure:** Cataracts, pterygium
- **Smoking:** Cataracts (2–3× increased risk), AMD
- **Poor access to healthcare:** Uncorrected refractive errors, untreated cataracts, delayed glaucoma diagnosis
- **Infections:** Trachoma (Chlamydia trachomatis), river blindness (onchocerciasis), keratitis
- **Nutritional deficiency:** Vitamin A deficiency — leading cause of preventable childhood blindness
- **Prematurity:** Retinopathy of prematurity (ROP)
- **High myopia:** Increases risk of retinal detachment, macular degeneration, glaucoma

## Diagnosis
- **Visual acuity testing:** Snellen chart or LogMAR chart — fundamental screening tool
- **Refraction:** Autorefractometry or retinoscopy to determine refractive error
- **Slit-lamp examination:** Anterior segment evaluation (cornea, lens, iris)
- **Tonometry:** Intraocular pressure measurement (glaucoma screening)
- **Fundoscopy/ophthalmoscopy:** Retinal examination — detects diabetic retinopathy, AMD, glaucoma optic disc changes
- **Visual field testing (perimetry):** Detects glaucomatous or neurological visual field loss
- **Optical coherence tomography (OCT):** High-resolution retinal imaging for AMD, diabetic macular edema, glaucoma
- **Fundus photography:** Screening for diabetic retinopathy (including AI-assisted screening programs)

## Treatment
**Refractive errors:**
- Spectacles (glasses) — most cost-effective intervention in eye care
- Contact lenses
- Refractive surgery (LASIK, PRK) for suitable candidates

**Cataracts:**
- Cataract surgery with intraocular lens (IOL) implantation — one of the most cost-effective surgical interventions in medicine; >95% success rate

**Glaucoma:**
- Topical medications: Prostaglandin analogs (latanoprost — first-line), beta-blockers, alpha-agonists, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors
- Laser therapy: Selective laser trabeculoplasty (SLT)
- Surgery: Trabeculectomy, tube shunt, MIGS (minimally invasive glaucoma surgery)
- Acute angle-closure: Emergency treatment with topical/systemic IOP-lowering agents + laser peripheral iridotomy

**Age-related macular degeneration:**
- Dry AMD: AREDS2 supplements (vitamins C, E, zinc, lutein, zeaxanthin); monitoring
- Wet AMD: Intravitreal anti-VEGF injections (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab, faricimab) — transformed outcomes for wet AMD

**Diabetic retinopathy:**
- Optimize glycemic control, blood pressure, and lipids
- Intravitreal anti-VEGF for diabetic macular edema
- Panretinal laser photocoagulation for proliferative diabetic retinopathy
- Vitrectomy for vitreous hemorrhage or tractional retinal detachment

**Trachoma:**
- WHO SAFE strategy: **S**urgery (trichiasis), **A**ntibiotics (azithromycin mass drug administration), **F**acial cleanliness, **E**nvironmental improvement (water, sanitation)

## Prevention
- Regular comprehensive eye examinations (especially after age 40 and for diabetic patients)
- Correct refractive errors with appropriate spectacles
- UV protection (sunglasses, hats)
- Smoking cessation
- Good glycemic control in diabetes (HbA1c <7%) with annual retinal screening
- Vitamin A supplementation for children in deficient populations
- Trachoma elimination: WHO SAFE strategy
- Childhood vision screening programs
- Newborn eye screening (red reflex test) for congenital cataracts, retinoblastoma
- ROP screening for premature infants
- WHO VISION 2020 and World Report on Vision: Universal eye health coverage

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Seek immediate ophthalmic evaluation for sudden vision loss, flashing lights with new floaters (possible retinal detachment), sudden severe eye pain with redness and halos (acute angle-closure glaucoma), or eye injury. Consult an eye care provider for gradual blurring of vision, difficulty reading, distortion of straight lines, or any change in visual function. Diabetic patients should have annual dilated eye examinations. All adults should have a baseline comprehensive eye examination by age 40.
