# Eczema (Dermatitis)

## Key Facts
- Eczema (atopic dermatitis) is the most common chronic inflammatory skin disease, affecting up to 20% of children and 3–10% of adults worldwide.
- Atopic dermatitis typically begins in early childhood; 60% of cases start within the first year of life.
- It is part of the "atopic triad" — along with asthma and allergic rhinitis.
- Eczema significantly impacts quality of life due to intense itching, sleep disturbance, and psychological distress.
- The prevalence of eczema has increased 2–3 fold in industrialized countries over the past several decades.
- While there is no cure, effective management can control symptoms and improve quality of life.

## Overview
Eczema (atopic dermatitis — AD) is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin condition characterized by pruritus (itching), dry skin, and eczematous lesions. The disease results from a complex interplay of genetic factors (particularly skin barrier dysfunction), immune dysregulation (Th2-dominant inflammation), and environmental triggers.

The hallmark pathogenic mechanism involves mutations in the filaggrin gene (FLG), which impairs the skin barrier, allowing increased transepidermal water loss and penetration of allergens and irritants, which trigger immune-mediated inflammation.

## Symptoms
- **Intense pruritus (itching):** The hallmark symptom — often worse at night, disrupting sleep
- **Dry, scaly skin (xerosis)**
- **Eczematous lesions:** Erythematous, papulovesicular, weeping, and crusted lesions (acute); lichenified, thickened skin (chronic)
- **Age-dependent distribution:**
  - Infants: Face (cheeks), scalp, extensor surfaces
  - Children: Flexural areas (antecubital fossae, popliteal fossae, neck, wrists)
  - Adults: Hands, face, neck, flexural areas; may become generalized
- **Complications:** Secondary bacterial infection (Staphylococcus aureus colonizes >90% of AD skin), eczema herpeticum (HSV superinfection — medical emergency), sleep disturbance, anxiety, depression

## Causes / Risk Factors
- **Genetics:** Family history of atopy (eczema, asthma, allergic rhinitis); filaggrin (FLG) gene mutations (strongest genetic risk factor — present in ~30% of AD patients)
- **Immune dysregulation:** Th2-skewed immune response with elevated IgE, IL-4, IL-13, IL-31
- **Skin barrier dysfunction:** Impaired epidermal barrier allowing allergen penetration
- **Environmental triggers:** Irritants (soaps, detergents, fragrances), allergens (dust mites, pet dander, pollen), climate (low humidity, extreme temperatures), stress, infections
- **Hygiene hypothesis:** Reduced microbial exposure in early life may contribute to atopic disease
- **Microbiome:** Reduced skin microbial diversity; Staphylococcus aureus overgrowth

## Diagnosis
- **Clinical diagnosis:** Based on history and physical examination. No definitive diagnostic test.
- **Hanifin and Rajka criteria (or simplified UK Working Party criteria):** Major criteria include pruritus, typical morphology/distribution, chronic/relapsing course, personal/family history of atopy.
- **Severity assessment:** SCORAD (Scoring Atopic Dermatitis), EASI (Eczema Area and Severity Index), IGA (Investigator Global Assessment)
- **Allergy testing:** Skin prick tests or specific IgE — to identify relevant allergen triggers (not diagnostic for AD itself)
- **Skin biopsy:** Rarely needed; may be performed to exclude other diagnoses (psoriasis, cutaneous T-cell lymphoma)
- **Differential diagnosis:** Contact dermatitis, seborrheic dermatitis, psoriasis, scabies, dermatophytosis, immunodeficiency-associated eczema

## Treatment
**Foundational — skin barrier repair:**
- Regular emollient use (moisturizers) — at least twice daily and immediately after bathing; the cornerstone of AD management
- Gentle skin care: Short lukewarm baths, soap-free cleansers, pat dry

**Topical anti-inflammatory (flare management):**
- Topical corticosteroids (TCS): First-line; potency matched to severity and body area (low-potency for face/folds; medium-high potency for body/limbs)
- Topical calcineurin inhibitors (TCI): Tacrolimus, pimecrolimus — steroid-sparing, suitable for sensitive areas (face, skin folds)
- Topical PDE4 inhibitor: Crisaborole
- Topical JAK inhibitor: Ruxolitinib

**Systemic therapy (moderate-severe AD unresponsive to topical treatment):**
- **Biologics:** Dupilumab (anti-IL-4Rα — first-line systemic), tralokinumab (anti-IL-13)
- **JAK inhibitors:** Abrocitinib, upadacitinib, baricitinib
- **Conventional immunosuppressants:** Cyclosporine (most rapid onset), methotrexate, azathioprine, mycophenolate (off-label)
- **Phototherapy:** Narrowband UVB — effective for moderate AD

**Adjunctive:**
- Antihistamines: Sedating (hydroxyzine) for sleep; non-sedating antihistamines have limited evidence for itch in AD
- Treat secondary infections: Topical or systemic antibiotics for S. aureus; antivirals for eczema herpeticum
- Psychological support: CBT, relaxation techniques for stress-related flares

## Prevention
- Emollient use from birth in high-risk infants may reduce AD incidence (emerging evidence)
- Identify and avoid individual triggers (allergens, irritants)
- Maintain consistent skin hydration and barrier care
- Exclusive breastfeeding for 4–6 months (may reduce early atopic disease risk)
- Proactive "weekend therapy" with topical anti-inflammatories to prevent flares in frequently relapsing areas
- Avoid known irritants: Harsh soaps, wool clothing, excessive heat/sweating

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if eczema is widespread, severe, or not responding to regular emollients and over-the-counter treatments. Seek urgent care for signs of skin infection (increased redness, warmth, pus, crusting, fever) or eczema herpeticum (clusters of painful vesicles, fever, rapid deterioration). Referral to a dermatologist is recommended for moderate-to-severe AD or when systemic therapy is being considered.
