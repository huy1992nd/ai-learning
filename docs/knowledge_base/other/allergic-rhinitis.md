# Allergic Rhinitis

## Key Facts
- Allergic rhinitis affects an estimated 400 million people worldwide.
- It is the most common allergic disease and one of the most common chronic conditions globally.
- Allergic rhinitis is classified as intermittent or persistent, and mild or moderate-severe (ARIA classification).
- It is strongly associated with asthma — up to 40% of patients with allergic rhinitis have asthma, and 80% of asthmatics have allergic rhinitis.
- Allergic rhinitis significantly impairs quality of life, sleep, work productivity, and school performance.
- It is often underdiagnosed and undertreated despite available effective therapies.

## Overview
Allergic rhinitis is an IgE-mediated inflammatory disease of the nasal mucosa triggered by exposure to inhaled allergens. Upon allergen exposure in sensitized individuals, IgE-mediated mast cell degranulation releases histamine, leukotrienes, and other inflammatory mediators, causing nasal symptoms. A late-phase inflammatory response (4–8 hours later) involves eosinophils, T cells, and basophils, contributing to chronic symptoms.

**ARIA Classification (Allergic Rhinitis and its Impact on Asthma):**
- **Intermittent:** Symptoms <4 days/week or <4 consecutive weeks
- **Persistent:** Symptoms ≥4 days/week and ≥4 consecutive weeks
- **Mild:** Normal sleep, no impairment of daily activities/work/school
- **Moderate-severe:** Impaired sleep, daily activities, work, or school performance

## Symptoms
- Nasal congestion (obstruction)
- Rhinorrhea (clear, watery nasal discharge)
- Sneezing (paroxysmal)
- Nasal itching
- Ocular symptoms: Itchy, watery, red eyes (allergic conjunctivitis — present in >50% of patients)
- Post-nasal drip, throat clearing
- Reduced or absent sense of smell (hyposmia/anosmia)
- Mouth breathing, snoring
- Fatigue, irritability, difficulty concentrating ("brain fog")
- "Allergic shiners" (dark circles under eyes), "allergic salute" (upward rubbing of nose), "Dennie-Morgan lines" (infraorbital creases)

## Causes / Risk Factors
**Common allergens:**
- **Seasonal (outdoor):** Tree pollen (spring), grass pollen (late spring/summer), weed pollen/ragweed (fall)
- **Perennial (indoor):** House dust mites (most common perennial allergen), pet dander (cats, dogs), cockroach allergens, mold spores

**Risk factors:**
- Family history of atopy (strongest risk factor)
- Personal history of atopic disease (eczema, asthma, food allergy)
- Early childhood allergen exposure
- Exposure to environmental tobacco smoke
- Urban living, air pollution
- Male sex in childhood (equalizes in adulthood)

## Diagnosis
- **Clinical history:** Characteristic symptoms triggered by allergen exposure; seasonal or perennial pattern; family history of atopy
- **Nasal examination:** Pale, swollen, bluish nasal turbinates; clear secretions; nasal polyps should be excluded
- **Allergy testing (to confirm sensitization and identify triggers):**
  - Skin prick testing (SPT): First-line; rapid, sensitive, cost-effective
  - Serum-specific IgE (ImmunoCAP): When SPT not feasible (dermographism, antihistamine use, severe eczema)
- **Nasal cytology:** Eosinophilia supports allergic etiology (not routinely performed)
- **Differential diagnosis:** Non-allergic rhinitis (vasomotor, gustatory), infectious rhinitis, rhinitis medicamentosa (decongestant overuse), nasal polyps, deviated septum, CSF rhinorrhea

## Treatment
**Stepwise approach based on ARIA severity classification:**

**Allergen avoidance (foundation):**
- Dust mite measures: Encasings, wash bedding ≥60°C, reduce humidity, remove carpets
- Pollen: Keep windows closed during high pollen counts, shower after outdoor activity, use air purifiers
- Pets: Ideally remove from home; if not possible, keep out of bedroom, HEPA filters
- Mold: Fix moisture sources, use dehumidifiers

**Pharmacotherapy:**
- **Intranasal corticosteroids (INCs) — most effective treatment:** Fluticasone, mometasone, budesonide. First-line for moderate-severe symptoms. Regular use more effective than as-needed.
- **Second-generation oral antihistamines:** Cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine, bilastine. Effective for sneezing, rhinorrhea, itching; less effective for nasal congestion. Non-sedating.
- **Intranasal antihistamines:** Azelastine — rapid onset; effective as monotherapy or combined with INCs.
- **Combination sprays:** Azelastine + fluticasone (superior to either alone for moderate-severe AR).
- **Leukotriene receptor antagonists:** Montelukast — second-line; useful when asthma coexists.
- **Oral/topical decongestants:** Short-term only (<5–7 days for topical to avoid rhinitis medicamentosa).
- **Intranasal anticholinergics:** Ipratropium — for predominant watery rhinorrhea.

**Allergen immunotherapy (AIT):**
- Subcutaneous (SCIT) or sublingual (SLIT) immunotherapy
- Indicated for moderate-severe AR inadequately controlled by pharmacotherapy
- Disease-modifying: Can induce long-term tolerance, prevent asthma development in children, reduce new sensitizations
- Typically 3–5 years of treatment

## Prevention
- Minimize allergen exposure (environmental controls)
- Early treatment of allergic rhinitis may reduce progression to asthma
- Allergen immunotherapy in children may prevent development of asthma (preventive effect)
- Avoid environmental tobacco smoke exposure in children
- Maintain good indoor air quality (ventilation, HEPA filters, humidity control)

## When to Seek Medical Attention
Consult a healthcare provider if nasal symptoms persist for more than 2 weeks and interfere with sleep, work, or daily activities, or if over-the-counter antihistamines provide inadequate relief. Seek evaluation for unilateral nasal symptoms, bloody discharge, facial pain, or loss of smell (to exclude other pathology). Referral to an allergist is recommended for diagnostic allergy testing, consideration of immunotherapy, or when symptoms coexist with asthma.
